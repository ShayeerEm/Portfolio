Civil Litigation in NSW: From Pleadings to Judgment under the UCPR

SE
Shayeer Emanee
June 2026
5 min read
Dispute Resolution Case Analysis Australian Law
⚖

Civil litigation in New South Wales is governed principally by the Civil Procedure Act 2005 (NSW) and the Uniform Civil Procedure Rules 2005 (NSW) (UCPR). Together, these instruments establish the framework within which disputes are pleaded, evidence is exchanged, interlocutory applications are brought, and trials are conducted. For legal assistants and paralegals working in a dispute resolution practice, familiarity with this framework - in both its procedural mechanics and its underlying policy objectives - is foundational.

1. The Overriding Purpose: Just, Quick, and Cheap

Section 56 of the Civil Procedure Act 2005 (NSW) establishes the overriding purpose of the Act and UCPR: to facilitate the just, quick, and cheap resolution of the real issues in civil proceedings. Every procedural decision - whether relating to the scope of discovery, the timing of interlocutory injunctions, or the conduct of experts - must be consistent with this purpose. Courts have increasingly treated s 56 as a substantive constraint on dilatory or disproportionate litigation tactics, and the overriding purpose has been used to justify curtailing excessive discovery, refusing adjournments, and imposing costs orders on parties who conduct litigation wastefully.

2. Commencing Proceedings: The Statement of Claim

Civil proceedings in the Supreme Court, District Court, and (for smaller claims) the Local Court are commenced by the filing and service of a statement of claim under r 6.1 of the UCPR. The statement of claim is a pleading document that must identify the parties, set out the material facts relied upon in support of each cause of action, and specify the relief claimed. It must not contain argument or evidence.

In the Supreme Court of NSW, a statement of claim commences proceedings that will proceed in the Common Law Division (for torts, contract, and certain statutory claims) or the Equity Division (for equitable claims, trust disputes, and corporate matters). The distinction between Common Law and Equity Division proceedings is not merely jurisdictional; it affects the procedural rules that apply and the nature of the remedies available.

4. The Defence and Cross-Claims

Within 28 days of service of a statement of claim (or such further time as the court allows), the defendant must file and serve a defence. The defence must respond to each allegation in the statement of claim by admitting it, denying it, or stating that the defendant does not know and cannot admit the allegation. A blanket denial without specific admissions or traversal will often attract criticism from the court and may be struck out as evasive under r 14.23 of the UCPR.

A defendant who has a claim against the plaintiff may file a cross-claim under r 9.1 of the UCPR. A cross-claim is treated as a separate proceeding joined to the main action, enabling all related disputes between the parties to be resolved in a single proceeding. The defendant-cross claimant must file a cross-claim summons or cross-claim statement in the appropriate form.

5. Discovery and Subpoenas

Discovery is the process by which parties are required to disclose documents relevant to the proceedings. Under r 21.1 of the UCPR, a party may seek an order for discovery against another party. The scope of discovery is limited to documents that are directly relevant to the issues in dispute, and discovery must be proportionate to the complexity and value of the proceedings. Courts are increasingly resistant to wide-ranging "fishing expedition" discovery applications that impose disproportionate costs on the responding party.

In addition to party discovery, a party may issue a subpoena to a non-party requiring them to produce documents or to attend court to give evidence. Subpoenas must identify the documents or evidence sought with reasonable particularity, and the recipient has a right to object to the subpoena on grounds of relevance, privilege, or oppressiveness.

6. Interlocutory Applications

During the course of civil litigation, parties frequently seek interlocutory relief - provisional orders made before the final determination of the dispute. Common interlocutory applications include: interlocutory injunctions (to restrain conduct that will cause irreparable harm pending trial); search orders (Anton Piller orders, preserving evidence at risk of destruction); freezing orders (Mareva injunctions, preventing dissipation of assets pending judgment); and orders for summary judgment or dismissal.

The test for an interlocutory injunction is established in Australian Broadcasting Corporation v O'Neill (2006) 227 CLR 57 (applying and refining the American Cyanamid formulation): the applicant must demonstrate (a) a prima facie case for the final relief sought; and (b) that the balance of convenience favours the grant of an injunction. The strength of the prima facie case affects the balance of convenience analysis; a stronger case requires less favourable balance of convenience.

7. Case Management and the Trial

NSW courts actively manage civil proceedings through directions hearings, pre-trial conferences, and case management decisions. The Supreme Court's Practice Notes set out the standard directions that apply in Common Law and Equity Division matters. Parties are expected to comply with directions, file evidence in accordance with the timetable, and identify any expert evidence at an early stage. Expert evidence must comply with the Expert Witness Code of Conduct in Sch 7 of the UCPR, which requires experts to express independent and impartial opinions.

At trial, civil proceedings are determined on the balance of probabilities. The standard of proof requires the court to be satisfied that the fact in issue is more likely than not to have occurred. Where the facts in issue have particularly serious consequences - such as fraud allegations - the Briginshaw principle (Briginshaw v Briginshaw (1938) 60 CLR 336) applies, requiring a degree of satisfaction proportionate to the gravity of the allegation.

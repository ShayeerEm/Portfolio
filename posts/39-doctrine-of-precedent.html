The Doctrine of Precedent: Stare Decisis, Ratio Decidendi, Obiter Dicta, and the Rules of Binding Authority

SE
Shayeer Emanee
June 2026
8 min read
Foundations of Law Case Analysis Australian Law
§

The doctrine of precedent is the structural foundation of the common law system. It is what makes Australian law predictable and consistent across time and across courts. Without it, each judge would decide each case from scratch, producing not a body of law but a collection of individual opinions. With it, legal principles accumulate - refined and tested across thousands of cases - and the result is a system in which lawyers can advise clients, citizens can plan their affairs, and parties can anticipate outcomes with reasonable confidence.

This post examines the doctrine's philosophical basis, its two core components, and the rules governing which courts must follow which decisions.

1. Stare Decisis: Standing on What Has Been Decided

The Latin phrase stare decisis - "to stand on what has been decided" - is the philosophical basis of the doctrine of precedent. It means that once a legal principle has been established by a court, future courts should follow it. The principle rests on a cluster of related values: consistency (like cases should be treated alike), certainty (people should be able to know what the law is), efficiency (courts should not relitigate settled questions), and institutional legitimacy (law should not vary based on the preferences of individual judges).

Stare decisis is not absolute. The common law has always permitted some departure from earlier decisions in order to correct error, keep pace with changing social values, and prevent the accumulation of injustice in the name of consistency. But departure is the exception, not the rule - and the exceptions are carefully circumscribed by the doctrine itself.

2. The Two Parts of Every Court Decision

When a court gives its judgment, not every word carries the same legal weight. The doctrine of precedent draws a critical distinction between two categories of judicial statement.

2.1 Ratio Decidendi - The Binding Part

Ratio decidendi means "the reason for deciding." It is the legal principle or rule that the court actually used to resolve the legal question before it. The ratio is the part of the judgment that creates binding precedent - future courts in the appropriate relationship (lower courts in the same hierarchy) must follow it.

Identifying the ratio is often more difficult than it sounds. Judgments do not come with a clearly labelled "ratio" section. A judge may discuss many legal principles, consider alternatives, and review the case law at length before arriving at their ruling. The ratio must be extracted from this discussion by identifying:

  • The legal question the court was actually asked to decide.
  • The legal principle the court applied to answer that question.
  • The relationship between that principle and the material facts of the case.

A useful sign that the ratio has been reached is a shift in the judge's language - from reviewing and comparing authorities to making a decisive, first-person statement of principle. Phrases like "In my view the principle is..." or "The rule that governs this case is..." often mark the ratio. The ratio directly answers the legal issue (the question of law the court was deciding); anything else, however interesting, is potentially obiter.

2.2 Obiter Dicta - Persuasive Only

Obiter dicta means "things said by the way." Obiter dicta are statements of legal principle made in a judgment that were not strictly necessary to decide the case. They commonly take the form of hypotheticals ("If the facts had been different in this way, the result would have been..."), observations about the law that go beyond what the case required, or comments on legal issues raised but not ultimately dispositive.

Obiter dicta are not binding on future courts. A lower court is not required to follow a principle expressed only in obiter, even from a much higher court. However, they can be highly persuasive - particularly if the obiter observation comes from a senior court, represents the considered view of multiple judges, and has been consistently endorsed in subsequent decisions.

The most important qualification to this general rule concerns obiter from the High Court. Under the principle established in Farah Constructions Pty Ltd v Say-Dee Pty Ltd [2007] HCA 22, High Court obiter from a majority of the Court should be "seriously considered" by lower courts and, unless the lower court is convinced it is plainly wrong (an extremely rare conclusion), should be followed. In practice, this means that majority obiter from the High Court operates almost as binding precedent for Australian lower courts - even though technically it is not.

3. The Rules of Precedent: Who Follows Whom

The binding force of a precedent depends entirely on the relationship between the court that decided it and the court now asked to apply it. The rules can be summarised as follows:

Source of Precedent Effect on Current Court Reason
Higher court in the SAME hierarchy BINDING - must follow the ratio This is the core operation of stare decisis; lower courts are subordinate to higher courts in their hierarchy
HIGH COURT OF AUSTRALIA BINDING on every Australian court The High Court is the apex court of both state and federal hierarchies; its decisions bind all courts throughout Australia
Same-level court in the same hierarchy PERSUASIVE - should follow unless the earlier decision is plainly wrong Horizontal precedent; courts at the same level are not technically bound by each other but generally follow prior decisions for consistency (Babaniaris v Lutony Fashions)
Lower court in the same hierarchy PERSUASIVE - may follow, may decline A lower court's decision may be useful but it is not authoritative for a higher court
Court in a DIFFERENT hierarchy PERSUASIVE - weight depends on context A NSW Supreme Court judge is not bound by a Federal Court decision and vice versa; the weight given depends on the nature of the matter and whether the same statute is being interpreted (see Farah principle)
OVERSEAS common law courts PERSUASIVE - weight depends on the connection UK, Canadian, and New Zealand decisions carry significant persuasive weight; civil law jurisdictions carry very little
Obiter dicta from any court PERSUASIVE only - not binding But High Court majority obiter should be followed unless plainly wrong (Farah)

4. The NSW Court Hierarchy - Applied

To apply the rules of precedent, it is necessary to know where each court sits in the hierarchy. The NSW state hierarchy, from lowest to highest, is:

  • Local Court (Magistrates Court, Children's Court, Coroner's Court) - original jurisdiction only; hears matters for the first time
  • District Court - original and appellate jurisdiction; hears appeals from the Local Court
  • Supreme Court of NSW - original and appellate jurisdiction; hears the most serious civil and criminal matters
  • Court of Appeal / Court of Criminal Appeal - appellate jurisdiction only; always a bench of multiple judges (usually 3 or 5)
  • High Court of Australia - apex court for both state and federal hierarchies; original jurisdiction for constitutional matters; binds all Australian courts

A critical point about the Court of Appeal and the Supreme Court: they sit in the same building and are often referred to as "the Supreme Court" colloquially, but the Court of Appeal is a higher court than the Supreme Court at first instance. A single judge of the Supreme Court is bound by Court of Appeal decisions; the Court of Appeal is not bound by a single judge of the Supreme Court.

Australia also has a separate federal court hierarchy: the Federal Circuit and Family Court of Australia → the Federal Court of Australia → the High Court of Australia. Both the state and federal hierarchies converge at the High Court, which sits above them all.

5. Courts and Their Own Past Decisions

A question that arises frequently is whether a court is bound by its own earlier decisions. The general answer is no - a court is not bound by its own previous decisions in the way that a lower court is bound by a higher court. However, the practice of departing from one's own earlier decisions is treated with extreme caution and occurs only in rare circumstances.

Courts will depart from their own earlier decisions only where the earlier decision is "plainly wrong" - a high threshold. The reasoning is that stability and predictability are values that the doctrine of precedent serves, and a court that routinely overrules itself provides no more consistency than no precedent at all. The high bar for self-departure reflects the seriousness with which courts treat the stability of settled law.

The High Court has the power to overrule its own earlier decisions - and has done so, though relatively rarely. Mabo v Queensland (No 2) [1992] HCA 23 is the most prominent example: the High Court there overruled the doctrine of terra nullius that had been applied by Australian courts since colonisation. More recently, in NZYQ v Minister for Immigration (2023), the High Court overruled its own decision in Al-Kateb v Godwin (2004) on the question of indefinite immigration detention.

6. Conclusion

The doctrine of precedent - stare decisis - is the mechanism by which a common law system is more than the sum of its individual decisions. By requiring lower courts to follow the ratio of higher courts, it creates a body of law that is coherent, predictable, and capable of generating consistent outcomes across different courts and over time. The distinction between binding ratio and persuasive obiter is not a technicality; it is the architecture of the system. Understanding which courts bind which, and what part of any judgment carries that binding force, is the starting point for almost every question of law a common law lawyer will ever address.

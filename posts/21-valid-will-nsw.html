Making a Valid Will in NSW: Formalities, Testamentary Capacity, and Informal Wills under s 8

SE
Shayeer Emanee
June 2026
6 min read
Wills & Estates Case Analysis Australian Law
📜

The making of a valid will is one of the most important steps a person can take to ensure that their property is distributed according to their wishes on death. In New South Wales, the law of wills is governed principally by the Succession Act 2006 (NSW), which substantially modernised and consolidated the prior law. This post examines the formal requirements for a valid will, the doctrine of testamentary capacity, the significance of testamentary freedom, and the circumstances in which a court may validate an informal will.

1. The Formal Requirements: Section 6 of the Succession Act 2006 (NSW)

Section 6 of the Succession Act 2006 (NSW) sets out the formal requirements for a valid will. A will must be:

  • In writing.
  • Signed by the testator (the person making the will) or, at the testator's direction and in their presence, by another person on their behalf.
  • The testator's signature must be made or acknowledged in the presence of two or more witnesses present at the same time.
  • At least two of those witnesses must attest and sign the will in the presence of the testator (but need not sign in the presence of each other).

The signature must be made with the intention of executing the will. A testator who does not sign but instead places a mark, an initial, or even a stamp in the appropriate location with the intention of executing the will may satisfy the requirement, depending on the circumstances. The critical element is intention, not the form of the signature.

Position of the Signature

Section 6(2) of the Act provides that the will is not taken to be duly executed unless the signature is made or acknowledged by the testator at the foot or end of the will. In practice, modern wills are signed at the end of the document and initiated on each preceding page, which satisfies this requirement and reduces the risk of contested alterations. A signature that appears in the wrong position - such as in the body of the will rather than at the end - may be invalid, although courts have shown some flexibility where the intention is clear.

Witnesses

A person who is a beneficiary under the will (or who is the spouse of a beneficiary) should not act as a witness. Section 10 of the Succession Act 2006 (NSW) provides that a gift to a witness (or the spouse of a witness) is not invalidated by the witnessing, but it may be ineffective to transfer the gift. The witness is not technically disqualified from witnessing, but acting as a witness creates a risk of the gift failing. Best practice is to ensure that witnesses have no beneficial interest under the will.

2. Testamentary Capacity

A testator must have "testamentary capacity" at the time of making the will. The test for testamentary capacity was established in Banks v Goodfellow (1870) LR 5 QB 549, a decision of the English Queen's Bench that has been accepted as the applicable test in Australian law. The testator must:

  • Understand the nature of the act of making a will and its effects.
  • Understand the extent of the property being disposed of.
  • Comprehend and appreciate the claims of those who might reasonably expect to benefit from the estate.
  • Not be suffering from any disorder of the mind that poisons their affections, perverts their sense of right, or prevents the exercise of their natural faculties in disposing of their property.

A testator may have testamentary capacity even if they suffer from dementia, mental illness, or cognitive impairment, provided that the will is made in a "lucid interval" - a period during which the impairment does not affect their understanding. The capacity assessment is specific to the time of execution; a person who lacked capacity when they first instructed their solicitor may have capacity when they sign the will, and vice versa. For this reason, practitioners should consider obtaining a contemporaneous medical opinion when preparing wills for clients who may have borderline capacity.

3. Knowledge and Approval

Even where the testator had capacity, the will must represent their instructions - they must have had "knowledge and approval" of the contents of the will. Where the will was prepared by a third party, and particularly where that third party benefits from the will, the court requires positive proof that the testator knew and approved the contents. The circumstances in which knowledge and approval may be questioned include: where the testator could not read the will and did not have it read to them; where there was a fraudulent misrepresentation of the will's contents; or where the beneficiary who prepared the will concealed its terms from other potential beneficiaries.

4. Undue Influence and Suspicious Circumstances

A will may be refused probate where it was procured by undue influence - that is, where the will-maker's wishes were overborne by coercion or manipulation such that the will does not represent a free and voluntary act. The standard in probate law for undue influence is stricter than in contract law: the influence must have been such that the testator's will was coerced, not merely that they were persuaded or influenced. Persuasion, however persistent, that leaves the testator's volition intact is not undue influence.

5. Informal Wills: Section 8 of the Succession Act 2006 (NSW)

Section 8 of the Succession Act 2006 (NSW) empowers the Supreme Court to admit to probate a document that does not comply with the formal requirements of s 6 if the court is satisfied that the person intended the document to constitute their will. The section has been applied to handwritten notes, text messages, voice recordings, draft wills on computers, and even letters - provided there is evidence that the document was intended to be the person's last will. Section 8 is a remedial provision that attempts to give effect to the genuine intentions of a testator who failed to comply with the formalities.

The leading NSW case on s 8 is Re Masters (dec'd) (1994) 33 NSWLR 446, where the Court of Appeal confirmed that the section requires a two-stage inquiry: (1) is there a document (or notional document) that the deceased intended to be their will? (2) Did the deceased intend that document to constitute their will, operative without further action on their part? A draft will sent to the testator for review, but not yet signed, may satisfy the test where the testator expressed a clear intention that the draft should operate as their will if anything happened to them before they could execute the formal version.

6. Revocation of Wills

A will is revoked by marriage (under s 12 of the Succession Act 2006 (NSW), marriage automatically revokes a prior will unless the will was made in contemplation of the marriage), by the subsequent making of a new will that expressly or impliedly revokes the prior will, by destruction of the will with the intention to revoke it, or by a codicil that revokes specific provisions of the prior will. Divorce does not revoke a will in NSW; it revokes specific gifts to the former spouse and any appointment of the former spouse as executor, but the remainder of the will stands.

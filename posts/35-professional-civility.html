Professional Civility: The Duty, Its Scope, and the Structural Causes of Its Decline

SE
Shayeer Emanee
June 2026
7 min read
Professional Responsibility Case Analysis Australian Law
📋

Professional civility is sometimes dismissed as a secondary concern - a matter of good manners rather than serious ethical obligation. This is a misunderstanding. Civility in the legal profession is a codified duty, a structural safeguard for the justice system, and a dimension of the obligation to act as an officer of the court. Its decline - which academic commentators have documented over several decades - carries measurable costs: wasted judicial resources, eroded public confidence in the profession, and legal environments in which junior practitioners learn to equate aggression with effectiveness.

1. What is Professional Civility?

Civility in the legal profession means more than politeness. It has been defined by Harre as requiring politeness, the capacity to genuinely listen to the other side, self-confidence and courage (remaining civil under adversarial pressure requires strength, not weakness), maturity and personal integrity, and respect and appreciation for the humanity of opposing counsel, witnesses, and parties - even in the context of fierce disagreement.

The ASCR provides that a solicitor must act with courtesy in all dealings in the course of legal practice. This duty is not qualified by the behaviour of the opposing party - it does not evaporate if the other side is rude, aggressive, or unreasonable. The duty is owed by the solicitor as a professional, and it persists regardless of provocation.

What civility is not:

  • It is NOT a duty to be passive or to avoid forceful advocacy. A solicitor can be vigorous, firm, and persistent in advancing a client's case while remaining civil.
  • It is NOT a duty to agree with the other side or to make concessions out of politeness. Hard negotiating positions, firm cross-examination, and aggressive applications for costs are all civil if conducted professionally.
  • It does NOT override freedom of expression. A lawyer may criticise judgments, comment on systemic problems in the justice system, and advocate for law reform - but must do so through professional language and appropriate channels.

2. To Whom Is the Duty Owed?

The duty of civility runs in multiple directions simultaneously. A solicitor owes the obligation to:

Recipient Key Obligations
Clients No condescension; respect for client autonomy; frank advice without demeaning the client's position or intelligence
Opposing clients Basic human dignity; no personal attacks on the opposing party (as distinct from their legal position)
Colleagues within the firm Professional respect; no demeaning communication to junior staff or support personnel
Opposing counsel Must not adopt the client's animosity toward opposing counsel; must engage professionally regardless of personal feelings about the opposing lawyer
Judges and the court Absolute respect for the bench; must not publicly disparage judicial decisions in intemperate terms even where criticism of the reasoning is legitimate
Third parties and witnesses No abusive or unnecessarily aggressive conduct during examination; basic courtesy in correspondence
Members of the public The solicitor's public conduct reflects on the entire profession; discourtesy in public contexts carries professional implications

The category "opposing counsel" deserves particular attention. Clients in litigation frequently develop intense animosity toward the opposing party and, by extension, toward opposing counsel. A solicitor who adopts the client's personal hostility - who treats opposing counsel not merely as the legal representative of an adverse interest but as a personal enemy - has lost the professional detachment that effective representation requires. The client's emotional state should inform the solicitor's understanding of the stakes; it should not dictate the solicitor's professional conduct.

3. Forums: Where the Duty Applies

The duty of civility is not confined to the courtroom. It applies across all contexts in which a solicitor operates in the course of legal practice:

3.1 The Courtroom

The courtroom is the most regulated environment for professional conduct. Interrupting opposing counsel during submissions, making personal remarks about opposing counsel before the court, using intemperate language in examining witnesses, or disrespecting the court's rulings are all direct breaches of the duty of civility as well as potentially contempt of court.

3.2 Written Communications

Aggressive correspondence - letters that are sarcastic, demeaning, or designed to intimidate rather than to advance a legal position - constitutes a breach of the duty of civility. Disciplinary tribunals have found that the tone of correspondence, not just its content, can give rise to professional misconduct findings. A demand letter may be firm and uncompromising; it should not be insulting. A letter advising that a costs order will be sought may be clear and pointed; it should not contain personal attacks on opposing counsel.

3.3 Social Media

Social media has opened a new frontier for civility failures. Lawyers who vent about opposing counsel, judges, or tribunal members on LinkedIn, Twitter, or other public platforms - even in vague or anonymous terms - have faced disciplinary action. The platform does not change the professional obligation: a statement that would constitute a breach of civility if made in the courtroom does not become permissible merely because it is made from a personal social media account. Prudent solicitors maintain a strict demarcation between professional and personal online conduct, and apply the same standards to each.

4. The Decline of Civility: Academic Analysis

Academic commentators have documented a sustained decline in professional civility in common law jurisdictions, including Australia. Two scholars provide particularly useful frameworks:

4.1 Sarat: Institutional Culture

Austin Sarat's analysis identifies institutional incentives for incivility. Large law firm culture actively rewards lawyers who are seen as "aggressive" and "tough." The growth of billable hours as the primary measure of professional value creates incentives to over-litigate - to pursue every conceivable application, to contest every procedural step, to discover every irrelevant document - because more adversarial activity means more billing. The lawyer who settles a case early and cheaply, however much better that outcome might be for the client, earns less for the firm than the lawyer who runs it for three years.

4.2 O'Hara: Rambo Tactics

Erin O'Hara's work on "Rambo tactics" or "adversarial excesses" documents the costs of incivility: loss of credibility with judges, massive waste of judicial resources, and a measurable erosion of public esteem for the profession. O'Hara identifies a connection between increased student debt, competitive legal employment markets, and the emergence of cutthroat professional norms that treat civility as naive.

Additional structural causes of civility decline identified in the literature include:

  • Increasing firm size: as firms grow, the senior partner mentoring that once transmitted professional norms disappears. Junior lawyers have fewer role models for ethical professional conduct.
  • Greater anonymity: in large cities, lawyers rarely encounter the same opposing counsel repeatedly. The social consequences of incivility - reputation, professional relationships - are diminished when you are unlikely to see the same person again.
  • Cultural shifts: the pop culture ideal of the lawyer has shifted from Atticus Finch (calm, principled, motivated by justice) toward characters who represent winning at any cost.

5. Mental Illness and the Civility Standard

A specific issue that arises in disciplinary proceedings is whether mental illness can excuse uncivil conduct. The general answer is that it cannot provide a complete defence to a civility-based professional misconduct finding, though it may be relevant to the appropriate penalty.

The tribunal's primary obligation is to protect the public and the profession. A solicitor whose mental illness causes them to send abusive correspondence, to behave inappropriately in court, or to engage in harassment of colleagues poses a risk to the clients and other participants in the justice system who encounter them. The tribunal cannot discharge its protective function by accepting that mental illness provides a blanket excuse for conduct that damages the people the profession serves.

Mental illness may, however, support a penalty focused on rehabilitation - conditions on the practising certificate requiring treatment and monitoring - rather than permanent exclusion from the profession. The tribunal will consider the nature of the illness, the degree of insight the practitioner has developed, and the prospect of adequate management. But the diagnosis is an explanation, not a justification.

6. Conclusion

Professional civility is not politeness for its own sake. It is the expression, in interpersonal terms, of the solicitor's obligations as an officer of the court and a trusted professional. The lawyer who is civil to opposing counsel preserves the conditions necessary for negotiation and settlement. The lawyer who is civil to witnesses protects the integrity of the evidence. The lawyer who is civil to the court maintains the respect that makes the court's authority credible. And the lawyer who recognises that aggressive and uncivil behaviour is typically less effective than firm, professional advocacy - not more - will serve their clients better, whatever the cultural pressures pushing in the other direction.

The Verdins Principles in NSW Criminal Sentencing: Mental Illness, Culpability, and the Limits of Deterrence

SE
Shayeer Emanee
June 2026
5 min read
Criminal Law Case Analysis Australian Law
⚡

The Verdins principles - named after Verdins v R [2007] VSCA 102 - represent the most comprehensive framework in Australian criminal law for the consideration of mental illness as a mitigating factor in sentencing. Although originating in Victoria, these principles have been expressly adopted and applied by the NSW Court of Criminal Appeal and are now regarded as settled law across the common law jurisdictions. This analysis surveys the six Verdins propositions, traces their application in recent NSW authority, and identifies the critical limits on their operation.

1. Origins and Status

In Verdins v R [2007] VSCA 102, the Victorian Court of Appeal (Eames, Nettle and Vincent JJA) set out, for the first time in a systematic way, the six pathways through which evidence of mental disorder can mitigate a sentence. The Court drew on a body of earlier authority - from both Victoria and other jurisdictions - and synthesised it into a coherent analytical framework. The judgment was not creating new law so much as articulating and organising principles that already existed but had been applied inconsistently.

In NSW, the Court of Criminal Appeal adopted the Verdins framework in Muldrock v The Queen (2011) 244 CLR 120 (High Court) and in numerous subsequent decisions. The principles operate within the framework of ss 5, 21A and 24 of the Crimes (Sentencing Procedure) Act 1999 (NSW), which direct the court to take into account all relevant mitigating factors, including the extent to which the offender's mental condition at the time of the offence reduced moral culpability.

2. The Six Verdins Propositions

The six propositions identified in Verdins v R can be stated as follows:

  • Mental impairment may reduce the offender's moral culpability or blameworthiness for the offence, warranting a lesser sentence than would otherwise be appropriate.
  • Mental illness may make the experience of imprisonment more onerous than it would be for a person without the illness.
  • The existence of mental illness may indicate that the offender poses a lesser risk of future offending, making specific deterrence a less significant sentencing consideration.
  • Where the offender's mental condition makes it unlikely that imprisonment will serve as a deterrent, either to the offender or to others, the importance of general deterrence is reduced.
  • Mental illness may indicate a greater prospect of rehabilitation.
  • Where mental illness is relevant to the sentencing exercise, the court should consider whether a non-custodial or alternative sentencing option is available.

3. Proposition 1: Reduced Moral Culpability

The most commonly invoked Verdins pathway is the first: that mental disorder reduces the offender's moral culpability for the offending. The threshold requirement is a causal or quasi-causal connection between the disorder and the offending. It is not sufficient that an offender simply had a mental illness at the time of the offence; the illness must have relevantly contributed to the conduct. This connection need not rise to the level of a defence - the mental illness need not have completely deprived the offender of understanding or volition - but there must be some meaningful nexus.

In Luchetti v R [2025] NSWCCA 222, the psychiatric evidence established "a close correlation - though not a direct causal link - between [the appellant's] substance use, his psychotic symptoms, and his offending behaviour." The Court accepted this as sufficient to engage Verdins proposition 1, significantly reducing the weight given to general deterrence in the re-sentencing exercise. The Court described the appellant as "not a suitable vehicle for general deterrence" - language that has become standard in NSW courts when mental illness substantially reduces moral culpability.

4. Proposition 2: Onerousness of Custody

Where mental illness makes the experience of imprisonment more burdensome than it would otherwise be - through, for example, difficulty accessing appropriate treatment, or an elevated risk of harm from other inmates - this ordinarily supports a reduction in the length of the sentence. However, the application of this proposition requires individualised assessment.

Luchetti v R [2025] NSWCCA 222 illustrates the limits of this proposition in a striking way. The psychiatric evidence established that Mr Luchetti was institutionalised and actually felt safer in custody than in the community. The Court held that, in these circumstances, custody was not "more onerous" for him than for other prisoners - and the usual assumption underpinning proposition 2 was therefore inapplicable. This is a rare outcome and serves as a reminder that the Verdins framework must be applied to the specific evidence before the court, not assumed.

5. Propositions 3 and 4: Deterrence

General deterrence reflects the policy of using an individual's punishment as a warning to others who might be tempted to offend in similar ways. Specific deterrence aims to discourage the individual offender from reoffending. Both forms of deterrence assume a degree of rational deliberation - the capacity to weigh the consequences of one's actions against the potential benefits of offending.

Where mental illness significantly impairs this capacity for rational deliberation - as in cases of active psychosis, severe substance-induced cognitive impairment, or intellectual disability - both forms of deterrence are diminished in their sentencing significance. The Court in Luchetti expressly held that the appellant's schizophrenia and substance use disorder, combined with the minimal objective gravity of the offence, meant that general deterrence should receive "very little weight" in the re-sentencing exercise.

6. Verdins and the Non-Parole Period

A critical procedural consideration in NSW is the relationship between the Verdins principles and the calculation of the non-parole period. Under s 44 of the Crimes (Sentencing Procedure) Act 1999 (NSW), the non-parole period must generally be at least three-quarters of the head sentence unless there are special circumstances. Where mental illness is a factor, special circumstances within the meaning of s 44(2) will often be established - allowing the court to set a longer parole period to ensure the offender receives appropriate supervision and treatment in the community upon release.

7. Evidential Requirements

A party who seeks to rely on the Verdins principles must adduce evidence - typically a forensic psychiatrist's report - establishing the nature and severity of the mental disorder, the connection (if any) between the disorder and the offending, and the likely impact of the disorder on the experience of custody. Courts are not entitled to simply assume the existence of a mental illness or its effects; the principles are engaged by evidence, not assertion.

Where psychiatric evidence is tendered, the sentencing judge must engage with it. A failure to address Verdins principles in circumstances where they are clearly raised by the evidence constitutes an error of law that may vitiate the sentence: R v Nguyen [2016] NSWCCA 23.

Applying the Doctrine of Precedent: Step-by-Step Framework and Worked Example

SE
Shayeer Emanee
June 2026
8 min read
Foundations of Law Case Analysis Australian Law
§

Knowing the rules of precedent in the abstract is only the first step. The harder skill is applying those rules to a set of cases - working out which decisions you must follow, which you should consider, how to handle conflicts between authorities, and what to do when a binding case gives you a result you cannot accept. This post provides a step-by-step analytical framework for precedent questions and illustrates it with a worked example.

1. The Step-by-Step Framework

When faced with a precedent problem - whether in an exam, in practice, or in a moot - work through the following steps in sequence.

Step 1: Identify Your Forum

Which court are you sitting in? This determines the hierarchy that applies to you. If you are a single judge of the NSW Supreme Court, you are bound by the Court of Appeal, bound by the High Court, and not bound by the District Court or Local Court. If you are the Federal Court, you are not bound by any NSW court. If you are the High Court, you are (in principle) not bound by anyone - though you treat your own prior decisions as highly persuasive.

Getting the forum right before anything else prevents the common error of treating a decision from a different hierarchy as binding when it is only persuasive.

Step 2: Identify the Subject Matter

Is the legal question about a state statute, a Commonwealth statute, or a common law issue? This matters because it affects how much weight interstate decisions carry. Under the Farah Constructions principle (Farah Constructions Pty Ltd v Say-Dee Pty Ltd [2007] HCA 22), where courts in different hierarchies are interpreting the same Commonwealth statute, the desirability of national uniformity elevates the persuasive weight of interstate intermediate appellate decisions to near-binding. A NSW Supreme Court judge interpreting a Cth statute should not depart from a Full Federal Court interpretation of the same provision unless convinced it is plainly wrong.

Step 3: Classify Each Case in the Problem

For each case you have been given, determine:

  • Is it from a higher court in your hierarchy? → Binding; you must follow its ratio.
  • Is it from the same level in your hierarchy? → Persuasive; follow it unless plainly wrong (Babaniaris principle).
  • Is it from a lower court? → Persuasive; you may follow or decline.
  • Is it from a different hierarchy (federal vs state)? → Persuasive; weight elevated for Cth statute matters (Farah).
  • Is it from an overseas court? → Persuasive; weight depends on the common law connection (UK and New Zealand carry more than civil law countries).
  • Is the relevant part of the case ratio or obiter? → Ratio is binding if from a higher court; obiter is persuasive only (but High Court majority obiter should be followed unless plainly wrong).

Step 4: Identify Conflicts Between Cases

Where cases point in different directions, work out which takes priority. If a higher court has criticised or disapproved of a lower court's decision, the higher court's position prevails. If two courts at the same level have reached different conclusions, neither is technically binding on the other - apply the test of which is more persuasive and better reasoned, acknowledging the tension.

A particular situation to watch for is where a higher court has disapproved the reasoning of an earlier decision but followed its outcome. In that case, you are bound by the outcome (because the apex court followed it) but you cannot use the earlier reasoning (because the apex court said it was wrong). You must use the apex court's reasoning to reach the same result.

Step 5: Apply the Ratio to Your Facts

Apply each binding ratio to the facts of the problem. Ask: are the material facts of the precedent case sufficiently similar to the facts of the current case that the ratio applies? The closer the factual similarity, the stronger the case for applying the ratio. Where the facts are significantly different, consider whether the ratio can be distinguished.

Step 6: Distinguish Where Necessary

If a binding case would produce a result that is unjust or inappropriate, look for meaningful factual differences between that case and your case. If those differences are genuine and go to the material facts of the case - facts that actually affected the legal outcome - you may be able to distinguish the earlier case and decline to apply its ratio. Distinguishing is not a device for avoiding inconvenient precedents; it is a principled argument that the earlier case simply does not apply to different facts.

2. Distinguishing Cases

Distinguishing is the technique by which a court - even one that is nominally bound by an earlier decision - avoids applying it. The technique is legitimate where genuine factual differences exist; it is impermissible where it is used to disguise a refusal to follow binding authority without proper grounds.

To distinguish a case, you must identify:

  • The material facts of the earlier case - the facts that actually determined the outcome.
  • The facts of the current case - what is different?
  • Why the difference matters - explain why the factual difference means the ratio of the earlier case simply does not extend to the current situation.

Example: Suppose Case A held that a dog that growled at a person entering a property posed a "risk" for the purpose of a dangerous animal clause. In the current case, the dog was silent until the person attempted to pet it. The factual difference - the dog's behaviour was not directed at an intruder but was a reaction to being handled - may be a genuine distinguishing factor. The ratio in Case A (growling at an entrant = risk) does not necessarily extend to the current facts (growling when handled by an invited guest).

3. Worked Example

The following example is the kind of precedent question commonly set in Foundations of Law assessments. Work through it using the framework above.

The Problem

You are a single judge of the NSW Supreme Court interpreting a Commonwealth statute - the Hypothetical Federal Services Act 2001 (Cth). Four cases are in evidence:

  • Case A (2010) NSWSC: Interpreted the statutory provision in favour of the applicant.
  • Case B (2014) Full Federal Court: Held that Case A was "plainly wrong" and reached the opposite conclusion.
  • Case C (2018) High Court of Australia: Disapproved Case A's reasoning in full, but followed Case A's outcome, reaching the same result as Case A for different reasons.
  • Case D (2020) Victorian Court of Appeal: Applied Case A and reached the same result.

Step 1: Forum

You are a single judge of the NSW Supreme Court. Your hierarchy: Local Court → District Court → Supreme Court (single judge) → Court of Appeal → High Court.

Step 2: Subject Matter

This is a Commonwealth statute. The Farah principle applies: interstate intermediate appellate courts' interpretations should be treated as near-binding unless plainly wrong.

Step 3: Classify Each Case

Case Classification Effect
Case A (2010) NSWSC Same-level court in your own hierarchy (single judge of the NSW Supreme Court) Persuasive only. But: the High Court in Case C disapproved its reasoning - you CANNOT use Case A's reasoning even if you reach the same result.
Case B (2014) Full Federal Court Different hierarchy (federal) - but this is a Cth statute, so Farah applies. The Full Federal Court is an intermediate appellate court. Near-binding under Farah - should be followed unless plainly wrong. Case B said Case A was plainly wrong. However, the High Court in Case C did NOT follow Case B's outcome (the HCA followed Case A's result). The HCA is above the Full Federal Court.
Case C (2018) High Court Apex court - highest court in your hierarchy BINDING. You must follow Case C. Case C disapproved Case A's reasoning but followed Case A's outcome. You must: (1) reach Case A's outcome (because the HCA did), and (2) use Case C's reasoning (because the HCA said Case A's reasoning was wrong).
Case D (2020) Victorian Court of Appeal Different state hierarchy - persuasive. Cth statute → elevated weight but it is an interstate appellate decision Persuasive. But: it applied Case A - which the HCA has already criticised. Case C overrides Case D wherever they conflict.

Step 4: Resolve the Conflict

Cases A, C, and D all produce the same outcome. Case B produces the opposite outcome. The High Court in Case C is binding on you and reached the same result as Cases A and D (though for different reasons). Case B is from the Full Federal Court - which, under Farah, carries near-binding weight on a Cth statute question. But Case B is inconsistent with the High Court's binding decision in Case C. The High Court always prevails over the Full Federal Court. Case B is therefore overridden by Case C.

Conclusion

Follow Case C (the High Court). Adopt Case A's outcome (which the HCA affirmed). Use Case C's reasoning, not Case A's (which the HCA disapproved). Treat Case B as having been effectively overruled by the High Court on this point. Give Case D limited independent weight because it relied on Case A's reasoning, which is no longer available.

4. Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Confusing "binding" and "persuasive" - never say a case "is" the law without first identifying whether its court is above you in your hierarchy.
  • Treating all High Court statements as binding - only the ratio of the High Court is binding; its obiter should be "seriously considered" and followed unless plainly wrong, but is technically persuasive.
  • Ignoring the Farah principle - when a Commonwealth statute is being interpreted, interstate intermediate appellate decisions are much stronger than ordinary "persuasive" authority.
  • Failing to distinguish ratio from obiter - the binding force of a decision depends on what part of the judgment you are relying on.
  • Using an earlier case's reasoning when a higher court has disapproved it - you must abandon the reasoning even if you maintain the outcome.

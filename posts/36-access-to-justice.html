Access to Justice in Australia: Barriers, Dietrich, Legal Aid, and Pro Bono

SE
Shayeer Emanee
June 2026
9 min read
Professional Responsibility Case Analysis Australian Law
📋

Access to justice is the link between having a formal legal right and being able to enforce it. A right that cannot be practically exercised is, in functional terms, not a right at all. Australia has an elaborate and sophisticated legal system - but the ability to participate in that system, to understand one's rights, and to obtain the representation necessary to vindicate them is profoundly unequally distributed across the population. This post examines the key barriers to justice in Australia, the constitutional and common law framework (particularly Dietrich v The Queen), and the mechanisms through which the legal profession attempts to address the gap between formal and effective access.

1. What is Access to Justice?

Effective access to justice requires more than the formal availability of courts. It requires that a person who has a legal problem can identify that they have a legal problem, understand what rights they have in relation to it, obtain competent advice and (where necessary) representation, participate meaningfully in the processes designed to resolve the problem, and afford to do all of this without the cost itself becoming the injustice.

The failure of any of these conditions is a failure of access to justice. A tenant who does not know that their landlord's conduct is unlawful has no practical access to their rights, even if a tribunal exists to adjudicate the dispute. A parent who cannot afford legal representation in family law proceedings cannot participate meaningfully in decisions about their children's future, even if the Family Court is accessible in principle.

2. The Key Barriers

Barrier Nature and Scope
Financial cost The primary barrier. Legal representation in even routine matters can cost tens of thousands of dollars. Complex litigation, disputes over property or custody, or criminal proceedings can cost orders of magnitude more. For the significant portion of the population with modest incomes, legal representation is simply unaffordable.
The 'missing middle' Persons who are too wealthy to qualify for Legal Aid but too poor to afford private representation. This gap is particularly severe in family law and civil disputes, where means-tested Legal Aid leaves most ordinary working people without assistance.
Geographic barriers Rural, regional, and remote (RRR) communities face acute shortages of legal practitioners. A person in remote outback NSW may have no access to a solicitor within hundreds of kilometres. Telephone and video conferencing have ameliorated but not solved this problem.
Court delays 'Justice delayed is justice denied.' Significant backlogs in court lists mean that disputes can take years to resolve, during which time the parties live with uncertainty and legal costs accumulate.
Complexity and intimidation Legal language, court procedures, and formal processes are inaccessible to many people without legal training. Procedural errors can be fatal to claims. The system itself excludes through its complexity.
Cultural and linguistic barriers Non-English speakers, First Nations peoples, and communities with cultural mistrust of legal institutions face additional systemic barriers beyond cost and geography.

3. Is There a Right to Legal Representation in Australia?

A common assumption - imported in part from the American constitutional tradition - is that there is a right to a publicly funded lawyer when facing serious criminal charges. In Australia, the position is more nuanced.

The short answer is: no. Australia does not have a constitutional right to publicly funded legal representation. There is no equivalent of the Sixth Amendment right to counsel in the Australian Constitution, and the common law does not recognise such a right at large.

The leading authority is Dietrich v The Queen (1992) 177 CLR 292. Dietrich was charged with drug smuggling and applied for Legal Aid. Legal Aid was refused unless he pleaded guilty. He went to trial unrepresented in a complex drug matter, was convicted, and appealed to the High Court.

The High Court held:

  • There is NO common law right to be provided with legal representation at public expense.
  • However, there IS a right to a fair trial - a right that is fundamental to the common law and to the judicial system as a whole.
  • Where an accused person is unrepresented through no fault of their own and faces a serious criminal charge, and where their lack of representation makes it likely that the trial will be unfair, the trial judge has the power - and in appropriate circumstances, the duty - to adjourn or stay the proceedings until adequate representation is available.

Dietrich does not create a positive right to a lawyer. It creates a negative right - a right not to be put on trial in circumstances that make the trial unfair. The distinction is significant: the state is not required to provide representation, but it cannot proceed with a trial that would be unfair due to the absence of representation. In practice, this creates pressure on Legal Aid authorities to provide funding for serious criminal matters, because the alternative is a stay of proceedings.

4. The Legal Aid System

Legal Aid commissions in each state and territory provide government-funded legal services to eligible persons. In NSW, Legal Aid NSW is established under the Legal Aid Commission Act 1979 (NSW) and funded by both Commonwealth and state government contributions.

Access to Legal Aid is conditional on two tests:

  • A means test: the applicant's income and assets must be below specified thresholds. Legal Aid is targeted at those who genuinely cannot afford representation, not at those who simply prefer not to pay.
  • A merit test: the matter must have a reasonable prospect of success, and must be of a type for which Legal Aid is available. Historically, Legal Aid has been concentrated on criminal law and family law, with very limited coverage for civil matters.

Legal Aid is chronically underfunded relative to the need it is designed to meet. The "missing middle" problem - where persons above the means test threshold but unable to afford private representation are left without assistance - is a persistent structural failure of the system.

5. Community Legal Centres

Community Legal Centres (CLCs) are independent, not-for-profit organisations providing free legal services. They typically specialise in specific areas of law particularly affecting disadvantaged communities - tenancy, welfare, domestic violence, refugee and asylum law, consumer law, and employment. CLCs are often the first point of contact for people who have no idea how to navigate a legal problem.

CLCs are heavily underfunded and must triage their services, accepting only cases that meet their criteria and declining many more. The demand for CLC services far exceeds supply. They serve a critical function in the access to justice ecosystem, but their capacity is structurally limited by inadequate public funding.

6. Pro Bono Legal Services

"Pro bono publico" means "for the public good." Pro bono legal work is legal assistance provided free of charge, or at a substantially reduced rate, to people who cannot afford to pay for it and who would otherwise go without representation. It is delivered by private law firms as a professional commitment, rather than by government funding.

6.1 The National Pro Bono Target

The Australian Pro Bono Centre administers a voluntary national target of 35 hours of pro bono work per lawyer per year (20 hours for in-house lawyers). The 2025 national data shows that 844,999 hours were delivered nationally - equivalent to 469 full-time lawyers working entirely on pro bono matters. The average was 42.3 hours per lawyer. However, only 52% of lawyers met the target, reflecting the uneven distribution of pro bono contributions across the profession.

Australia's pro bono target is voluntary. This distinguishes the Australian position from Indonesia, which mandates 50 pro bono hours per year. The debate about whether mandatory pro bono would be appropriate or effective in Australia continues. Critics argue that mandatory pro bono would reduce quality and commitment; proponents argue that the voluntary model allows the public to be let down whenever firms decide that commercial pressures outweigh pro bono obligations.

6.2 Professional Standards Apply Equally to Pro Bono Work

A critical and sometimes overlooked point: the fact that pro bono work is provided without payment does not reduce the professional standards that apply. All conduct rules apply equally to pro bono engagements.

  • A practising certificate is required: s 47 LPUL. A solicitor cannot provide pro bono legal advice without holding a current practising certificate.
  • Professional indemnity insurance is required: s 211 LPUL. The client who receives pro bono advice and suffers loss through the solicitor's negligence has the same right to compensation as any other client.
  • A letter of engagement is required: the scope of the engagement, its limitations, and the basis on which the solicitor is acting should be documented, even where the fee is zero. Failure to define the scope of a pro bono retainer creates the same risk of liability for matters outside the intended scope as any other undefined retainer.
  • Confidentiality applies: all information received from a pro bono client is subject to the same duty of confidentiality as information received from a paying client.

6.3 The Policy Debate About Pro Bono

Pro bono sits within a broader policy debate about how access to justice should be funded and delivered. Three positions recur in the academic literature:

First, the quid pro quo view: lawyers have a state-protected monopoly on legal services and benefit from self-regulation. Pro bono is the price they pay for these privileges - a professional obligation flowing directly from the advantages the profession enjoys.

Second, the moral obligation view: lawyers, as officers of the court and professionals uniquely positioned to understand and navigate the legal system, have a special moral duty to make the system accessible to those who cannot use it unaided.

Third, the systemic critique: heavy reliance on lawyer charity - however admirable - lets governments off the hook for adequately funding the justice system. If access to justice is a public good (which it is), its provision should be a public responsibility, funded through taxation, not dependent on the professional generosity of private lawyers. Each view captures something true, and the tension between them is unlikely to be resolved while Legal Aid remains underfunded.

7. Conclusion

Access to justice is not an abstract aspiration. It is the concrete condition that determines whether the legal rights that Parliament creates and the courts interpret have any practical meaning for ordinary people. The combination of chronically underfunded Legal Aid, patchy CLC coverage, insufficient pro bono reach, and structural barriers of cost, geography, and complexity means that a large proportion of Australians with legal problems do not get legal help. Understanding this landscape - its causes, its manifestations, and the mechanisms (however imperfect) through which the profession addresses it - is essential context for any lawyer who seeks to practise with genuine professional consciousness.

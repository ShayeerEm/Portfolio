Judicial Philosophies: Legal Formalism, Realism, and Judicial Activism Compared

SE
Shayeer Emanee
June 2026
8 min read
Foundations of Law Case Analysis Australian Law
§

Every judge brings to their work a set of beliefs about what the law is, what it should do, and what the proper role of courts is in a democratic society. These beliefs - whether acknowledged or not - shape how judges approach difficult cases, how they reason about ambiguous provisions, and where they draw the line between applying the law and making it. The study of judicial philosophies is the study of these beliefs made explicit: an examination of the different frameworks within which judges understand their own authority and function.

This post examines three principal judicial philosophies - legal formalism, legal realism, and judicial activism - together with the arguments for and against each. These are not merely academic categories; they explain outcomes in actual cases and provide the conceptual vocabulary for evaluating judicial decisions.

1. Legal Formalism: The Law as a Complete System

1.1 Core Tenets

Legal formalism - sometimes called "black-letter law" or "strict legalism" - holds that law is a self-contained, objective system of rules that always has one correct answer for any given legal question. The judge's role is to find and apply that answer, not to create it. Judicial reasoning is (or should be) a logical deduction from established rules to a determinate conclusion: the facts, plus the rule, produces the outcome. The judge who does their job correctly is not exercising discretion or making policy - they are executing a logical operation.

The most prominent advocate of this view in Australia was Sir Owen Dixon, who served as Chief Justice of the High Court from 1952 to 1964 and who articulated the aspiration of "strict and complete legalism" as the guiding principle of judicial method. For Dixon, the judge's task was to identify the applicable legal rules and apply them, leaving the responsibility for law reform to the legislature. The legitimacy of judicial decision-making depended on its adherence to this principled restraint.

1.2 Strengths

  • Predictability and certainty: if the law is a set of determinate rules that can be applied mechanically, lawyers can advise clients with confidence and people can plan their affairs accordingly. The rule of law requires that law be knowable in advance.
  • Respect for democracy: in a democracy, law should be made by the elected legislature, not by unelected judges. Formalism constrains judicial law-making and preserves the legislature's role as the primary source of legal change.
  • Doctrinal coherence: a formalist approach produces consistent development of doctrine across cases, as each new decision follows from established principles.
  • Judicial independence from political pressure: a judge who applies the law as written is insulated from the political consequences of their decisions in a way that a judge who exercises wide discretion is not.

1.3 Weaknesses

  • Inflexibility: the real world generates situations that established rules do not address or that they address unjustly. A formalist judge cannot respond to these situations by adapting the law - they must apply the rules as they are and leave it to Parliament to fix the injustice, which may take years.
  • Language is not as clear as formalism assumes: statutory and common law rules are expressed in language, and language is inherently ambiguous. The claim that there is always one "correct" answer that can be read off the rules without any evaluative judgment is empirically doubtful.
  • Concealed discretion: critics argue that formalist judges make choices even when they claim to be "just applying the law" - they simply do not acknowledge those choices. Pretending that legal outcomes are logically determined when they actually involve discretion is less honest than acknowledging the discretion openly.
  • Historical use to perpetuate injustice: formalist reasoning was used by Australian courts before Mabo to apply the doctrine of terra nullius - itself an unjust legal fiction - without questioning its foundational premises. Strict adherence to established rules prevented the law from recognising the prior rights of Indigenous Australians.

2. Legal Realism: What Courts Actually Do

2.1 Core Tenets

Legal realism - developed primarily in the United States in the 1920s and 1930s - takes a sceptical and empirical approach to law. Realists argue that law is not a complete logical system that produces determinate answers; it is, in practice, the result of choices made by human beings who are influenced by instinct, policy preference, social context, and personal values - even when those choices are expressed in the language of logical rule-application.

The realist critique of formalism is essentially a claim about honesty. Realists do not deny that courts should apply legal rules; they deny that courts always, or only, apply legal rules when they decide cases. They argue that where law is genuinely indeterminate - where multiple outcomes are consistent with the applicable rules - judges make choices. Those choices are influenced by the judge's views about policy, justice, and social consequences. Realism demands that judges be transparent about this, rather than hiding policy reasoning behind the formal appearance of rule application.

2.2 Strengths

  • Honesty about the nature of adjudication: where the law is genuinely unclear or incomplete, acknowledging that the judge is making a choice is more intellectually honest than pretending the outcome was logically compelled.
  • Accountability: if judges acknowledge the policy reasoning that drives their decisions, that reasoning can be scrutinised, criticised, and improved. Hidden policy reasoning is immune from this external check.
  • Explanatory power: realism accurately describes how many hard cases are actually decided, where formalist accounts struggle to explain the divergence of outcomes between different judges facing identical rules.

2.3 Weaknesses

  • Predictability undermined: if law is reducible to the choices of individual judges, it loses the consistent, predictable character that makes it useful. "The law is what the judge had for breakfast" is the satirical extreme of the realist position.
  • Rule of law concerns: the idea that law is just a cover story for policy preferences undermines the authority of courts as institutions governed by principled rules rather than personal discretion.

3. Judicial Activism: Courts as Instruments of Justice

3.1 Core Tenets

Judicial activism describes an approach in which the judge sees the court's primary mission as promoting justice - particularly in situations where strict application of existing rules would produce unjust outcomes, where the law has failed to keep pace with changed social values, or where minority rights are threatened by majority political processes. An activist judge is prepared to extend, adapt, reinterpret, or even create legal principles to achieve a just outcome, rather than leaving the injustice in place and waiting for Parliament to act.

Justice Michael Kirby of the High Court was Australia's most prominent proponent of judicial activism, describing the judge's duty as including law-making where existing law provides no answer: "If there is no apparent law, the judge is duty-bound to create it." Kirby argued that the appellate judge who consistently refuses to develop the common law, always deferring to Parliament, is abdicating a legitimate and important judicial function.

3.2 Strengths

  • Law evolves with society: strict application of outdated rules produces injustice. Judicial activism allows the law to adapt to changed social circumstances without waiting for the slow processes of parliamentary reform.
  • Protection of minorities: electoral majorities may be indifferent or hostile to minority rights. An activist judiciary can protect those rights against majority will, performing a counter-majoritarian function that democratic processes alone cannot.
  • The common law has always been judge-made: the common law developed precisely through judges extending, adapting, and creating principles in individual cases. Activism is simply acknowledging and embracing what the common law has always done.

3.3 Weaknesses

  • Democratic legitimacy: unelected, unaccountable judges making policy decisions are substituting their own values for the judgments of democratically elected representatives. Justice Dyson Heydon described activism as "the death of the rule of law" and activist judges as a "third Parliament" without democratic mandate.
  • Unpredictability: if judicial outcomes depend on what a particular judge considers "just," the law becomes unpredictable. The consistency and stability that the rule of law requires is undermined.
  • Institutional competence: courts decide specific cases on the basis of arguments from two parties. They lack the capacity to gather wide-ranging evidence, consult affected communities, and assess systemic consequences in the way that a parliamentary law reform process can. Policy-making requires these capacities; adjudication does not provide them.

4. The Three Philosophies Compared

Dimension Formalism Realism Activism
What law is A complete, objective system of determinate rules An incomplete system that is often indeterminate in hard cases A developing instrument for achieving justice
What judges do Find and apply the law; they do not make it Apply rules, but also make choices that reflect policy preferences Apply rules and actively develop them to promote justice
Key virtue Certainty, consistency, democratic restraint Honesty, transparency about the judicial role Justice, responsiveness to social change, minority protection

5. Conclusion

No judicial philosophy is simply right or wrong. Each captures genuine values that matter to a functioning legal system: certainty and consistency (formalism), honesty about the nature of adjudication (realism), and responsiveness to injustice (activism). The tension between them is not a problem to be solved but a productive friction that keeps the legal system honest about its own nature. In examination questions that ask about judicial philosophy, the goal is not to identify the "correct" philosophy but to demonstrate an understanding of the arguments for and against each, and to apply those arguments to the specific case or scenario at hand.

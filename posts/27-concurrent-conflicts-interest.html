Concurrent Conflicts of Interest: The Firm-as-One-Entity Rule and ASCR Rule 11

SE
Shayeer Emanee
June 2026
7 min read
Professional Responsibility Case Analysis Australian Law
📋

Conflicts of interest are the most litigated and most professionally dangerous area of solicitor conduct. A conflict of interest arises whenever a solicitor's duty to one person is, or may be, inconsistent with a duty to another, or with the solicitor's own interests. The Law Society of NSW has identified concurrent client conflicts - representing two clients at the same time on adverse matters - as one of the leading sources of professional negligence claims and disciplinary proceedings in NSW. This post examines concurrent conflicts and the foundational rule that makes them so pervasive: the treatment of the entire law firm as a single entity.

1. The Firm as One Entity: Imputation of Knowledge

The most important structural principle governing conflicts of interest in solicitor practice is that a law firm is treated as a single unit for the purposes of both conflicts and confidential information. This is called the "imputation rule": information known by one solicitor in the firm is deemed to be known by all other solicitors in the firm.

This principle was authoritatively established in Blackwell v Barroile Pty Ltd (1994), where the Court held that a firm is "in no better position than a sole practitioner if it purports to act for separate clients who are in contention." You cannot cure a conflict simply by having two different solicitors in the firm handle each side - the firm's knowledge is pooled. The conflict infects the entire practice.

The rule was confirmed in a family law context in Hamdan & Alzahrani (Full Court of the Federal Circuit and Family Court of Australia, 2022), which applied the same imputation principle to a firm whose solicitors were acting for parties on opposite sides of property proceedings.

By contrast, the imputation rule does NOT apply to barristers. Barristers are sole practitioners - they do not share knowledge with one another simply by occupying the same chambers. Each barrister's ethical obligations are assessed individually.

Entity Imputation Rule Applies? Consequence
Law firm (solicitors) YES - knowledge of any solicitor is attributed to all Any solicitor acting for Client A is deemed to know everything held for Client B - creating a conflict
Barristers' chambers NO - each barrister is a sole practitioner Barristers in the same chambers can act against each other's clients
Government legal departments Complex - courts have applied imputation with modifications Subject to specific guidance from the relevant authority

2. Concurrent Client Conflicts: ASCR Rule 11

A concurrent (or "current") client conflict arises when a solicitor or firm represents two clients at the same time, and those clients have adverse interests in the same or a related matter. Rule 11 of the Australian Solicitors' Conduct Rules 2015 (NSW) (ASCR) is the governing provision.

Under ASCR Rule 11, a solicitor must not act for two or more clients in the same matter if doing so would, or would be likely to, involve a conflict between the duties owed to each client. The rule allows representation of multiple clients only where:

  • Each client is fully informed about the conflict and its implications.
  • Each client provides written consent to the concurrent representation.
  • An effective information barrier is in place between the solicitors handling each client's matter.
  • The solicitor is satisfied that acting for both clients will not compromise the quality of advice given to either.

In practice, these conditions are rarely satisfied in any matter where the clients' interests are genuinely adverse. Informed consent is not a cure-all: if acting for both clients would compromise the quality of advice to either - for example, if advice that would benefit Client A would harm Client B - consent cannot authorise the representation. The Legal Practitioners Liability Committee (2021) identified concurrent representation as a systemic source of negligence claims because solicitors consistently overestimate their ability to serve two adverse masters simultaneously.

3. High-Risk Scenarios in Concurrent Conflicts

The following scenarios are consistently identified in the professional conduct literature as presenting unacceptable conflict risk:

3.1 Lender and Borrower in the Same Transaction

Acting for both the lender and borrower in a mortgage transaction is one of the most common grounds for discipline in NSW conveyancing practice. The interests of lender and borrower are structurally adverse: the lender wants the strongest possible security, the shortest repayment terms, and the broadest enforcement rights; the borrower wants flexible terms, limited personal liability, and minimal security. A solicitor cannot give full, unqualified advice to both simultaneously. Even in routine residential transactions where there is no obvious dispute, the potential for conflict is latent throughout.

3.2 Buyer and Seller in a Property Transaction

Acting for vendor and purchaser in the same conveyancing transaction presents the same structural problem. The vendor wants the highest price, the tightest conditions, and the fastest settlement; the purchaser wants the opposite. A solicitor cannot act as an impartial broker between adverse contractual parties. This representation is prohibited in most circumstances and expressly identified as a high-risk conflict scenario by the Law Society of NSW.

3.3 Co-Accused in a Criminal Trial

Representing two accused persons in the same criminal matter presents one of the most acute concurrent conflicts in legal practice. From the moment the police charge two people with the same offence, those people have a latent adverse interest: if the prosecution's case against one weakens, it often strengthens against the other. If one accused decides to give evidence implicating the other - or to negotiate a plea deal that involves cooperating with the prosecution - the conflict crystallises immediately. The solicitor is paralysed: advancing the defence of one client requires harming the other. The only permissible course is to cease acting for at least one, and typically both, accused.

3.4 Competing Business Partners or Shareholders

Acting for both parties to a commercial joint venture, or for a company and its individual directors, presents ongoing conflict risk. As long as the relationship is harmonious, the conflict remains latent. But disputes between joint venturers or between a company and its directors are extremely common, and when they arise, the solicitor who acted for both is immediately compromised. They hold each party's confidential information and cannot use it against either.

4. What Must a Solicitor Do When a Concurrent Conflict Arises?

When a concurrent conflict is identified - whether at the outset of a matter or part-way through - the solicitor's obligations are:

  • Immediately cease taking instructions in the conflicted matter until the position is resolved.
  • Assess whether the conflict is one that can be cured by informed consent and an information barrier, or whether it is irreconcilable.
  • If irreconcilable, cease acting for at least one (and potentially both) clients, and advise each client to seek independent legal advice.
  • Ensure that in ceasing to act, the withdrawal does not prejudice either client - adequate notice must be given and, in litigation, court permission may be required.
  • Do not use confidential information obtained from one client in acting for the other.

The solicitor's obligations do not end at withdrawal. Even after ceasing to act for the conflicted party, the duty of confidentiality continues. Information obtained from that client during the retainer cannot subsequently be used for any purpose adverse to them.

5. Information Barriers (Chinese Walls)

An information barrier - sometimes called a "Chinese wall" or "ethical screen" - is an internal firm procedure designed to prevent confidential information held for one client from reaching solicitors acting for an adverse client. Where a genuine and effective information barrier is in place, ASCR Rule 11 permits concurrent representation in some circumstances.

For an information barrier to be effective, it must be more than nominal. Courts have scrutinised these arrangements closely and have required:

  • Physical separation of files and systems - different servers, different filing systems, locked access.
  • Personnel restrictions - solicitors on each side of the barrier must have no contact about the matter and must be instructed accordingly in writing.
  • Supervisory arrangements - a senior partner not involved on either side must monitor compliance.
  • Documentation - the existence and terms of the information barrier must be recorded.

Even a well-constructed information barrier may not be sufficient where the confidential information held is so sensitive and central to the adverse matter that any risk of leakage is unacceptable. In small firms, information barriers are very difficult to make credible - if only three solicitors work in the firm, it is hard to argue that each is truly isolated from the others' information.

6. Conclusion

The concurrent conflict rules protect the solicitor's most fundamental obligation: undivided loyalty to each client. The imputation rule - treating the whole firm as a single unit - means that conflicts can arise even where different solicitors handle each matter without any deliberate sharing of information. The solicitor or firm that fails to conduct a thorough conflict check before accepting a new client, or that persists in a conflicted representation on the basis of hollow consent and inadequate information barriers, exposes itself to professional discipline, negligence liability, and - most importantly - serious damage to the clients it was engaged to serve.

Domestic Violence Law in NSW: AVOs, Criminal Proceedings, and the CDPVA Framework

SE
Shayeer Emanee
June 2026
5 min read
Criminal Law Case Analysis Australian Law
⚡

Domestic and family violence law is one of the highest-volume practice areas in NSW criminal and civil law. Boutique criminal firms in Western Sydney, including many operating in Blacktown and the surrounding area, regularly encounter domestic violence matters in both their criminal and civil jurisdictions. This post provides a comprehensive overview of the NSW domestic violence legal framework: the Crimes (Domestic and Personal Violence) Act 2007 (NSW), the apprehended violence order (AVO) regime, the intersection with criminal proceedings, and the practical considerations that govern this practice area.

1. The Statutory Framework: Crimes (Domestic and Personal Violence) Act 2007 (NSW)

The Crimes (Domestic and Personal Violence) Act 2007 (NSW) (CDPVA) is the primary legislation governing domestic and family violence in NSW. The Act serves two functions: it creates specific criminal offences that apply in the domestic context (including stalking, intimidation, and contravention of AVO orders), and it provides the civil AVO mechanism that allows the courts to impose protective orders on persons who pose a risk of violence or intimidation to a protected person.

The Act defines "domestic relationship" broadly in s 5, encompassing marriage, de facto relationships, intimate personal relationships, cohabitation (past or present), family relationships (including extended family), and caring relationships. This breadth reflects the legislature's recognition that domestic violence does not only occur in the context of current intimate partnerships.

2. Apprehended Domestic Violence Orders (ADVOs)

An Apprehended Domestic Violence Order (ADVO) is a protective civil order made under Part 4 of the CDPVA. An ADVO may be sought by: a police officer on behalf of the person in need of protection (PINOP); the PINOP themselves; or, in some circumstances, a third party with a legitimate interest in the PINOP's protection. In practice, the majority of ADVOs in NSW are made on police application - which means that once police attend a domestic incident, the decision to seek an ADVO is theirs, not the victim's.

The standard conditions of an ADVO prohibit the defendant from: assaulting, threatening, stalking, harassing, or intimidating the PINOP; approaching or contacting the PINOP; going within a specified distance of the PINOP's home, work, or school; and damaging the PINOP's property. Additional conditions can be tailored to the circumstances, including exclusion from a shared residence, surrender of firearms, and restrictions on alcohol consumption.

4. The AVO as a Civil Order: Contravention as Criminal Offence

Although an AVO is itself a civil order, contravention of an AVO is a criminal offence under s 14 of the CDPVA. Section 14 provides that a defendant who, without reasonable excuse, contravenes a prohibition or restriction imposed by an AVO is guilty of a criminal offence punishable by up to two years' imprisonment (for a first offence) or five years' imprisonment (for a subsequent offence or where the contravention involved violence). This creates a significant nexus between the civil and criminal jurisdictions.

The prosecution must prove beyond a reasonable doubt that: the AVO was in force; the defendant was aware of the order; and the defendant's conduct constituted a contravention of the order. The element of knowledge is established where the defendant was present when the order was made, was served with a copy of the order, or otherwise came to know of its terms. The prosecution need not prove that the defendant knew the specific terms in technical detail; awareness of the general nature of the prohibition is sufficient.

5. Interaction with Criminal Proceedings

Where an AVO arises from the same set of facts as a criminal charge - such as an assault occasioning actual bodily harm - the AVO application and the criminal charge are typically heard in the same Local Court proceedings. Section 39 of the CDPVA permits the court to make an ADVO at the same time as sentencing for a domestic violence offence. This is a significant provision because it means that a final ADVO is often made as a consequence of a guilty plea to a related criminal charge, without requiring a separate contested AVO hearing.

This intersection creates complexity for legal practitioners. The appropriate defence strategy in the criminal proceedings may affect the defendant's position on the AVO application - particularly where the defendant consents to an AVO "without admissions" in an attempt to resolve the AVO without conceding the factual basis for the criminal charge. Solicitors must advise clients carefully on how an AVO consent, even without admissions, may affect subsequent proceedings including Family Court proceedings regarding children.

6. Family Violence and the Family Law Act

A state AVO made in connection with domestic violence does not automatically bind the Family Court when it makes orders about the care of children, but it is highly relevant to the Family Court's assessment of risk under s 60CC of the Family Law Act 1975 (Cth). A finding that domestic violence has occurred - particularly where it involves violence towards children or in the presence of children - significantly affects parenting orders and the weight given to risk considerations under the "best interests" framework.

Legal representatives must be alert to the possibility that NSW AVO proceedings and Family Court proceedings are proceeding concurrently. Admissions, consents, or findings in one jurisdiction can materially affect the position in the other. Coordination between NSW criminal/AVO solicitors and family law solicitors is therefore essential in multi-jurisdictional domestic violence matters.

7. Safe Lives, Safe Families: The NSW Strategy

NSW domestic violence law sits within a broader policy framework of the NSW Government's Domestic Violence Strategy and the Safe Lives, Safe Families initiative. This framework prioritises early intervention, coordinated responses between police, courts, and support services, and perpetrator accountability. Legal practitioners advising defendants in AVO proceedings should be familiar with the Domestic Violence Disclosure Scheme and with the Integrated Domestic Violence Court model operating in certain NSW Local Courts, which provides a coordinated approach to civil and criminal proceedings in domestic violence matters.

The Duty of Confidentiality: Sources, Scope, Exceptions, and the Immortal Obligation

SE
Shayeer Emanee
June 2026
8 min read
Professional Responsibility Case Analysis Australian Law
📋

The duty of confidentiality is sometimes described as the foundation on which all of legal practice rests. Without it, clients could not safely disclose to their lawyers the full facts of their situation - the admissions, the weaknesses, the embarrassments, the past conduct that, if disclosed to the wrong party, could destroy them. A client who does not tell their lawyer everything cannot get proper advice. The duty of confidentiality exists precisely to make complete disclosure safe.

What makes confidentiality distinctive in Australian legal ethics is the breadth of its scope and the permanence of its obligation. It has been called the "immortal duty" - it does not expire when the retainer ends, it does not expire when the client dies, and it persists even where the information has become publicly known. This post examines the sources, scope, and exceptions to the solicitor's duty of confidentiality.

1. The Three Sources of the Duty

The duty of confidentiality in Australian legal practice derives from three distinct but overlapping sources. Each source independently creates the obligation, and understanding all three is important because they carry different remedies and operate in different ways.

1.1 Contract

Confidentiality is an implied term of every retainer. Even where the costs agreement or letter of engagement says nothing explicit about confidentiality, the law implies an obligation on the solicitor to keep client information secret. Breach of this implied term gives rise to a claim in contract. However, a contractual claim requires proof of damage - if the disclosure has not caused measurable loss to the client, the contractual remedy may be nominal.

1.2 Equity

The lawyer-client relationship is a fiduciary relationship. Equity imposes on every fiduciary a duty to preserve the confidence of the principal. The equitable basis for the confidentiality duty is significant for two reasons.

First, equity does not require proof of damage. A breach of the equitable obligation of confidence can be remedied even where no actual loss has yet occurred - through an injunction requiring the lawyer not to disclose the information, or through an account of profits if a financial gain was made from the breach.

Second, and crucially for practice, equity permits a client to obtain an urgent injunction to restrain a threatened disclosure before it happens. This is far more valuable than any remedy available after the fact. Once confidential information has been disclosed to the opposing party in litigation, for example, the damage is done; an injunction prevents the breach from occurring in the first place.

1.3 Professional Conduct Rules

ASCR Rule 9 codifies the duty of confidentiality for solicitors. Rule 9.1 provides that a solicitor must not disclose any information which is confidential to a client and acquired by the solicitor during the engagement. Rule 9.2 clarifies that the duty applies even after the retainer has ended.

For barristers, equivalent obligations are found in Rules 114 and 115 of the Legal Profession Uniform Conduct (Barristers) Rules 2015 (NSW).

2. Scope: Extraordinarily Broad

The scope of the confidentiality obligation is extremely wide. It extends to all information received from or about the client during the retainer, regardless of whether it is legally sensitive or personally embarrassing. There is no requirement that the information be "secret" in some technical sense - if it was communicated to the solicitor in the course of the retainer, it is confidential.

This includes:

  • The client's legal strategy and instructions.
  • The client's financial position and assets.
  • The client's admissions - including admissions that are adverse to the client's legal position.
  • The client's personal circumstances, health, relationships, and family situation.
  • Documents and records provided by the client for the purpose of the retainer.
  • Third-party information that the client has shared with the solicitor in the course of getting advice.
  • Even mundane information - what coffee the client drinks, who they are in a relationship with, where they live.

The broad scope reflects the policy rationale: if clients were uncertain about which pieces of information their solicitor might disclose, they would self-censor. Self-censorship prevents full disclosure. Full disclosure is necessary for proper legal advice. The rule is wide precisely because the consequence of drawing it narrowly would be to undermine the entire advisory relationship.

3. The Immortal Character of the Duty

The confidentiality obligation is permanent in a way that most other contractual and professional duties are not. There are two specific dimensions to its persistence:

First, the duty survives the end of the retainer. When a client discharges their solicitor, or when the matter concludes and the engagement ends, the solicitor's confidentiality obligation continues without diminution. A former solicitor cannot give an interview disclosing what their client told them, cannot cooperate with a book about the client's case, and cannot advise a new client using the former client's confidential information.

Second, the duty survives the death of the client. Even after the client has died, their confidential communications with their solicitor cannot be disclosed without lawful authority. This is significant in probate disputes, where parties may attempt to obtain a deceased's solicitor's file to assist in challenging a will.

4. Exceptions: When Disclosure is Permitted

ASCR Rule 9 recognises that the duty of confidentiality, while fundamental, is not absolute. There are five recognised exceptions:

Exception Description Conditions
Client consent Client expressly or impliedly authorises disclosure Must be clear and informed; implied authority must be genuinely implicit in the instructions (e.g., authority to correspond with the other party implies authority to disclose relevant facts of the dispute)
Compulsion by law A court order, subpoena, or statutory requirement compels production The compulsion must be lawful and properly directed at the solicitor; a demand from the police is not sufficient without court process - note that legal professional privilege may still resist compulsory production (see separate post)
Ethical advice Disclosure is necessary to obtain advice about the solicitor's own ethical obligations Limited to disclosure to another solicitor or the Law Society for the purpose of seeking guidance; not a general licence to discuss client matters
Preventing a serious criminal offence Disclosure is necessary to prevent the client or another person from committing a serious criminal offence Discretionary, not mandatory; the offence must be serious; the threat must be sufficiently real and imminent
Preventing probable serious physical harm Disclosure is necessary to prevent probable serious physical harm to the client or another person The risk of harm must be probable (not merely possible) and serious; the exception does not apply to economic harm

Note the critical distinction between exceptions (4) and (5): they permit disclosure but do not require it. Whether to exercise the discretion is a matter of professional judgment. The solicitor must weigh the client's interest in confidence against the seriousness and imminence of the threatened harm.

5. The Molly Tutt Problem: Limits of the Exceptions in Practice

The exceptions to confidentiality must be applied carefully. Consider the following scenario, which illuminates their limits:

Mickey is charged with murder. He tells his defence solicitor Molly that he did in fact commit the killing and has hidden the weapon. Molly, unable to keep this information to herself, contacts the prosecution and discloses the confession. Has Molly acted permissibly?

The analysis:

  • The "prevent serious criminal offence" exception: The crime has already been committed. The exception addresses future crimes, not past conduct. Mickey's confession to the killing cannot be disclosed under this exception.
  • The "prevent physical harm" exception: Again, the harm is past. There is no ongoing threat of physical harm to identify. This exception does not apply.
  • Client consent: Mickey has not consented to disclosure - indeed, the entire point is that he told Molly this in confidence.
  • Compulsion by law: There is no court order requiring Molly to disclose. She is acting voluntarily.

Conclusion: Despite the moral weight of the situation, Molly has breached her duty of confidentiality. She has handed her client's confession to the prosecution. This is a fundamental breach of the adversarial system and of ASCR Rule 9. The adversarial system depends on clients being able to tell their defence lawyers the truth without fear. If defence lawyers could unilaterally disclose admissions of past guilt, clients would stop disclosing - and the system of defence would collapse.

The correct course for Molly was to advise Mickey of his options (including surrendering the weapon through proper channels), decline to put a false case of innocence at trial, and potentially withdraw from the representation - but not to disclose the confession to the prosecution.

6. AI Tools and Confidentiality Risks

A contemporary threat to client confidentiality that the traditional case law did not anticipate is the use of artificial intelligence tools by solicitors and their staff. Where a solicitor uploads client documents, or types client facts, into a public AI platform (such as a commercial large language model tool), that information enters the service provider's system. It may be used to train the AI model, accessed by the provider's staff, or inadequately secured against third-party access.

ASCR Rule 9 does not distinguish between deliberate disclosure to a human being and inadvertent disclosure via a technology platform. Uploading client information to an insufficiently secured AI tool without appropriate data handling protections may constitute a breach of the duty of confidentiality. Solicitors using AI tools for legal tasks must ensure those tools comply with appropriate data security standards, that client information is not used for training purposes, and that terms of service are understood and reviewed.

7. Conclusion

The duty of confidentiality is one of the oldest and most fundamental obligations in the legal profession. Its breadth - extending to all information received during the retainer - and its permanence - persisting after the retainer ends and after the client dies - reflect the foundational importance of the policy it serves: creating conditions in which clients can disclose everything to their lawyers without fear. The exceptions are narrow and must be applied carefully. And in an age of digital practice, the technical means by which confidentiality might be breached have multiplied, requiring solicitors to maintain vigilance not just over their deliberate conduct but over the systems and tools they use in their daily work.

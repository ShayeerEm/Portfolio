Easements in NSW: Creation, Types, and Remedies for Easement Disputes

SE
Shayeer Emanee
June 2026
5 min read
Property Law Case Analysis Australian Law
🏛

Easements are one of the oldest and most practically significant categories of proprietary interest in real property. An easement is a right that one landowner has over the land of another - the classic example being a right of way across a neighbour's property. In New South Wales, easements arise under the common law, by statute, or by implication, and their creation, modification, and extinguishment are governed by a combination of the Conveyancing Act 1919 (NSW) and the Real Property Act 1900 (NSW). This post examines the conceptual foundations of easements, their mode of creation in NSW, and the remedies available for easement disputes.

1. The Essential Characteristics of an Easement

The classic formulation of the characteristics of an easement was set out in Re Ellenborough Park [1956] Ch 131, a decision of the English Court of Appeal that has been accepted as authoritative in Australian law. The four requirements are:

  • There must be a dominant tenement (the land that benefits from the easement) and a servient tenement (the land that is burdened by it).
  • The easement must accommodate and serve the dominant tenement - that is, it must benefit the land as land, not merely confer a personal advantage on the landowner.
  • The dominant and servient tenements must not be owned and occupied by the same person.
  • The right must be capable of forming the subject matter of a grant.

The fourth requirement is perhaps the most subtle. It means that the right claimed must be sufficiently definite and certain to be capable of being conferred by a deed, and it must not amount to a right to exclusive possession of the servient land. An easement that gives its holder effective exclusive control over the servient land is not a true easement but something else - potentially a lease or a profit a prendre.

2. Common Types of Easements in NSW

Type Description Common Trigger
Right of way (carriageway) Right to pass and repass over the servient land Rear lots without street access; drainage access routes
Right to drain water Right to run drainage pipes through the servient land Stormwater/sewerage from landlocked lots
Right of support Right to have buildings supported by adjacent land or structures Shared walls; excavation neighbouring developments
Right to use services Right to run electricity, gas, water, or telecommunications lines Infrastructure servicing residential or commercial lots
Positive covenant Obligation on the servient owner to do something (e.g., maintain a fence) Subdivision conditions; developer-imposed obligations
Restrictive covenant Restriction on use of the servient land (e.g., single dwelling only) Subdivision conditions; protecting character of area

3. Creation of Easements in NSW

Express Grant

The most straightforward method of creating an easement is by express grant - the owner of the servient land executes a deed (or, in NSW, a Dealing) expressly granting the easement to the owner of the dominant tenement. For Torrens title land, the easement must be registered under the Real Property Act 1900 (NSW) to be enforceable against subsequent purchasers of the servient land. Registration is effected by lodging a Dealing in the form prescribed by NSW Land Registry Services.

Section 88B Instrument (Strata and Community Subdivision)

A large proportion of easements in NSW are created on subdivision of land through a s 88B instrument under the Conveyancing Act 1919 (NSW). The s 88B instrument is prepared by the developer's solicitor and lodged with the deposited plan (the subdivision map) at NSW Land Registry Services. It identifies, for each lot in the subdivision, the easements that are to be created - specifying the burden and benefit of each easement by reference to the lot numbers on the deposited plan. Once the plan is registered, the easements are automatically incorporated into the folios of the register for the affected lots.

Easement by Implication and Necessity

Where land is subdivided and sold in circumstances that make it necessary for the transferred land to have access over the retained land - and this necessity is not expressly provided for - the courts may imply an easement by necessity. The doctrine of easement by implication operates on the principle that a grantor cannot derogate from their own grant by withholding a right without which the grant would be worthless. Easements by implication are most commonly encountered in cases where a parcel of land is "landlocked" after subdivision - that is, it has no access to a public road.

Section 88K: Court-Imposed Easements

Section 88K of the Conveyancing Act 1919 (NSW) empowers the Supreme Court to make an order imposing an easement over land where the easement is reasonably necessary for the effective use or development of other land, having regard to public interest. The applicant must demonstrate: that the burden of the easement would be no greater than necessary; that the landowner has been offered compensation; and that the servient land would not be harmed to a degree that would be unreasonable having regard to the purpose for which the easement is required.

4. Extinguishment of Easements

An easement may be extinguished by express release (executed as a deed and registered), by merger (where the dominant and servient tenements come under the same ownership), by abandonment (long non-use coupled with evidence of intention to abandon), or by statute (s 89 of the Conveyancing Act 1919 (NSW) empowers the court to modify or extinguish easements that have become obsolete or have ceased to provide any benefit to the dominant tenement).

5. Remedies for Easement Disputes

Where the servient owner interferes with an established easement - by blocking a right of way, obstructing drainage pipes, or building structures that interfere with a right of support - the dominant owner has a private law remedy in the tort of private nuisance, as well as equitable remedies including injunction and specific performance. In practice, disputes about easements commonly arise in the context of residential developments, where neighbours disagree about the extent of a right of way or the obligations imposed by a s 88B easement. The first step in any easement dispute is a careful review of the registered dealing and the plan of subdivision to determine the precise terms and dimensions of the easement.

Professional Responsibility

Artificial Intelligence in Legal Practice: Hallucinations, Confidentiality, and the Duty to the Court

SE
Shayeer Emanee
June 2026
8 min read
Professional Responsibility Case Analysis Australian Law
📋

Artificial intelligence tools have entered legal practice with a speed that has outpaced the development of the ethical and regulatory frameworks designed to govern their use. Large language models (LLMs) - tools like ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini - can draft documents, summarise cases, and generate legal research at a speed no human lawyer can match. For a profession defined by its monopoly on specialist knowledge and by the precision with which that knowledge must be applied, this development raises questions that go to the foundations of professional ethics: confidentiality, competence, and the duty to the court.

1. Understanding AI Tools in the Legal Context

1.1 Types of AI Relevant to Law

Two categories of AI are relevant to legal practice. Expert systems are rule-based systems that apply "if-then" logic programmed by humans. They are transparent in their reasoning, cannot learn from new information, and produce deterministic outputs. Contract review software that flags non-standard clauses against a pre-set checklist is an example.

Machine learning, and particularly generative AI based on large language models (LLMs), is fundamentally different. LLMs learn from vast datasets of text and generate probabilistic outputs - they predict what text should follow the input, based on patterns in their training data. They are not programmed with explicit rules about legal doctrine. They do not "look up" cases. They produce text that statistically resembles what a person would say in response to the prompt, based on everything they were trained on. The practical consequence is that they can sound authoritative while being entirely wrong.

1.2 The Hallucination Problem

The most significant AI risk for legal practice is "hallucination": the generation by an AI tool of information that does not exist but is presented with complete confidence. In the legal context, this primarily takes the form of fabricated case citations - the AI generates a case name, citation, and plausible-sounding ratio, none of which correspond to any real decision.

The leading example is the United States case Mata v Avianca (2023). A law firm used ChatGPT to assist with legal research. The AI generated a brief containing citations to several cases. The lawyers submitted the brief to the court without verifying whether the cases existed. Not one of the cited cases was real. The court identified the fabrications during oral argument and ordered sanctions against the lawyers.

Mata v Avianca is an extreme example of inadequate supervision of AI output, but the underlying failure - treating AI-generated content as reliable without independent verification - is a risk present in every use of these tools. The AI is designed to sound plausible. Sounding plausible and being accurate are not the same thing. A lawyer who cannot distinguish between the two has not developed the professional judgment that their role requires.

2. The Australian Regulatory Response: Federal Court Practice Note 23

The Federal Court of Australia was among the first Australian courts to issue specific guidance on the use of AI in litigation. Federal Court Practice Note GPN-AI (commenced 3 February 2025) establishes a framework for the use of AI tools in proceedings before the Federal Court and the Federal Circuit and Family Court of Australia. The framework distinguishes between three categories of AI use:

Category Status under PN-AI Conditions
Generating affidavits, witness statements, or evidence PROHIBITED AI must not be used to generate the content of evidence - affidavits and witness statements must reflect the witness's own words and recollection, not AI-generated prose
Expert reports RESTRICTED - requires court permission AI may be used in the preparation of expert reports only with the permission of the court and with full disclosure of the nature and extent of its use
Written submissions and summaries PERMITTED - with strict human verification AI may assist with drafting submissions and research summaries, but only if the lawyer has independently verified all factual and legal content before filing; the lawyer remains personally responsible for the accuracy of every submission

Practice Note 23 also requires that where AI has been used in the preparation of a document filed with the court, the practitioner must certify that they have reviewed and verified the content. This certification requirement makes explicit what should have always been implied: the lawyer, not the AI, is responsible for what goes before the court.

3. Duty of Confidentiality: ASCR Rule 9 and AI

The duty of confidentiality under ASCR Rule 9 is one of the most fundamental obligations in legal practice. It extends to all information received from or about the client during the retainer - and it operates regardless of how the breach of confidentiality occurs. A solicitor who deliberately discloses client information to a third party and a solicitor who inadvertently discloses it by uploading it to an insufficiently secured AI platform have both, in substance, breached the same duty.

The specific risk posed by consumer AI tools is this: when a solicitor enters client information into a public AI platform - describing the client's legal problem, pasting in documents, or uploading correspondence - that information enters the AI service provider's systems. Depending on the terms of service of the platform:

  • The information may be used to train the AI model - meaning it could potentially influence the outputs the AI provides to other users.
  • The information may be retained by the service provider for an indefinite period and subject to their own data security arrangements.
  • The information may be accessible to the service provider's staff under certain conditions.
  • In the event of a data breach, the information may be accessible to unauthorised third parties.

The fact that this disclosure is not deliberate does not make it permissible. ASCR Rule 9 does not require intentional disclosure to create a breach. The solicitor who uploads a client's tax dispute details into a public AI tool without assessing the platform's data handling practices has failed to take adequate steps to protect confidential information.

The appropriate response is not to refuse to use AI tools at all, but to ensure that any AI tool used for client work either operates on a private, enterprise instance that does not share data with the AI provider's training processes, or is subject to data processing agreements that provide appropriate contractual protections for client data. Major AI providers offer enterprise tiers specifically designed for compliance-sensitive environments.

4. Duty of Competence: ASCR Rule 4.1.3

ASCR Rule 4.1.3 requires a solicitor to deliver legal services competently, diligently, and as promptly as reasonably possible. This rule was designed for a world in which the tools of legal research were human law librarians, printed reporters, and legal databases. Its application to AI is straightforward: using AI tools in a way that exposes the client to risk through inadequate verification is a breach of the competence obligation, not a compliance with it.

The competence rule requires that a solicitor who uses AI tools must:

  • Understand sufficiently how the tool works to assess the reliability of its outputs - a solicitor who is entirely ignorant of the hallucination risk is not competent to use AI for legal research.
  • Independently verify all AI-generated content - every case citation, every statutory reference, every factual claim - before relying on it in advice to a client or in submissions to a court.
  • Exercise professional judgment to assess whether the AI's output is consistent with the law as the solicitor understands it. AI output that contradicts well-known legal principles should trigger scepticism, not deference.
  • Not use AI to replace professional judgment, but only to augment or assist it. "Augmentation not automation" is the appropriate model.

5. Duty to the Court: ASCR Rule 19

The duty of candour to the court - the obligation not to make false or misleading statements to the court - is engaged directly by the AI hallucination risk. A solicitor who submits a document to a court containing a fabricated case citation has made a false statement to the court, even if the falsity was not intentional. ASCR Rule 19 does not contain a mental element - it is not limited to knowing misstatements. It extends to situations where the solicitor has placed before the court information that is in fact false, regardless of how that information came to be in the document.

This is not merely theoretical. If a solicitor submits AI-generated submissions citing a case that does not exist, and the opponent or the court identifies the false citation, the solicitor is in breach of Rule 19. The fact that the AI generated the false citation does not transfer responsibility to the AI - the solicitor is responsible for every word in every document they file.

The duty to the court also extends to any arbitration, mediation, or dispute resolution process (not just formal court proceedings). An AI-generated submission placed before a mediator carries the same potential for breach as one filed in the Federal Court.

6. The Learning Dimension: Augmentation, Not Automation

Beyond professional responsibility, there is a longer-term concern about the impact of AI on the development of legal competence. The skills that make a good lawyer - identifying legal issues in factual matrices, researching doctrine, constructing arguments, evaluating evidence - are built through the process of doing those things with difficulty. A law student who uses AI to produce their legal research assignments is not merely risking academic misconduct - they are depriving themselves of the developmental experiences through which professional judgment is built.

The principle "augmentation not automation" captures the appropriate relationship between the lawyer and AI tools. AI that helps a lawyer think more rigorously, check their reasoning, explore alternative arguments, or summarise long documents so that more time can be spent on analysis - this is AI used as a professional tool. AI that produces the analysis for the lawyer to rubber-stamp is AI that is producing a lawyer-shaped output without a lawyer's judgment. The courts and clients are entitled to the latter, not the former.

7. Conclusion

AI tools represent a genuine transformation in the capacity of legal professionals to process information. They also represent a genuine ethical risk for a profession whose obligations rest on precision, confidentiality, and accountability for the content of every document filed and every statement made. The solicitor who ignores these tools will be less effective than peers who use them well. The solicitor who uses them uncritically will find themselves in breach of fundamental professional obligations. The path between these failures runs through professional judgment: understanding what the tools can and cannot do, supervising their outputs rigorously, and maintaining personal responsibility for every professional act.

Binding Financial Agreements under the Family Law Act: Requirements, Risks, and Thorne v Kennedy

SE
Shayeer Emanee
June 2026
5 min read
Family Law Case Analysis Australian Law
🏠

Binding Financial Agreements (BFAs) - colloquially referred to as "prenuptial agreements" (when made before marriage) or "postnuptial agreements" (when made during or after marriage) - allow parties to a relationship to contract out of the property settlement regime under Part VIII of the Family Law Act 1975 (Cth). Where a valid BFA exists, the court's jurisdiction to make property orders is ousted. This makes BFAs a powerful but technically demanding instrument. A BFA that is not correctly executed is not merely unenforceable - it may expose the solicitors who advised on it to negligence claims.

1. Legislative Basis

Financial agreements between married couples are governed by ss 90B to 90KA of the Family Law Act 1975 (Cth). Section 90B provides for agreements before marriage; s 90C during marriage; and s 90D on or after divorce. For de facto couples, the equivalent provisions are ss 90UB to 90UN. The agreement must deal with how the property or financial resources of the parties - or either of them - are to be divided "if the de facto relationship breaks down." A financial agreement that fails to meet the statutory requirements will not oust the court's jurisdiction.

2. Formal Requirements: The Section 90G Trap

Section 90G(1) of the FLA specifies the formal requirements for a financial agreement to be binding. Each party must have received independent legal advice, before signing, from a legal practitioner on the effect of the agreement on their rights and the advantages and disadvantages to that party of making the agreement. The legal practitioner who gave that advice must provide a signed statement confirming that the advice was given, and a copy of that statement must be provided to the other party. The agreement itself must be signed by both parties.

The consequences of non-compliance are severe. An agreement that does not meet the s 90G(1) requirements is not binding. It cannot be made binding by ratification or subsequent conduct. The only remedy for non-compliance is to terminate the defective agreement and execute a new, compliant agreement. This makes the solicitor's obligation of accurate compliance acute: any slip in the certification or in the scope of advice given can invalidate the agreement years later.

3. Setting Aside a Financial Agreement: Section 90K

Even a formally compliant BFA may be set aside by a court under s 90K of the FLA on a number of grounds. The principal grounds for setting aside include:

  • Fraud: The agreement was obtained by fraud, including non-disclosure of a material matter.
  • Unconscionable conduct: The agreement was obtained through unconscionable conduct within the meaning of the unwritten law (including the doctrine of unconscionability in Amadio).
  • Duress or undue influence: A party was forced into the agreement or their will was overborne.
  • Changed circumstances relating to children: Since the agreement was made, a material change in circumstances has occurred relating to the care, welfare, or development of a child, such that the child or carer would suffer hardship if the agreement were not set aside.
  • Impossibility of performance: The agreement cannot be carried out.
  • Unjust and inequitable: Certain provisions of the agreement make it impractical to divide property and it would be unjust and inequitable not to set aside those provisions.

The ground of non-disclosure has been significant in practice. In Thorne v Kennedy (2017) 263 CLR 85, the High Court held that financial agreements entered into in circumstances of emotional pressure, power imbalance, and inadequate time for consideration could be set aside for duress or undue influence. In Thorne, the parties were an Australian woman and a wealthy Greek-Australian man; the woman, who was in an economically vulnerable position and faced a realistic threat that the relationship would end if she did not sign, was found to have signed under duress. The High Court set aside both the pre-nuptial and post-nuptial agreements.

4. Thorne v Kennedy: Implications for Drafting

The High Court's decision in Thorne v Kennedy (2017) 263 CLR 85 has become the leading authority on the limits of BFAs. The Court identified the following features of the Thorne case that contributed to the finding of undue influence and unconscionability: a significant power imbalance; the woman's relative financial and emotional dependence on the man; the short time between presentation of the agreement and the wedding; the terms of the agreement being unfavourable to the woman (she would receive little on breakdown compared to his wealth); and the threat - implicit or explicit - that the relationship would not proceed if she did not sign.

Practitioners drafting BFAs must now attend carefully to all of these matters. Best practice includes: serving the draft agreement on the other party well in advance of the date on which it is to be signed; ensuring both parties have access to independent advice; drafting provisions that are genuinely fair in the circumstances; and ensuring that the solicitor giving advice confirms that the advice was adequate and that the party was freely consenting.

5. Spousal Maintenance in Financial Agreements

Section 90E of the FLA provides that a financial agreement may deal with spousal maintenance. Where the agreement purports to exclude spousal maintenance altogether, s 90E(2) provides that the exclusion is not binding to the extent that it would result in one party to the agreement being unable to support themselves without an income-tested payment under Commonwealth law. In other words, an agreement that would leave one party destitute and reliant on Centrelink is not fully enforceable to the extent of that destitution.

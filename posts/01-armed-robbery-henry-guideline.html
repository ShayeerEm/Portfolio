Objective Seriousness in Armed Robbery: The Henry Guideline and Its Limits

SE
Shayeer Emanee
June 2026
6 min read
Criminal Law Case Analysis Australian Law
⚡

Few sentencing principles have proved as persistently contested in New South Wales criminal law as the proper assessment of objective seriousness in armed robbery. The guideline judgment of R v Henry (1999) 46 NSWLR 346 established a framework for sentencing the "frequently recurring case" of armed robbery - but, as the appellate courts have repeatedly emphasised, that framework is not a straitjacket. This post examines how NSW courts assess objective seriousness in armed robbery across the full spectrum of factual circumstances, drawing on the foundational decisions and their subsequent application.

1. The R v Henry Guideline: Scope and Mechanism

In R v Henry (1999) 46 NSWLR 346, the NSW Court of Criminal Appeal issued a guideline judgment directed at the "frequently recurring case" of armed robbery of small businesses by offenders wielding knives. The guideline established that, for an offence of that character, the appropriate starting point for a first-time offender who pleaded not guilty was a sentence in the range of four to five years' imprisonment. The judgment was not intended to be applied prescriptively to every armed robbery; its purpose was to anchor sentencing discretion where the factual matrix closely resembled the paradigm case.

The Court in Henry identified the key objective features that informed the guideline: a high degree of premeditation, a weapon with real capacity to cause grievous bodily harm (specifically a knife), deliberate intimidation of a vulnerable victim, and theft of cash or goods as the primary motivation. Where these features are substantially absent or present only in attenuated form, the guideline's prescriptive force is correspondingly diminished.

2. The Spectrum of Weapon Severity

The nature and capacity of the weapon is the single most important objective factor in armed robbery sentencing. In R v Henry itself, the Court surveyed a range of weapons: filleting knives, carving knives, and syringes. In associated companion proceedings, the use of a syringe filled with red liquid was described as falling "right at the outside limits of seriousness" of the weapons the guideline contemplated.

Subsequent appellate authority has further developed the spectrum. In Luchetti v R [2025] NSWCCA 222, the Court held that the use of a cigarette lighter - brandished as if a firearm but incapable of causing grievous bodily harm - placed the offence so far below the Henry paradigm that the guideline had "no role to perform." The Court described the weapon's capacity for harm as "profoundly different" from knives and directed sentencing courts to articulate clearly the distance between the offence before them and the guideline scenario.

Knife (filleting/carving) High - potential lethal Directly within Henry guideline
Syringe (liquid) Moderate-High - psychological terror and potential contamination At outer limits of guideline
Imitation firearm Low physical / High psychological Partial application - assess proximity
Cigarette lighter (as pseudo-gun) Minimal Guideline has no role - Luchetti v R

3. The Role of Mental Health in Culpability

Sections 21A and 24 of the Crimes (Sentencing Procedure) Act 1999 (NSW) direct the court to have regard to the extent to which the offender's mental condition at the time of the offence reduced their moral culpability. This principle was central in Luchetti v R [2025] NSWCCA 222, where the appellant suffered from chronic treatment-resistant schizophrenia, auditory hallucinations, and substance use disorder. The Court affirmed the long-standing approach from Verdins v R [2007] VSCA 102, applied in NSW, which identifies six ways in which mental disorder can mitigate sentence.

  • Reduced moral culpability or blameworthiness
  • Mental illness makes imprisonment a more onerous experience
  • Mental illness means the offender is less suitable for general deterrence
  • Mental illness makes specific deterrence less relevant
  • Mental illness increases the prospect of rehabilitation
  • Mental illness may make the offender more vulnerable in custody

In Luchetti, the Court made a significant observation regarding the onerousness of custody. Where psychiatric evidence establishes that an offender is institutionalised and actually prefers custody to the community setting due to mental illness, the ordinary assumption that custody is "more onerous" for the mentally ill may not apply. The Court found that, on the evidence, Mr Luchetti was "more frightened in the community than in custody," which negated this particular mitigating pathway.

4. Procedural Framework: Appeals Against Sentence

An offender who seeks to challenge a sentence imposed in the District Court must apply for leave to appeal to the NSW Court of Criminal Appeal pursuant to s 5(1)(c) of the Criminal Appeal Act 1912 (NSW). The grounds of appeal must identify a legal error - whether of principle, fact, or in the exercise of discretion. A sentence will not be disturbed merely because the appellate court might have imposed a different sentence; there must be an identifiable legal error or a sentence so manifestly excessive as to imply error: Markarian v R (2005) 228 CLR 357.

The most common errors identified in armed robbery sentences are: (i) erroneous application of a guideline to factually remote circumstances; (ii) failure to disclose the assessment of objective seriousness in the reasons; (iii) failure to give weight to mental health conditions under the Verdins principles; and (iv) failure to properly calculate the discount for a guilty plea under s 25D of the Crimes (Sentencing Procedure) Act 1999 (NSW).

5. The Early Guilty Plea Discount

The discount for an early guilty plea is governed by ss 25A-25F of the Crimes (Sentencing Procedure) Act 1999 (NSW), as amended by the Justice Legislation Amendment (Committals and Guilty Pleas) Act 2017 (NSW). A discount of up to 25% applies for a plea entered at the earliest opportunity. Where a plea is entered after a committal but before trial, a discount of up to 10-15% is appropriate. In Luchetti, the 25% discount was applied and not challenged on appeal. The primary judge's starting point of four years' imprisonment was therefore reduced to a head sentence of three years, with the Court ultimately re-sentencing to 18 months' imprisonment after correctly assessing objective seriousness.

6. Synthesis: Principles for Practitioners

Armed robbery sentencing in NSW requires a holistic assessment that begins - but does not end - with the Henry guideline. The following propositions emerge from the authorities as settled law:

  • The Henry guideline applies to the "frequently recurring case" and must not be mechanically applied to factually remote circumstances.
  • A sentencing judge who invokes Henry is obliged to explicitly articulate the distance between the offence and the guideline scenario, particularly regarding the weapon.
  • Mental health conditions reduce moral culpability, but the reduction depends on the specific nature, causation, and interaction of the illness with the offending.
  • The onerousness of custody for mentally ill offenders requires individualised assessment; the ordinary presumption may be displaced by evidence of institutionalisation.
  • The guilty plea discount must be applied correctly and its calculation exposed in the reasons.
  • An appellate court re-sentences afresh on appeal; prior time in custody is credited against the new sentence.

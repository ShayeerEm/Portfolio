Legal Professional Privilege: Dominant Purpose, In-House Lawyers, and the Shield-Not-Sword Rule

SE
Shayeer Emanee
June 2026
8 min read
Professional Responsibility Case Analysis Australian Law
📋

Legal professional privilege - also called client legal privilege - is frequently confused with the duty of confidentiality. They are related but distinct concepts. Confidentiality prevents a solicitor from voluntarily disclosing client information. Privilege goes further: it resists compulsory disclosure, meaning that even where a court orders production of documents, or a regulator issues a subpoena, privileged communications may be withheld. Privilege is a stronger protection, but it covers a narrower category of communications. Understanding the distinction - and the precise boundaries of the privilege - is essential for any legal practitioner.

1. The Core Distinction

Feature Confidentiality Legal Professional Privilege
Legal basis Contract (implied term of retainer), equity, ASCR Rule 9 Common law (absolute right at common law); Evidence Act 1995 (NSW) ss 118-119
Scope ALL information received during the retainer, regardless of purpose Only communications made for the DOMINANT PURPOSE of legal advice or litigation
What it resists Voluntary disclosure by the solicitor Compulsory disclosure - court orders, subpoenas, regulatory demands
Strength Can be overridden by court order or statute Resists all compulsory processes (unless waived or another exception applies)
Duration Permanent - survives end of retainer and death of client Can be lost if confidentiality is waived or lost
Who owns it Belongs to the client (but also protects the solicitor) Belongs to the CLIENT - only the client can waive it

2. The Dominant Purpose Test

The most important limiting principle in privilege law is the dominant purpose test. Established by the High Court in Esso Australia Resources Ltd v Federal Commissioner of Taxation (1999) 201 CLR 49, the test requires that the dominant purpose - the ruling, primary, or prevailing purpose - of the communication must have been either:

  • To obtain, or provide, legal advice (legal advice privilege); OR
  • To use in actual or reasonably anticipated legal proceedings (litigation privilege).

"Dominant" means more than merely a purpose, or even one of several significant purposes - it must be the main, overriding purpose. If a document serves multiple purposes (both legal advice and commercial strategy, for example), privilege will only attach if the legal purpose is the principal one. A document prepared partly for legal advice and partly for business decision-making, where the commercial consideration is the driver, will not be privileged.

The test operates document by document. A file of 500 documents cannot be blanket-claimed as privileged; each document must independently satisfy the dominant purpose requirement. In practice, solicitors often over-claim privilege, asserting it for documents where the legal purpose was subsidiary to a commercial one. Courts have consistently scrutinised these claims.

3. What Does Privilege Protect?

3.1 Communications Between Solicitor and Client

The core category protected by privilege is confidential communications between a solicitor and their client made for the dominant purpose of legal advice. This includes:

  • Written legal advice provided by the solicitor to the client.
  • Letters and emails from the client to the solicitor seeking advice.
  • Notes taken by the solicitor in conference with the client.
  • Instructions given by the client to the solicitor about how to proceed.

Privilege does not protect the underlying facts the client communicates to the solicitor - only the communication itself. If a client tells their solicitor that they signed a contract on a particular date, the fact of that signing is not privileged (the client can be asked about it in cross-examination); but the solicitor's letter of advice about the legal consequences of that signing is privileged.

3.2 Communications for the Dominant Purpose of Litigation

Litigation privilege protects communications between a solicitor, client, and third parties - including experts, witnesses, and investigators - where the dominant purpose of those communications is preparation for litigation that is anticipated or on foot. A report commissioned from a medical expert for the purpose of a personal injury claim is privileged; a report commissioned to assist a business decision is not. The key question is always: why was this document brought into existence?

4. In-House Lawyers: The Two Hats Problem

In-house counsel - solicitors employed directly by a company rather than a law firm - present particular difficulties in privilege analysis. An in-house lawyer wears two hats: they are a legal adviser and a commercial executive simultaneously. They attend board meetings, make business decisions, participate in strategy, and provide legal opinions - sometimes in the same email chain.

In Seven Network Limited v News Limited [2005] FCA 142, Tamberlin J grappled with this problem. His Honour observed: "There is no bright line separating the role of an employed legal counsel as a lawyer advising in-house and his participation in commercial decisions." The Federal Court held that privilege should not be denied merely because there is some commercial involvement, provided the dominant purpose of the communication was legal advice.

The practical consequence for in-house lawyers is that the privilege analysis must be applied with particular care to mixed communications. An email that weaves legal advice into a broader commercial discussion may lose privilege if the commercial discussion is the dominant purpose. Prudent in-house lawyers separate their legal advice from their commercial input - ideally in separate documents - to preserve the clarity of the privilege claim.

5. Privilege as a Shield, Not a Sword: Glencore

One of the most important limitations on privilege in Australian law is that it operates as a shield only - it cannot be used as a cause of action or as a sword against a third party.

This was definitively established by the High Court in Glencore International AG v Commissioner of Taxation [2019] HCA 26. Glencore's internal documents - including privileged communications - were among the millions of files disclosed in the "Paradise Papers" data leak. The documents ended up in the hands of the Australian Taxation Office, which began using them in its audit of Glencore. Glencore brought proceedings seeking an injunction to compel the ATO to return the documents and prevent the ATO from making use of them, on the basis that the documents were subject to legal professional privilege.

The High Court unanimously rejected Glencore's claim. The Court held that privilege is a rule of evidence - it operates to prevent compulsory production in legal proceedings, but it does not create a cause of action to recover documents that have already left the client's control. Once the confidentiality of a privileged document is lost - whether through theft, hacking, inadvertent disclosure, or otherwise - privilege cannot be used as an independent legal basis to demand the documents back or to prevent their use.

Client legal privilege is a rule of substantive law which entitles a client to resist the adducing of evidence that would result in a disclosure of the contents of a confidential communication between a lawyer and a client made for the dominant purpose of giving or obtaining legal advice.

The Glencore decision has significant practical implications. Documents that are carelessly disclosed, digitally leaked, or inadvertently sent to the wrong recipient lose their practical protection against use by the recipient, even if the privilege claim technically survives the loss of confidentiality as a matter of law. The Court confirmed that the appropriate remedy for inadvertent disclosure is governed by other legal principles (such as the equitable obligations of the recipient), not by privilege itself.

6. Waiver of Privilege

Because privilege belongs to the client, only the client can waive it. Waiver can be express (the client chooses to disclose the privileged communication) or implied (the client's conduct is inconsistent with maintaining the privilege). Implied waiver is a trap for the unwary.

Examples of conduct that can imply waiver include:

  • A client who sues their solicitor for negligent advice impliedly waives privilege over the advice given, to the extent necessary to determine the negligence claim.
  • A party who discloses part of a privileged document in litigation may waive privilege over the whole document, particularly if partial disclosure would be unfair to the other side.
  • A party who relies on their solicitor's advice to establish a state of mind - for example, arguing they acted in good faith on legal advice - may waive privilege over that advice.
  • Inadvertent disclosure of a privileged document to the other side during discovery can constitute waiver if the client's conduct is inconsistent with maintaining confidentiality.

7. Without Prejudice Privilege in Negotiations

Closely related to legal professional privilege, but conceptually distinct, is "without prejudice" or negotiation privilege. This protects communications made in genuine settlement negotiations from being adduced as evidence in subsequent proceedings. The policy rationale - established in Field v Commissioner for Railways for NSW (1957) 99 CLR 285 - is that parties should be able to make offers, concessions, and admissions in the course of settlement discussions without fear that their candour will be used against them at trial.

Under s 131 of the Evidence Act 1995 (NSW), evidence of settlement negotiations is inadmissible except in the circumstances specified. The main exceptions include:

  • Where both parties consent to the admission of the communication.
  • Where there is a dispute about whether a settlement agreement was actually reached.
  • Where withholding the communication would be contrary to justice.
  • Where the communication was made to facilitate fraud or an abuse of power.

Marking a document "Without Prejudice" does not automatically make it privileged - just as marking it "Privileged and Confidential" does not automatically attract legal professional privilege. The label is a signal of the parties' intention, but the substantive test (whether the communication was genuinely made in settlement negotiations) must still be satisfied.

8. Conclusion

Legal professional privilege is one of the most important protections in the common law - and one of the most misunderstood. Its power is the power to resist even court-ordered compulsory disclosure, but it comes with exacting requirements: the dominant purpose of the communication must be legal advice or litigation preparation, the privilege must not have been waived, and once confidentiality is lost it cannot be reclaimed as a cause of action (Glencore). For solicitors, understanding the precise scope of privilege - and the difference between privilege and confidentiality - is not merely academic. It is a practical necessity in every transaction, every dispute, and every interaction with clients and their documents.

Family Provision Claims in NSW: Challenging a Will under Chapter 3 of the Succession Act 2006

SE
Shayeer Emanee
June 2026
5 min read
Wills & Estates Case Analysis Australian Law
📜

A testator in NSW has broad freedom to dispose of their property as they wish. But that freedom is not absolute. Chapter 3 of the Succession Act 2006 (NSW) empowers the Supreme Court to make orders for provision from a deceased estate where adequate provision has not been made for the "maintenance, education, and advancement in life" of an eligible person. These claims - commonly called family provision claims or TFM (testator's family maintenance) claims - represent one of the most litigated areas of succession law and generate a significant proportion of work in estate law practices.

1. Eligible Persons: Who Can Claim?

Section 57 of the Succession Act 2006 (NSW) defines the categories of "eligible persons" who may bring a family provision claim. The categories are:

  • The spouse (including de facto spouse) of the deceased at the time of death.
  • A child of the deceased (including adopted children and step-children).
  • A former spouse of the deceased.
  • A person who was, at any particular time, wholly or partly dependent on the deceased, and who is a grandchild of the deceased or who was, at that particular time, a member of the household of which the deceased was also a member.
  • A person with whom the deceased was living in a close personal relationship at the time of death.

The breadth of the eligible persons definition means that a wide range of claimants may apply. However, membership of an eligible category does not guarantee a successful claim - it is merely the threshold. Once eligibility is established, the court must separately assess whether adequate provision has been made and, if not, what further provision is appropriate.

2. The Two-Stage Inquiry

The family provision inquiry in NSW proceeds in two stages, as confirmed by the New South Wales Court of Appeal in Singer v Berghouse (1993) 16 Fam LR 502 and by the High Court in Singer v Berghouse (1994) 181 CLR 201:

  • Has the testator failed to make adequate provision for the proper maintenance, education, and advancement in life of the eligible person?
  • If so, what order should the court make to remedy the inadequacy?

These two stages are logically distinct. The court first assesses whether the will (or the intestacy provisions) adequately provides for the claimant. If the answer is no, the court then turns to what provision to make - and this second inquiry involves a broader range of considerations, including the size of the estate, the claims of competing beneficiaries, and the court's overall assessment of what is just in all the circumstances.

3. Factors in the Assessment: Section 60

Section 60 of the Succession Act 2006 (NSW) lists the factors that the court must consider in determining both stages of the inquiry. These factors include:

  • The relationship between the claimant and the deceased, including the nature and duration of the relationship.
  • The nature and extent of the claimant's financial resources and financial needs, both present and future.
  • The nature and extent of the deceased's estate.
  • Any contribution by the claimant to the acquisition, conservation, or improvement of the estate.
  • The nature and extent of obligations and responsibilities owed by the deceased to the claimant and to other persons.
  • The financial circumstances of any competing beneficiaries.
  • Any provision made for the claimant by the deceased during the deceased's lifetime, whether by way of gift, maintenance, or otherwise.
  • Whether the deceased made any family provision agreement with the claimant.
  • Any evidence of the testamentary intentions of the deceased.
  • The character and conduct of the claimant before and after the death of the deceased.

4. "Adequate Provision" and the Role of "Needs"

The concept of "adequate provision" is not defined in the Act. Case law establishes that adequacy is assessed by reference to the claimant's "needs" - not in a narrow sense, but broadly construed to include not merely bare subsistence but a proper standard of living having regard to the claimant's circumstances and reasonable expectations. In Vigolo v Bostin (2005) 221 CLR 191, the High Court confirmed that the assessment of adequate provision requires consideration of the claimant's situation, including their economic circumstances, their health, their relationship with the deceased, and their contributions to the estate.

5. Time Limit: Section 58

A family provision application must be filed in the Supreme Court within 12 months of the date of the death of the deceased: s 58(2) of the Succession Act 2006 (NSW). The court has a discretion to extend this time limit, but the discretion is exercised sparingly. An applicant who seeks an extension must demonstrate a satisfactory explanation for the delay and must establish that the extension would not cause prejudice to the estate or to other beneficiaries. Where significant distributions have been made from the estate, an extension becomes more difficult to obtain because the court cannot restore the position of those who received distributions in good faith.

6. The Notional Estate: Attacking Pre-Death Transfers

One of the most powerful and distinctive features of NSW family provision law is the "notional estate" jurisdiction under ss 75-88 of the Succession Act 2006 (NSW). Where the deceased, in the three years before death (or at any time, in certain cases), made a "relevant property transaction" - such as transferring property to a third party, creating a trust, or altering their superannuation nominations - the court may designate property transferred in that transaction as "notional estate" and make provision from it as if it formed part of the actual estate.

The notional estate provisions are designed to prevent testators (or their advisers) from "estate planning" around family provision claims by transferring assets out of the estate before death. They are one of the most controversial aspects of NSW succession law and have generated significant litigation, particularly in the context of testamentary trusts, family discretionary trusts, and superannuation binding nominations.

7. Costs in Family Provision Claims

Family provision proceedings are unusual in that the court has a broad discretion to order that costs be paid from the estate - including the costs of an unsuccessful claimant, in appropriate circumstances. This reflects the view that family provision claims serve an important social function (ensuring that family members are not left without provision) and that unsuccessful claimants should not always be deterred by the risk of a full adverse costs order. However, the discretion is not open-ended: a claimant who brings a hopeless or exaggerated claim, or who rejects a reasonable offer, will generally not be protected from adverse costs.

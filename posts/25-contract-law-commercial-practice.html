Contract Law Fundamentals for Commercial Practice: Formation, Breach, and Remedies under Australian Law

SE
Shayeer Emanee
June 2026
11 min read
Commercial Law Case Analysis Australian Law
$

Contract law is the connective tissue of commercial practice. Whether a solicitor acts for a client in a business sale, a service agreement dispute, or a construction project gone wrong, the same fundamental doctrines govern the analysis: formation, consideration, terms, breach, and remedies. For paralegals and legal assistants working in commercial, dispute resolution, or general practice settings, a confident command of Australian contract law principles is indispensable.

This post surveys the core doctrines of contract law as applied by Australian courts, with particular attention to leading High Court authority and the practical implications for commercial disputes.

1. Formation: Offer, Acceptance, and Certainty

1.1 Offer and Acceptance

A binding contract requires an offer by one party and an unqualified acceptance by the other. The classical analysis - while sometimes criticised as artificially mechanistic - remains the dominant analytical framework in Australian courts: see Masters v Cameron (1954) 91 CLR 353 and, more recently, Crown Melbourne Ltd v Cosmopolitan Hotel (Vic) Pty Ltd (2016) 260 CLR 1.

An offer must be distinguished from an invitation to treat. A shop displaying goods, an advertisement in a newspaper, or a tender request are generally invitations to treat - they invite offers from the other party but are not themselves capable of acceptance. The distinction matters because only an offer can be accepted to form a contract.

Acceptance must be unconditional and communicated to the offeror. A purported acceptance that introduces new terms is a counter-offer, which destroys the original offer: Hyde v Wrench (1840) 49 ER 132, applied in Australian courts consistently. In commercial negotiations conducted by an exchange of standard-form contracts, the "battle of the forms" question - which party's terms govern - requires careful analysis of the sequence of communications.

1.2 Certainty and Completeness

An agreement will not be enforced if its essential terms are too uncertain to give them meaning, or if material terms remain to be agreed. In Biotechnology Australia Pty Ltd v Pace (1988) 15 NSWLR 130, the NSW Court of Appeal held that an agreement to provide "share participation" in a company was too uncertain to enforce because the mechanism for allocating shares was undefined.

However, Australian courts apply a strong presumption in favour of contractual certainty: a court will strive to give effect to the parties' bargain if it is reasonably possible to do so. Courts will fill gaps by implication, use standard trade terms, or adopt market practice to render an otherwise uncertain agreement enforceable. Mere incompleteness - where the parties have agreed on all essential terms but left minor details to be worked out - is not fatal: Meehan v Jones (1982) 149 CLR 571.

2. Consideration

Consideration is the price paid for a promise. Every simple (non-deed) contract requires consideration from both parties. The classic formulation - that consideration is some benefit to the promisor or some detriment to the promisee - was affirmed by the High Court in Australian Woollen Mills Pty Ltd v The Commonwealth (1954) 92 CLR 424.

Key principles:

  • Consideration must be sufficient but need not be adequate - the courts will not inquire into whether the parties struck a fair bargain, provided each side has given something of legal value.
  • Past consideration is not good consideration - a promise to pay for something already done is unenforceable for want of consideration: Roscorla v Thomas (1842) 3 QB 234.
  • Performing an existing contractual duty is not generally good consideration for a fresh promise - though Australian courts have recognised exceptions where the performance conferred a practical benefit on the promisor: see Musumeci v Winadell Pty Ltd (1994) 34 NSWLR 723 (Santow J, adopting the English approach in Williams v Roffey).
  • Consideration must move from the promisee - a third party who has provided no consideration generally cannot enforce a contract (subject to the statutory exceptions for third-party beneficiaries in some jurisdictions).

In practice, consideration issues arise most commonly in the context of contract variations. Where a party agrees to a variation - for example, accepting a lower price, or agreeing to extend a deadline - the variation is only binding if supported by fresh consideration from both sides, or if executed as a deed.

3. Contractual Terms

3.1 Express and Implied Terms

Contractual obligations may be created expressly (by the parties' words) or by implication. Courts imply terms in two ways: terms implied in fact (specific to the contract) and terms implied in law (applicable to all contracts of a particular type).

The test for terms implied in fact was authoritatively stated in BP Refinery (Westernport) Pty Ltd v Shire of Hastings (1977) 180 CLR 266 (the BP Refinery test). A term will be implied in fact only if it is:

  • Reasonable and equitable.
  • Necessary to give business efficacy to the contract.
  • So obvious that it goes without saying.
  • Capable of clear expression.
  • Consistent with the express terms of the contract.

All five conditions must be satisfied. Implication on the basis of the "business efficacy" and "obvious" tests are alternatives rather than cumulative: Codelfa Construction Pty Ltd v State Rail Authority of NSW (1982) 149 CLR 337 (Mason J). Post-Codelfa, the High Court in Byrne v Australian Airlines Ltd (1995) 185 CLR 410 confirmed that terms may also be implied in law - as incidents of a particular category of contract - where the implication is necessary or just and reasonable.

3.2 Conditions, Warranties, and Intermediate Terms

Term Type Definition Effect of Breach
Condition A term going to the root of the contract; its performance is a precondition of the other party's obligation Innocent party may terminate AND claim damages
Warranty A lesser term that is collateral to the main purpose of the contract Innocent party may claim damages only; cannot terminate for breach of warranty alone
Intermediate (innominate) term A term whose classification depends on the seriousness of the breach Termination only if the breach goes to the root of the contract; otherwise damages only

The innominate (or intermediate) term category was recognised by the High Court in Koompahtoo Local Aboriginal Land Council v Sanpine Pty Ltd (2007) 233 CLR 115. The significance is that not all breaches of the same term will have the same consequence: a minor non-delivery of goods may sound in damages only, while a total failure to deliver may justify termination.

4. Breach of Contract

A breach of contract occurs when a party fails to perform a contractual obligation, or performs it defectively. Breaches are classified as:

  • Actual breach: the party simply fails to perform when performance is due.
  • Anticipatory breach: a party repudiates the contract before the time for performance arrives, either by express statement or by conduct making it clear they will not perform.

Repudiation - sometimes called "renunciation" - entitles the innocent party to accept the repudiation and treat the contract as terminated immediately, without waiting for the date for performance: Foran v Wight (1989) 168 CLR 385. Alternatively, the innocent party may elect to affirm the contract and insist on performance; but affirmation carries a risk that subsequent breaches by the innocent party may themselves constitute repudiation.

5. Termination of Contracts

A contract may be terminated on the following grounds:

Ground Requirements Key Authority
Breach of condition The breached term is a condition of the contract Associated Newspapers Ltd v Bancks (1951) 83 CLR 322
Repudiation/renunciation Clear indication of unwillingness or inability to perform Foran v Wight (1989) 168 CLR 385
Sufficiently serious breach of intermediate term The consequences of breach are so serious as to deprive the innocent party of substantially the whole benefit of the contract Koompahtoo v Sanpine (2007) 233 CLR 115
Failure of a condition precedent A pre-condition to the contract's operation has not been fulfilled Meehan v Jones (1982) 149 CLR 571
Frustration An event not caused by either party has made performance impossible or radically different from what was contracted for Davis Contractors Ltd v Fareham UDC [1956] AC 696; Scanlan's New Neon Ltd v Tooheys Ltd (1943) 67 CLR 169

The right to terminate must be exercised carefully. An erroneous termination - where the terminating party lacks a valid ground - is itself a repudiation of the contract, reversing the positions of the parties: Photo Production Ltd v Securicor Transport Ltd [1980] AC 827. In practice, advice on whether grounds to terminate exist is one of the most consequential (and commercially sensitive) opinions a solicitor gives.

6. Remedies for Breach

6.1 Damages

The primary remedy for breach of contract is compensatory damages. The purpose of contract damages is not to punish the defendant but to put the innocent party in the position they would have been in had the contract been performed: Robinson v Harman (1848) 1 Exch 850, adopted in Australia in Bellgrove v Eldridge (1954) 90 CLR 613.

Damages are subject to three limiting doctrines:

  • Remoteness: Only losses within the reasonable contemplation of both parties at the time of contracting are recoverable. The two limbs of Hadley v Baxendale (1854) 9 Exch 341 - as reformulated in Victoria Laundry (Windsor) Ltd v Newman Industries Ltd [1949] 2 KB 528 and applied in Australian courts - draw a distinction between losses arising naturally from the breach (first limb) and losses arising from special circumstances known to the defendant at contracting (second limb).
  • Mitigation: The innocent party must take reasonable steps to mitigate (reduce) their loss. They cannot recover for loss that could have been avoided by reasonable action: Payzu Ltd v Saunders [1919] 2 KB 581.
  • Causation: The breach must be a cause of the loss. Where the loss would have occurred in any event, the plaintiff may not recover: Alexander v Cambridge Credit Corp Ltd (1987) 9 NSWLR 310.

6.2 Specific Performance

Specific performance - an order requiring the defendant to perform their contractual obligations - is an equitable remedy granted at the court's discretion. It will be ordered only where damages are inadequate, typically in contracts for the sale of land (where each parcel is regarded as unique) or for the transfer of unique chattels.

Specific performance will not be ordered where: it would require constant court supervision; the contract is for personal services; the party seeking relief has not performed their own obligations (the "clean hands" principle); or hardship to the defendant disproportionate to the benefit to the plaintiff would result.

6.3 Injunction

An injunction may restrain a contracting party from acting in breach of a negative stipulation - for example, a restraint of trade clause or a confidentiality undertaking. The court applies the well-known American Cyanamid principles (as modified in Australia), asking whether there is a serious question to be tried, whether the balance of convenience favours an injunction, and whether damages would be an adequate remedy: Australian Broadcasting Corporation v O'Neill (2006) 227 CLR 57.

6.4 Restitution for Unjust Enrichment

Where a contract is ineffective (void, voidable, or frustrated), a party who has conferred a benefit may seek restitution to reverse the unjust enrichment of the other. In Australian jurisprudence, the unjust enrichment action was recognised in Pavey & Matthews Pty Ltd v Paul (1987) 162 CLR 221, where the High Court held that a builder could recover a quantum meruit for work done under an unenforceable oral contract.

7. Statutory Overlay: The Australian Consumer Law

Commercial contracts in Australia are overlaid by the Australian Consumer Law (ACL), Schedule 2 to the Competition and Consumer Act 2010 (Cth). Key provisions of practical relevance include:

  • s 18 ACL: Prohibition on misleading or deceptive conduct. This provision frequently supplements (or replaces) contract law claims where a party was induced to enter a contract by misrepresentations. Unlike common law fraudulent misrepresentation, s 18 requires no proof of intent; the conduct need only have a capacity to mislead.
  • ss 20-22 ACL: Unconscionable conduct. Courts may grant relief where one party engages in unconscionable conduct in the making or performance of a contract, having regard to the relative bargaining positions and other factors.
  • ss 23-28 ACL: Unfair contract terms. Unfair terms in standard-form consumer and small business contracts are void. From November 2023 (Treasury Laws Amendment (More Competition, Better Prices) Act 2022 (Cth)), unfair terms are also a civil penalty provision, significantly raising the stakes for businesses using standard form contracts.

8. Conclusion

Mastery of contract law fundamentals - formation, consideration, terms, breach, termination, and remedies - provides the analytical framework within which almost every commercial dispute can be understood. For the paralegal, this knowledge translates directly into the ability to assist with drafting instructions, reviewing contracts for breach, preparing chronologies of correspondence, and identifying the legal issues that will drive a matter. The intersection of contract law with the ACL means that the statutory dimension must always be considered alongside the common law analysis in any commercial context.

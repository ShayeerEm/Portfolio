The Latin Maxims of Statutory Interpretation: Noscitur, Ejusdem Generis, Expressio Unius, and Generalia Specialibus

SE
Shayeer Emanee
June 2026
7 min read
Foundations of Law Case Analysis Australian Law
§

The Latin maxims of statutory interpretation are tools that courts use to work out the meaning of words - particularly in lists, catch-all terms, and provisions where a term's scope is unclear. They are not rigid rules of law; they are presumptions about linguistic usage that can be displaced by context, purpose, or express indication in the statute. The High Court has been clear that these maxims are not inflexible, and that they must always give way to the overall interpretive task of giving effect to the legislature's intention. Used appropriately, however, they are valuable aids to reasoning about what a statute means.

1. Noscitur a Sociis - Known by Its Associates

Noscitur a sociis means "a word is known by its associates." The principle is that the meaning of an ambiguous or general word is illuminated by the company it keeps - by the other words used alongside it in the same provision. A word in a list takes colour from the other items in that list; if those other items share a common character, the ambiguous word should be understood in light of that character.

The rationale is linguistic common sense. When a legislature drafts a provision using a list of terms, the terms in the list are typically chosen because they are related - they share a genus or category. A word in that list should be read as belonging to that category.

Worked Example: Scott v Insurance Australia Limited

In Scott v Insurance Australia Limited, the question was whether "attendant care services" provided by the plaintiff's family members were covered by a motor accident insurance scheme. The phrase "attendant care services" appeared alongside references to professional nursing, therapy, and other clinical services. Applying noscitur a sociis, the court held that "attendant care services" in that context meant professional, formal services - not informal care provided by family members without payment. The surrounding words limited the meaning of the phrase to things of the same kind.

Limits of the Maxim

Noscitur a sociis does not override clear text. If the surrounding words in a list are genuinely heterogeneous - if they cover a wide range of different things with nothing in common - the maxim provides little guidance. It is most useful where the words in a provision share a clear common theme that can be used to calibrate the meaning of an ambiguous term.

2. Ejusdem Generis - Of the Same Kind

Ejusdem generis - "of the same kind" - is the most commonly applied of the Latin maxims. It addresses a specific drafting pattern: a list of specific items followed by a general catch-all term such as "and other things," "and any other similar matter," or "or otherwise." The maxim provides that the general term is limited to things of the same type or genus as the specific items that precede it.

The rationale is that a drafter who wanted the catch-all to be genuinely unlimited would not have begun with a specific list. The list signals the kind of thing Parliament had in mind; the catch-all extends to other things of the same kind, not to anything that can be imagined.

The Two Requirements

For ejusdem generis to apply, two conditions must be met:

  • There must be at least TWO specific items before the catch-all - a single specific item is not enough to establish a genus. A genus is a class or category, and a single example cannot define a class.
  • The specific items must share a common characteristic - they must form a genuine genus. If the list is internally diverse and the items share no common feature, ejusdem generis cannot operate because there is no genus to which the catch-all can be limited.

Worked Example: The Transport Infrastructure Case

A statute referred to "railway, road, pipeline, or other facility." A storage depot sought to be classified as an "other facility" to access certain regulatory protections. The argument was rejected. Applying ejusdem generis, the court observed that railway, road, and pipeline all share the characteristic of being infrastructure for transportation or movement of goods and people - they involve the passage of things from one place to another. A storage depot, which holds things in one place, does not share that characteristic and therefore does not fall within "other facility" as limited by the ejusdem generis principle.

3. Expressio Unius Est Exclusio Alterius - Express Mention Excludes the Rest

Expressio unius est exclusio alterius means "the express mention of one thing implies the exclusion of others." Where a statute explicitly lists certain things, the inference is that Parliament deliberately omitted other things from the list - and that the omission is intentional. If Parliament had wanted those other things to be included, it would have said so.

Application

A statute gives a right of appeal to a court on "questions of law." The expressio unius principle supports the inference that there is no right of appeal on questions of fact - if Parliament had intended fact-based appeals, it would have included them. The express mention of "law" implies the exclusion of "fact." This was the reasoning applied in FAI Properties Pty Ltd v Lovelock (a leading Australian application of the maxim).

The Caution: Use Sparingly

Of the four maxims, expressio unius is the most risky to apply mechanically. Parliament does not always draft with perfect precision; the failure to mention something may reflect oversight, a desire to leave the question open, or an assumption that the inclusion was obvious - not a deliberate exclusion. Courts have frequently declined to apply expressio unius where its application would produce consequences that Parliament plainly did not intend. The High Court in Australian Mines & Metals Association v CFMMEU confirmed that this maxim, like all others, can be overridden by context and purpose.

4. Generalia Specialibus Non Derogant - General Does Not Override Specific

This maxim provides that where a general provision and a specific provision in the same statute are in apparent conflict, the specific provision prevails. The rationale is that Parliament is presumed to have addressed the specific situation with particular care; the general provision was not designed to override that particular treatment.

Application

A railway act provides (generally) that no animals are permitted on trains. A separate provision specifically permits registered guide dogs. Applying generalia specialibus, the specific permission for guide dogs prevails over the general prohibition on animals. The general rule is modified by the specific, not the other way around.

This maxim is often relevant in complex statutory schemes where a broad rule is subject to a network of specific exceptions and qualifications. The maxim directs courts to give effect to the specific provision rather than allowing a broad general provision to swallow it.

5. The Overriding Principle: Maxims Are Not Rigid Rules

The Latin maxims are presumptions about how language is typically used. They are not rules of law that must be applied whenever their conditions are technically satisfied. As the High Court confirmed in Australian Mines & Metals Association v CFMMEU, these maxims can be displaced by the context of the specific statute, by the evident purpose of the provision, or by clear indications that their application would produce an outcome contrary to the legislative intent.

The correct approach is to use the maxims as one element of the overall interpretive exercise - as tools that assist in identifying the most plausible reading of the statutory language - not as algorithms that determine the outcome regardless of other considerations.

In an examination or in practice, the appropriate use of a maxim involves:

  • Identifying the textual feature that triggers the maxim (e.g., a list followed by a catch-all for ejusdem generis).
  • Applying the maxim to identify the meaning it would support.
  • Checking that meaning against the context and purpose of the provision.
  • Concluding whether the maxim's result is consistent with the overall interpretive task, or whether context and purpose require a different answer.

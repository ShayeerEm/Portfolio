From Arrest to Verdict: A Procedural Guide to NSW Criminal Law for Legal Practitioners

SE
Shayeer Emanee
June 2026
5 min read
Criminal Law Case Analysis Australian Law
⚡

The NSW criminal justice system is governed by a complex but coherent procedural architecture that tracks a matter from the point of arrest through to final verdict and, if necessary, to appeal. For legal assistants and paralegals working in criminal law firms, an understanding of this architecture is fundamental. This guide provides a comprehensive walkthrough of NSW criminal procedure, from arrest to verdict, with particular attention to the stages that generate the most significant work at the firm level.

1. Arrest and Charge

The criminal process typically commences with an arrest and charge by police. Under ss 99 and 99A of the Law Enforcement (Powers and Responsibilities) Act 2002 (NSW) (LEPRA), a police officer may arrest a person without a warrant if the officer suspects on reasonable grounds that the person has committed an offence and arrest is reasonably necessary for one of the stated purposes - such as preventing the continuation of the offence, preserving evidence, or ensuring the person's attendance at court.

Following arrest, a person must be charged with an offence and brought before a court as soon as practicable. The charge establishes the formal accusation against the accused and determines the jurisdiction and procedure that will govern the matter. A charge may be for a summary offence (dealt with in the Local Court), an indictable offence dealt with summarily, or a strictly indictable offence (dealt with on indictment in the District or Supreme Court).

3. Bail

Following charge, the accused's liberty pending the hearing is governed by the Bail Act 2013 (NSW). The Act establishes a "show cause" threshold for certain serious offences - including murder, serious violence offences, and terrorism - where the accused bears an evidential burden of showing cause why detention is not justified. For most other offences, the prosecution bears the burden of establishing an unacceptable risk that the accused will fail to appear, endanger others, or interfere with witnesses or evidence.

Bail conditions may include reporting obligations, curfews, surety requirements, electronic monitoring, and geographical restrictions. A bail decision may be reviewed on application to the same court or appealed to a superior court. Legal representatives assisting with bail applications should gather evidence going to the accused's community ties, employment status, residence stability, prior record of compliance, and the strength of the prosecution case.

4. First Appearance in the Local Court

The accused's first appearance is typically a brief mention in the Local Court at which the charge is identified, the accused's representation is noted, and the matter is adjourned for the next step in the proceedings. At this stage, the prosecution should provide the accused with a copy of the facts sheet and, for indictable matters, ideally with the brief of evidence. The accused does not enter a plea at the first mention.

For matters proceeding by way of the Early Appropriate Guilty Plea (EAGP) process, the Local Court will make orders for service of the prosecution brief, with a view to facilitating an early plea. For complex matters, directions hearings may be convened to manage the timetable.

5. Committal Proceedings

For strictly indictable offences and for indictable offences not otherwise resolved in the Local Court, the matter proceeds through a committal process under Pt 2, Div 4 of the Criminal Procedure Act 1986 (NSW). The purpose of the committal is to determine whether the prosecution has sufficient evidence to support a prima facie case on each element of the charge before the matter is committed for trial in the District or Supreme Court.

Under the EAGP reforms, the committal hearing has become less common. Many matters are committed for trial without an oral committal hearing, on the basis of a paper committal - an assessment of the documentary evidence disclosed in the brief. However, accused persons retain the right to cross-examine prosecution witnesses at a committal hearing in certain circumstances, particularly where there is a genuine and substantial basis for challenging the reliability of a key witness.

6. The Trial on Indictment

Following committal, the accused is arraigned - formally called before the District or Supreme Court and required to enter a plea to the indictment. If a not guilty plea is entered, the matter proceeds to trial. A criminal trial on indictment is presided over by a judge, who determines questions of law and (where there is no jury) questions of fact. Where a jury is empanelled, the jury determines questions of fact.

The trial proceeds as follows: opening addresses; prosecution case (including examination-in-chief, cross-examination, and re-examination of prosecution witnesses); a submission of no case to answer at the close of the prosecution case (if made); defence case; closing addresses; directions from the judge to the jury; jury deliberation; and verdict.

7. Verdict and Sentencing

Where the accused is found not guilty, they are acquitted and discharged. Where the accused is found guilty - whether after trial or following a guilty plea - the matter proceeds to sentencing. Sentencing may be adjourned for the preparation of a pre-sentence report (from Corrective Services NSW), psychiatric or psychological reports, victim impact statements, and written submissions. The sentencing judge is required to sentence in accordance with the Crimes (Sentencing Procedure) Act 1999 (NSW) and the relevant guideline judgments.

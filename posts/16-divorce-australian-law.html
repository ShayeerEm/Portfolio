Divorce in Australian Law: The No-Fault Ground, Separation Under One Roof, and the 12-Month Limitation

SE
Shayeer Emanee
June 2026
5 min read
Family Law Case Analysis Australian Law
🏠

Australia moved to a no-fault divorce regime with the enactment of the Family Law Act 1975 (Cth), which abolished the former fault-based grounds and replaced them with a single ground: irretrievable breakdown of marriage, proved by 12 months of continuous separation. This reform fundamentally altered the relationship between divorce law and matrimonial misconduct: in principle, it is irrelevant whether one spouse "caused" the marriage to fail. What matters is the fact of breakdown, evidenced by separation. This post examines the divorce regime in Australian family law, the meaning of "separation," the procedures for obtaining a divorce order, and the time limits that apply to ancillary proceedings.

1. The Single Ground: Irretrievable Breakdown

Section 48 of the Family Law Act 1975 (Cth) provides that the sole ground for divorce is that the marriage has broken down irretrievably. Section 48(2) provides that the court must be satisfied of this only if and only if the parties have separated and thereafter lived separately and apart for a continuous period of not less than 12 months immediately before the date of filing the application. The connection between the single ground and the 12-month separation period is fundamental: Australia does not, unlike some other jurisdictions, permit divorce on the basis of adultery, cruelty, or other matrimonial fault.

2. Separation: Meaning and Proof

The concept of "separation" is not defined in the FLA, but has been extensively analysed by the courts. Separation involves both a physical component (living apart) and an intention component (at least one party treating the marriage as at an end). In In the Marriage of Todd (No 2) (1976) 25 FLR 260, the Full Court held that separation requires one spouse to apprise the other of the decision to end the matrimonial relationship - though the apprisement need not be explicit; it may be inferred from conduct.

Separation Under One Roof

One of the most significant features of Australian divorce law is the recognition of separation under one roof. Where parties continue to reside in the same dwelling but have otherwise ceased to function as a couple - maintaining separate bedrooms, conducting separate social and domestic lives, and treating the relationship as ended - they may be regarded as separated for the purposes of s 48 of the FLA. This recognition is practically important in circumstances where financial constraints, housing market conditions, or the need to maintain stability for children means that immediate departure from the family home is impracticable.

Where separation occurs under one roof, the court requires corroborating evidence - from neighbours, family members, health professionals, or financial records - to confirm that the parties were genuinely living separately and apart within the same dwelling. An affidavit from a corroborating witness is required by the Family Law Rules 2004 (Cth).

3. The 12-Month Period: Continuity and Reconciliation

The 12-month separation period must be continuous. Section 50 of the FLA addresses reconciliation: where the parties resume cohabitation with a genuine view to reconciliation during the 12-month period, the period of separation is interrupted and must restart. However, s 50(2) provides a "grace period": where the parties resume cohabitation for a period (or periods) not exceeding three months in total, for the purpose of reconciliation, the three months does not interrupt the 12-month separation period. This provision was designed to encourage reconciliation attempts without penalising parties who try and fail.

4. Application for Divorce: Procedure

An application for divorce is made in the Federal Circuit and Family Court of Australia (Division 2) (FCFCOA). Since 2022, all family law applications - including divorce applications - are filed in the FCFCOA, which was formed by the amalgamation of the Family Court of Australia and the Federal Circuit Court of Australia. Applications are filed online through the Commonwealth Courts Portal. A filing fee applies, with a reduced fee for concession card holders.

A divorce application may be made jointly (by both parties) or by one party only (sole application). Where a sole application is made, the applicant must serve the application on the other party in accordance with the Family Law Rules 2004 (Cth). A hearing is held, typically by videoconference, unless the application is uncontested and there are no children under 18 (in which case the divorce order is generally made in the absence of the parties). Where there are children under 18, the court must be satisfied that proper arrangements have been made for their care, welfare, and development before granting the divorce.

5. Time Limits on Property Settlement and Spousal Maintenance

A critical limitation period governs property settlement and spousal maintenance applications. Under s 44(3) of the FLA, an application for property settlement or maintenance by a spouse must be filed within 12 months of the date the divorce order takes effect. After that date, the parties may only bring an application with the leave of the court. Leave will only be granted where it is just and equitable having regard to the circumstances - a discretionary and difficult threshold to satisfy.

The divorce order takes effect one month and one day after the date on which it is made. Legal practitioners must therefore counsel clients, at the time of divorce, about the need to either finalise or commence property proceedings within 12 months of the effective date. The one-year anniversary is a hard deadline with potentially catastrophic consequences for a party who misses it. Many unrepresented litigants are unaware of this limitation and seek advice only after the deadline has passed.

6. De Facto Relationships: Different Time Limits

For de facto couples, there is no divorce as such - the relationship ends on separation. However, the time limit for property settlement under s 44(5) of the FLA is two years from the date of separation (not from a divorce order). This is a critical distinction: a de facto couple who separate have two years to file for property settlement; a married couple who separate may have considerably longer, because the 12-month limit runs only from the effective date of divorce (not from separation).

Parenting Orders under the Family Law Act: Best Interests, the 2024 Amendments, and Family Violence

SE
Shayeer Emanee
June 2026
5 min read
Family Law Case Analysis Australian Law
🏠

Parenting disputes are the most emotionally and legally complex matters that family law practitioners encounter. The Family Law Act 1975 (Cth) (FLA) provides a comprehensive but contested framework for the resolution of parenting disputes, built around the central concept of the "best interests of the child." This post examines the legislative framework for parenting orders under the FLA, with particular focus on the amended s 60CC best interests provisions, the presumption of equal shared parental responsibility, and the interaction with family violence findings.

1. The Framework: Best Interests as the Paramount Consideration

Section 60CA of the Family Law Act 1975 (Cth) provides that, in deciding whether to make a parenting order and what order to make, a court must regard the best interests of the child as the paramount consideration. "Paramount" means more than merely "primary" - it means that the child's interests override any other consideration, including the preferences and competing interests of the parents, where those interests conflict.

Significant amendments to the best interests framework were introduced by the Family Law Amendment Act 2023 (Cth), which took effect from 6 May 2024. These amendments removed the former two-tier structure of "primary" and "additional" considerations and replaced it with a unified list of factors in the new s 60CC. The reforms also repealed the presumption of equal shared parental responsibility in the former s 61DA, which had been one of the most contested provisions in the history of the FLA.

2. The New Section 60CC: Best Interests Factors

Under the amended s 60CC(2) of the Family Law Act 1975 (Cth), a court determining what is in the child's best interests must consider:

  • The safety of the child and each person who has care of the child (including each parent).
  • The benefit to the child of having a relationship with both parents and other significant people (such as grandparents).
  • The views expressed by the child and any factors that the court considers relevant to the weight to be given to those views (including age and maturity).
  • The developmental, psychological, emotional, and cultural needs of the child.
  • The capacity of each person who has, or is proposed to have, parental responsibility to provide for the child's needs, including emotional and intellectual needs.
  • Any family violence involving the child or a member of the child's family and any Family Violence Order that applies to the child or family member.
  • Anything else that is relevant to the child's particular circumstances.

3. The Repeal of the Equal Shared Parental Responsibility Presumption

Prior to the 2024 amendments, s 61DA of the FLA created a rebuttable presumption that it was in the best interests of the child for parents to have equal shared parental responsibility. This presumption was displaced where there were reasonable grounds to believe that a parent had engaged in family violence or child abuse. Where equal shared parental responsibility was ordered, the court was required to consider whether equal time or substantial and significant time was reasonably practicable and in the child's best interests.

The 2024 amendments repealed s 61DA entirely, along with the associated provisions in ss 65DAA and 65DAC. The repeal was controversial. Proponents argued that the presumption had been misapplied in practice - leading to equal time arrangements even in cases involving family violence - and that its removal would better protect children and victims of violence. Critics argued that the removal of the presumption signals a retreat from a commitment to shared parenting and may lead to less involvement by non-primary-carer parents.

4. Parental Responsibility: The Concept After the Amendments

"Parental responsibility" is defined in s 61B of the FLA as all the duties, powers, responsibilities, and authority which, by law, parents have in relation to children. This includes the making of long-term decisions about education, medical treatment, religion, and where the child lives. The 2024 amendments did not alter the concept of parental responsibility itself; they simply removed the presumption that it should be shared equally between parents. Courts now determine parental responsibility as part of the overall best interests assessment under s 60CC, without any starting presumption.

5. Family Violence and Parenting Orders

Section 60CC(2)(a) places the safety of the child at the head of the best interests factors. Family violence involving the child or a member of the child's family is expressly required to be considered. "Family violence" is defined in s 4AB of the FLA to include a wide range of coercive, controlling, and threatening behaviours - not merely physical violence. Courts have been directed by the Full Court in Mast v Mast [2019] FamCAFC 62 to give proper weight to the risks that family violence poses for children, and to exercise caution before ordering time arrangements that expose children to an abusive parent.

A finding of family violence does not automatically preclude a parenting order that includes time with the perpetrating parent. However, where the court concludes that there is a risk of family violence, it must consider what protective mechanisms - if any - are appropriate and adequate. These may include supervised contact, graduated contact, requirements for the perpetrating parent to complete a behaviour change program, or, in serious cases, no contact orders.

6. Children's Views in Parenting Proceedings

The views of the child are a relevant factor under the new s 60CC(2)(c). However, the weight given to a child's expressed views depends on the child's age, maturity, understanding, and the circumstances in which the views were expressed. A child who has been coached or influenced by one parent to express a preference for that parent's position will be given less weight than a child who demonstrates genuine, independent reasoning. In complex matters, a family consultant or child psychologist may be appointed to assess the child's views and report to the court.

7. The Independent Children's Lawyer (ICL)

Where parenting proceedings are complex, contested, or involve allegations of abuse or family violence, the court may appoint an Independent Children's Lawyer (ICL) under s 68L of the FLA to independently represent the interests of the child. The ICL's role was clarified by the Full Court in Re K (1994) 17 Fam LR 537 and subsequently codified. The ICL is not the child's "mouthpiece" - they are required to form an independent view of the child's best interests and present that view to the court, even if it differs from the child's expressed wishes.

Costs in NSW Civil Litigation: Ordinary Costs, Indemnity Costs, and the Calderbank Offer

SE
Shayeer Emanee
June 2026
5 min read
Dispute Resolution Case Analysis Australian Law
⚖

Costs are the financial lifeblood of civil litigation. For many litigants, the prospect of an adverse costs order - being required to pay the other side's legal costs - is as significant a risk as the substantive outcome of the proceedings. Understanding the principles that govern costs awards in NSW, the mechanics of costs assessment, and the strategic use of settlement offers to shift costs liability is therefore essential knowledge for any practitioner in a dispute resolution practice.

1. The General Rule: Costs Follow the Event

The starting point in NSW civil litigation is that costs follow the event: the unsuccessful party pays the successful party's costs. This principle, sometimes described as the "loser pays" rule, is embodied in s 98(1) of the Civil Procedure Act 2005 (NSW) and r 42.1 of the UCPR. However, the rule is a starting point, not an absolute requirement. The court retains a broad discretion in awarding costs, and the overriding principle is that costs should reflect the justice of the particular case.

Departures from the general rule are common. A successful plaintiff who made an unreasonable offer may be denied some or all of their costs. A defendant who succeeded on most but not all issues may receive only a proportion of their costs. A party who engaged in misconduct - such as deceptive discovery, unmeritorious interlocutory applications, or deliberate delay - may be ordered to pay costs on an indemnity basis regardless of the outcome on the merits.

2. Scales of Costs: Ordinary versus Indemnity

Costs Basis Description Typical Trigger
Ordinary (party-party) Reasonable costs incurred in prosecuting or defending the action; typically 60-70% of actual costs Default where costs follow the event
Indemnity Costs on a solicitor-client basis; all costs reasonably incurred; typically 90-100% of actual costs Misconduct; rejection of Calderbank offer that was not beaten at trial; fraud; abuse of process
Costs of the day Wasted costs from an adjournment or failed interlocutory application One party causes unnecessary hearing; application fails

3. Calderbank Offers

A Calderbank offer - named after Calderbank v Calderbank [1976] Fam 93 (England) - is a settlement offer made "without prejudice save as to costs." The offer is confidential and cannot be adduced in evidence on the substantive issues at trial; but it can be tendered at the conclusion of the trial to inform the costs order. The purpose of the Calderbank mechanism is to create a costs incentive for parties to accept reasonable settlement offers: a party who rejects a Calderbank offer and then fails to do better at trial faces the risk of a costs order on the indemnity basis from the date of the offer.

For a Calderbank offer to have the desired costs consequence, it must be a genuine attempt to settle: it must be clearly communicated, open for a reasonable time, capable of acceptance without conditions, and pitched at a level that the rejecting party should have accepted having regard to the risks and costs of litigation. An offer that is illusory - such as an offer for a nominal sum on a claim worth millions - will not attract indemnity costs consequences even if the rejecting party fails to do better at trial: Leichhardt Municipal Council v Green (2004) 49 NSWLR 2.

4. Formal Offers of Compromise under UCPR r 20.26

In addition to Calderbank offers, NSW law provides a formal offer of compromise mechanism under r 20.26 of the UCPR. A formal offer of compromise that complies with the rule automatically triggers costs consequences under r 42.14 (for offers by plaintiffs) and r 42.15 (for offers by defendants), without requiring the court to exercise a further discretion. The rule provides a structured mechanism for creating costs incentives, with less reliance on the court's general discretion than the Calderbank regime.

A plaintiff's offer of compromise that is not accepted, and the plaintiff obtains a judgment not less favourable than the terms of the offer, ordinarily entitles the plaintiff to indemnity costs from the date the offer was made. A defendant's offer of compromise that is not accepted, and the plaintiff obtains a judgment not more favourable than the offer, entitles the defendant to indemnity costs from the date of the offer. The automatic costs consequence under the rule - as distinct from the discretionary Calderbank regime - makes the formal offer of compromise a powerful litigation tool.

5. The Costs Assessment Process

Where a costs order is made but the parties cannot agree on the quantum of costs, the costs must be assessed. In NSW, costs assessment is governed by Pt 3.2 of the Legal Profession Uniform Law (NSW) (LPUL) and is conducted by a costs assessor appointed by the Costs Assessment Manager in the Supreme Court. The costs assessor determines whether the costs claimed are fair and reasonable, having regard to the complexity of the matter, the time reasonably spent, the experience and seniority of the practitioners, and the outcome of the proceedings.

The costs assessment process can itself be time-consuming and expensive. For this reason, parties are often better served by negotiating an agreed costs figure rather than proceeding to formal assessment. Where assessment is necessary, legal representatives should prepare a detailed costs schedule and supporting documentation at the earliest opportunity.

6. Costs Against Practitioners Personally: Wasted Costs Orders

In exceptional circumstances, a court may make a costs order against a legal practitioner personally, rather than against the party they represent. A wasted costs order under s 99 of the Civil Procedure Act 2005 (NSW) may be made where the practitioner's conduct has caused costs to be incurred unnecessarily - for example, through failure to comply with directions, filing defective or hopeless interlocutory applications, or engaging in misleading conduct. The threshold for a wasted costs order is high, and courts are cautious about making such orders given the chilling effect on robust advocacy.

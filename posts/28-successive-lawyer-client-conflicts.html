Successive Client Conflicts and Lawyer-Client Conflicts: Rules 10 and 12 ASCR

SE
Shayeer Emanee
June 2026
7 min read
Professional Responsibility Case Analysis Australian Law
📋

Conflicts of interest are not limited to situations where a solicitor simultaneously represents two adverse clients. Two further categories generate significant disciplinary and negligence exposure: successive client conflicts (where a solicitor is asked to act against a former client) and lawyer-client conflicts (where the solicitor's own interests are engaged). Both categories share the same underlying principle - the solicitor's duty of undivided loyalty to each client cannot coexist with a competing obligation or personal interest.

Part I: Successive Client Conflicts

1.1 The Rule: ASCR Rule 10

A successive conflict arises when a solicitor who has acted for Client A is subsequently asked to act for Client B against Client A. The critical question is whether the solicitor holds confidential information from the former retainer that would be material and detrimental to Client A in the new matter.

ASCR Rule 10 provides that a solicitor must not act for a current client in a matter adverse to a former client if:

  • The solicitor possesses confidential information of the former client;
  • That information is material to the current matter; AND
  • Using or disclosing that information would be detrimental to the former client.

All three conditions must be met. If the information held from the former retainer is irrelevant to the current matter - for example, the solicitor previously advised the former client on a commercial lease but is now asked to act against them in an entirely unrelated personal injury claim - Rule 10 may not prevent the representation. However, the categories of "material" and "detrimental" are read broadly by disciplinary tribunals, and a cautious solicitor will conduct a careful analysis of every piece of information held before concluding the conflict does not bite.

1.2 How Long Does the Rule Apply?

There is no time limit on the former client conflict rule. Rule 10 does not expire after three years or five years. It applies permanently to any information obtained during the former retainer, for as long as that information remains confidential and material. A solicitor who represented a client on a commercial matter ten years ago and learned sensitive financial information during that retainer cannot act against that former client in a matter where that financial information is relevant, even a decade later.

1.3 Peter and Nick: A Worked Example

Consider the following scenario, drawn from the kinds of problems regularly set in professional responsibility examinations:

Peter is a sole practitioner who represented Nick on a fraud charge nine years ago. The matter concluded, Nick was convicted, and the retainer ended. Peter now has a new client, Kim - who is Nick's wife. Kim is seeking a property settlement and parenting orders in the context of a breakdown of her marriage to Nick.

The analysis under Rule 10:

  • Confidential information: During the fraud retainer, Peter would have received detailed information about Nick's financial position, his business dealings, his character, his propensity for dishonesty, and possibly information about assets not disclosed to Kim.
  • Material to the current matter: In Family Court proceedings, Nick's financial position, the nature and extent of his assets (including any concealed assets), and his character (relevant to parenting orders under s 60CC of the Family Law Act) are directly in issue.
  • Detrimental to Nick: Using Nick's confidential fraud-related financial information in adversarial proceedings against him would plainly be detrimental.

Conclusion: Peter has an irreconcilable successive conflict. He cannot represent Kim. Because Peter is a sole practitioner, no information barrier is available. Nick has not consented (and almost certainly would not). Peter must decline the retainer from Kim and advise her to seek representation from another firm.

It is also worth noting: if Peter were to accept Kim's retainer while sitting on Nick's confidential information, he would face an impossible dilemma. He could not give Kim full advice (because doing so properly requires using Nick's information), and he cannot use that information (because it belongs to Nick). Both clients would receive worse advice than they are entitled to.

1.4 Exceptions: Consent and Information Barriers

Under Rule 10, a successive conflict can be resolved if the former client gives informed written consent to the solicitor's representation of the adverse party. In practice, this is almost never forthcoming where the information is genuinely sensitive: a former client who learned their former solicitor was now advising their opponent in litigation would rarely consent.

An information barrier can also, in principle, permit the representation - but only in a firm large enough to plausibly segregate the conflicted solicitor from those acting on the current matter. In Peter's case as a sole practitioner, this is structurally impossible.

Part II: Lawyer-Client Conflicts - The Triple M Framework

Lawyer-client conflicts arise where the solicitor's own interests are adverse to, or may compromise, the client's interests. These conflicts are governed by ASCR Rule 12 and are analysed using what can be called the "Triple M" framework: conflicts arising from Money, from being a Material Witness, and from Morals (personal or romantic relationships).

2.1 M1: Money

The financial relationship between solicitor and client must remain at arm's length. Two specific categories of money-related conflict arise frequently:

First, undisclosed profit. A solicitor who makes a financial gain from the retainer beyond the disclosed fees is in breach of their fiduciary duty. This includes receiving referral fees, commissions, or benefits from third parties (such as real estate agents, financial planners, or insurance brokers) in connection with the client's matter, without the client's informed consent. The fiduciary principle requires that any benefit received in the course of acting for a client must be disclosed to and authorised by the client.

Second, lending and borrowing. A solicitor who borrows money from a client, or lends money to a client, creates a relationship in which their interests as a debtor or creditor are directly adverse to their interests as the client's fiduciary. ASCR Rule 12 expressly prohibits entering into financial arrangements with clients - including lending, borrowing, or providing guarantees - without informed written consent and, typically, independent legal advice for the client. The power imbalance inherent in the lawyer-client relationship makes such transactions inherently suspect.

2.2 M2: Material Witness

A solicitor cannot simultaneously be an advocate for their client in proceedings and a witness in those same proceedings. These roles are structurally incompatible: an advocate shapes and presents evidence to serve their client's case; a witness is supposed to give objective, disinterested evidence of facts within their knowledge.

The conflict most commonly crystallises when a solicitor has drafted a document that is later disputed in litigation. The client needs the solicitor to conduct the litigation; but the solicitor may also need to give evidence about the circumstances in which the document was drafted. If they do both, their evidence as a witness is coloured by their advocacy role; their advocacy is compromised by their obligation to give objective evidence.

When a solicitor recognises that they are likely to be called as a witness on a material issue, they must withdraw from the advocacy role. They may assist the client in briefing another solicitor, but they cannot continue to act as advocate once their witness role is engaged.

2.3 M3: Morals - Personal and Romantic Relationships

A personal or romantic relationship between a solicitor and their client is one of the most acute forms of lawyer-client conflict. Australian courts and disciplinary tribunals have consistently held that such relationships are incompatible with the professional independence that a solicitor owes their client.

The test is not whether actual bias can be demonstrated - it is whether "a fair-minded and reasonably informed member of the public" would conclude that the administration of justice is compromised by the relationship. Courts in family law proceedings have observed that personal relationships involve emotions of "love, attraction, sympathy, empathy and trust" that are "likely to colour and temper and detract from, if not skew" the objectivity and independence that professional legal service requires.

The practical problem is clear: a solicitor who is romantically involved with a client cannot give hard, unwelcome advice. The professional obligation to advise a client that their case is weak, that they should accept a settlement offer, or that their conduct may expose them to adverse costs orders - these conversations require independence that a personal relationship compromises. Even without any deliberate bias, the solicitor in a personal relationship with a client cannot credibly claim independence.

Where a personal relationship exists or develops, the solicitor must withdraw from the retainer and refer the client to independent representation. The withdrawal should occur before the relationship becomes a source of conflict, not after the client has been disadvantaged.

3. Conclusion

Successive and lawyer-client conflicts both proceed from the same principle: a solicitor's duty of loyalty must be undivided. The Rule 10 successive conflict analysis requires a careful and honest assessment of what information the solicitor holds, whether it is material to the adverse matter, and whether using it - even inadvertently - would harm the former client. The Triple M framework for lawyer-client conflicts reminds practitioners that their financial interests, potential witness status, and personal relationships can each, independently, make objective professional service impossible. In all these cases, the appropriate response is the same: identify the conflict early, advise the affected clients, and withdraw before damage is done.

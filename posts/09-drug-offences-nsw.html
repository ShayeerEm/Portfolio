Drug Offences in NSW: Possession, Supply, and the Deemed Supply Presumption under the DMTA

SE
Shayeer Emanee
June 2026
5 min read
Criminal Law Case Analysis Australian Law
⚡

Drug offences are among the most commonly prosecuted criminal matters in NSW, and they arise at every level of the court system - from simple possession matters dealt with in the Local Court, to large-scale commercial drug supply prosecuted in the District Court on indictment. This analysis examines the structure of NSW drug offences under the Drug Misuse and Trafficking Act 1985 (NSW) (DMTA), the critical "deemed supply" presumption, sentencing principles for drug supply, and the interaction with Commonwealth drug legislation.

1. Structure of the DMTA: A Tiered Offence Scheme

The Drug Misuse and Trafficking Act 1985 (NSW) creates a tiered scheme of drug offences calibrated primarily to the quantity of prohibited drug involved. The principal offences are possession (s 10), use (s 12), and supply (s 25), with trafficking provisions applying at the commercial scale (s 25A). The penalties associated with each offence increase dramatically as the quantity of drug increases through defined thresholds.

Quantity Category Threshold (MDMA by way of example) Maximum Penalty (Supply)
Small quantity Less than 0.25g 2 years / 20 penalty units (Local Court)
Traffickable quantity 0.25g or more 15 years / 2000 penalty units (District Court)
Indictable quantity 1.25g or more 15 years (District Court on indictment)
Commercial quantity 125g or more 20 years / 3500 penalty units
Large commercial quantity 1.25kg or more Life imprisonment / 5000 penalty units

The quantity thresholds differ substantially by drug type. The thresholds for cannabis are considerably higher than those for methamphetamine, MDMA, heroin, or cocaine, reflecting the legislature's calibration of the scheme to the relative harmfulness and commercial density of each substance. Schedule 1 to the DMTA sets out the complete table of prohibited drugs and their associated quantities.

2. Supply: Definition and Scope

"Supply" is defined expansively in s 3 of the DMTA to include: selling, distributing, dispensing, delivering, administering, giving, providing, offering to supply, keeping or having in possession for supply, agreeing or offering to supply, or making an arrangement for supply. The breadth of this definition means that the offence of supply captures conduct well beyond the paradigm of a street-level drug dealer. A person who hands drugs to a friend without charge commits supply; a person who agrees to source drugs for another - even if no money changes hands - commits supply.

3. The Deemed Supply Presumption

Section 29 of the DMTA creates the notorious "deemed supply" presumption. Where a person has a prohibited drug in their possession in a quantity equal to or greater than the "traffickable quantity," the law presumes that the drug is possessed for the purpose of supply unless the contrary is proved. The presumption reverses the ordinary burden of proof: the prosecution need only establish possession of the threshold quantity, and the accused bears the evidential and persuasive burden of proving that the drug was for personal use.

The standard of proof on the accused to rebut the presumption is proof on the balance of probabilities: s 29(2). Evidence that may support rebuttal includes: evidence of the accused's personal drug use history; evidence of a specific personal use occasion for which the drugs were acquired; absence of other supply paraphernalia (scales, bags, dealing records); and a relatively small quantity above the threshold. Conversely, the presence of deal bags, multiple denominations of cash, scales, and customer lists significantly reduces the prospects of successfully rebutting the presumption.

4. The Guideline Judgment for Drug Supply: R v Whyte

The NSW Court of Criminal Appeal issued a guideline judgment for the sentencing of drug supply offences in R v Whyte (2002) 55 NSWLR 252. The guideline addressed supply of prohibited drugs in or above the indictable quantity range and established the following key principles:

  • The seriousness of the offence increases markedly with the quantity of drug involved.
  • Commercial drug supply demands strong emphasis on general deterrence; the personal circumstances of the offender are given correspondingly less weight.
  • A sentence of full-time imprisonment is the appropriate starting point for supply of a commercial quantity.
  • A guilty plea discount remains available and is calculated in the ordinary way.
  • The role of the offender in the supply chain - whether principal, intermediate, or courier - affects the objective seriousness of the offence.

5. Sentencing Considerations in Drug Supply

Within the Whyte framework, courts apply a range of specific considerations. Relevant to objective gravity: the quantity and type of drug; the role of the offender in the supply chain (organiser, distributor, street dealer, or courier); the presence or absence of commerciality; the existence of commercial arrangements (fronting, discounting, consignment); and the number of transactions. Relevant to subjective factors: mental health (Verdins principles); addiction (which may reduce culpability but does not generally provide a strong argument against deterrence in commercial supply); prior criminal history; and the prospects of rehabilitation.

Where the accused is a low-level courier - particularly where they were coerced, exploited, or operating under a misapprehension as to the quantity they were carrying - courts have shown greater flexibility than the Whyte framework might initially suggest. The doctrine of "less than general culpability" allows a sentencing court to acknowledge that not all persons who technically supply drugs at the commercial level bear the same moral responsibility for the trade.

6. The Commonwealth CDSA: When Federal Law Applies

Where drug offences involve importation or exportation, or where the conduct has a cross-border character, the relevant legislation may be the Criminal Code Act 1995 (Cth) and the Customs Act 1901 (Cth) rather than the NSW DMTA. Importation offences are prosecuted by the Australian Federal Police and the Australian Border Force; they attract higher maximum penalties (including 25 years for commercial importation) and are heard in the Federal Court or the District Court exercising federal jurisdiction. The sentencing framework differs from NSW law and places even greater weight on general deterrence given the international character of the trade.

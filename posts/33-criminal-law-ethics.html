Criminal Law Ethics: Prosecutors as Ministers of Justice and the Defence of the Guilty Client

SE
Shayeer Emanee
June 2026
8 min read
Professional Responsibility Case Analysis Australian Law
📋

Criminal law is the area of legal practice that puts the ethical framework of the adversarial system under the greatest pressure. The stakes - liberty, reputation, often decades of a person's life - are among the highest the legal system administers. And the ethical framework in criminal practice is distinctive in ways that go beyond the general rules: prosecutors bear duties that have no analogue in civil practice, and defence lawyers confront dilemmas that arise nowhere else in the profession.

Part I: Prosecutors as Ministers of Justice

1.1 The Prosecutor's Role

The fundamental principle governing prosecutorial ethics in Australia is that a prosecutor is not a partisan advocate. The prosecutor represents the state - and, through the state, the community - in proceedings that may result in the loss of a person's liberty. The objective is not to win a conviction; it is to assist the court in determining the truth and ensuring a fair trial.

In Whitehorn v The Queen, the High Court articulated this principle clearly: the prosecutor must act "with fairness and detachment" and with the objective of "establishing the whole truth" and ensuring a fair trial for the accused. The prosecutor's success is not measured by conviction rates. It is measured by the fairness of the process.

This principle generates specific and demanding obligations that are codified in ASCR Rule 29.

1.2 ASCR Rule 29: The Specific Duties

Rule Obligation Practical Consequence
Rule 29.1 Must fairly assist the court to arrive at the truth and impartially place all relevant evidence before the court The prosecutor cannot cherry-pick evidence - all relevant material must be placed before the court, not just what helps the prosecution
Rule 29.2 Must not press the case beyond a full and firm presentation; must not use inflammatory or emotive language The prosecutor must not attempt to inflame the jury's emotions or overstate the evidence; submissions must be calibrated to what the evidence actually supports
Rule 29.5 Must disclose to the defence all material evidence relevant to guilt or innocence - including evidence that weakens the prosecution's case This disclosure obligation extends to exculpatory evidence. The prosecutor who withholds evidence that might assist the accused commits a serious ethical breach
Rule 29.6 If the prosecutor decides not to disclose evidence, must consider withdrawing the charge or reducing it The prosecutor cannot both keep evidence from the defence and continue pursuing the charge - one or the other must give way
Rule 29.7 Must call all necessary witnesses to present the whole truth - even witnesses whose evidence is unfavourable to the prosecution The prosecutor is not permitted to build a selective case that omits witnesses whose evidence might assist the defence

The disclosure obligation under Rule 29.5 is the most frequently litigated. The prosecution's duty to disclose exculpatory evidence - evidence that tends to show the accused may be innocent, or that undermines the prosecution's case - is a cornerstone of the right to a fair trial. Withholding such evidence, whether deliberately or through inadequate systems, constitutes a fundamental breach of prosecutorial duty and may result in a conviction being quashed on appeal.

1.3 The Unique Position of the Crown

These demanding obligations reflect the unique position of the Crown as a litigant. Unlike a private party, the prosecution has access to the police, the state's investigative resources, and the coercive powers of the state. It is not a party that needs to be protected from the superior power of the other side - it is the superior power. The heightened ethical obligations placed on prosecutors are the profession's response to this structural imbalance.

This also explains why prosecutors face obligations that civil lawyers do not: the civil lawyer acts for a private party, advancing private interests, in a context where each side is expected to look after itself. The criminal prosecutor acts for the community, in proceedings where the accused may be unrepresented, poorly resourced, and facing the full weight of state power. The ethical framework must be calibrated accordingly.

Part II: Defence Lawyers and the Guilty Client

2.1 The Role of the Defence

The defence lawyer's role is often misunderstood - both by the public and by law students encountering it for the first time. The defence lawyer's job is not to "get criminals off." It is to ensure that the prosecution meets its burden of proof beyond reasonable doubt, that the accused's rights are protected throughout the process, and that the court decides on evidence properly admitted and a process properly followed.

The adversarial system depends on the defence lawyer performing this role rigorously. If the prosecution can only convict when it has proven its case beyond reasonable doubt, in proceedings that are procedurally fair, then the whole system produces reliable verdicts. A defence that simply collapses whenever the client is guilty, or whenever the prosecution presents strong evidence, would undermine the entire framework.

2.2 Representing a Guilty Client Who Pleads Not Guilty: ASCR Rule 20.2

The most difficult ethical situation for a defence lawyer arises when the client confesses guilt to the lawyer but insists on pleading not guilty. This is not uncommon. Clients may confess privately to their lawyer while maintaining that the prosecution must prove its case - which is, technically, their legal right.

ASCR Rule 20.2 addresses this directly. It provides that where a client has instructed the solicitor that they are guilty but wishes to plead not guilty, the solicitor may continue to act, but is subject to specific constraints:

  • The solicitor CANNOT suggest to the court, in submissions, that another person committed the crime - this would be a false assertion inconsistent with the client's own confession.
  • The solicitor CANNOT call any witness, including the accused, to give evidence denying guilt - allowing the client to testify falsely and deny a crime they have admitted would be suborning perjury.
  • The solicitor CANNOT set up an affirmative defence (such as an alibi) that is inconsistent with the confession - presenting a defence the solicitor knows to be false is a direct breach of the duty to the court.
  • The solicitor CAN put the prosecution to proof - challenge the admissibility of evidence, test prosecution witnesses in cross-examination, and argue that the standard of proof has not been met.
  • The solicitor CAN make submissions that the evidence is legally insufficient - if the prosecution has not adduced evidence sufficient to establish each element of the offence, the solicitor may argue for a directed verdict of acquittal.

This framework allows the defence lawyer to fulfil their constitutional function - holding the prosecution to its burden of proof - without becoming complicit in a false case. The solicitor does not advance guilt or innocence; they advance the claim that the prosecution has not proven what it must prove. That is a legitimate and important role, even where the client has privately confessed.

2.3 The Innocent Client Who Wants to Plead Guilty: Meissner

The reverse situation also arises: a client who the solicitor believes to be innocent, but who wants to plead guilty. This may occur for reasons entirely unconnected to legal guilt - to protect a family member, to avoid the stress and publicity of a trial, to get the certainty of a known sentence, or because the client has simply lost hope.

In Meissner v The Queen, the High Court confirmed that a person is legally entitled to plead guilty even if they are innocent. The court does not look behind a guilty plea to verify the accused's actual guilt. The role of the defence lawyer in this situation is to advise clearly and firmly against the plea, to ensure the client understands that they are admitting to something they did not do and the consequences that follow, and to ensure the decision is the client's own free and informed choice. The solicitor cannot be the client's keeper - if the client persists in the guilty plea after being properly advised, the solicitor can continue to represent them, but cannot set up a false case of innocence.

2.4 Lawyer X: When the System Is Corrupted

The most extreme example of ethical failure in criminal defence practice in Australian history is Lawyer X - the pseudonym given to Nicola Gobbo, a prominent Victorian criminal defence barrister who covertly informed on her own clients to Victoria Police over many years.

In AB (a pseudonym) v CD; EF v CD [2018] HCA 58, the High Court described Gobbo's conduct in terms that leave no ambiguity about its gravity:

EF's actions in purporting to act as counsel to the Convicted Persons while covertly informing against them were fundamental and appalling breaches of EF's obligations as counsel to her clients and of EF's duties to the court. As a result, the prosecution of each convicted person was corrupted in a manner which debased fundamental premises of the criminal justice system.

Gobbo was simultaneously the advocate for accused persons and a police informant providing intelligence that assisted in prosecuting those same persons. This dual role - not merely a conflict of interest but an active betrayal - meant that every conviction in which she was involved as defence counsel was potentially tainted by the fundamental unfairness of the process. The High Court held that this situation was so destructive of the adversarial system that the prosecutions had been corrupted in their essence.

The Lawyer X case illustrates why the defence lawyer's loyalty to the client is not merely a professional nicety. It is the structural guarantee that the adversarial system works. When that loyalty is destroyed - when the defender becomes the informant - the entire premise of the criminal trial collapses.

3. Conclusion

Criminal law ethics confronts solicitors with the sharpest versions of the dilemmas that professional responsibility presents throughout practice. Prosecutors face duties of disclosure and fairness that would be incomprehensible in civil litigation, because the community's interest in a fair and reliable criminal justice system demands more than adversarial competence. Defence lawyers face the challenge of providing effective representation to clients they know to be guilty, within constraints that prevent them from actively advancing falsehood. Understanding both sets of obligations - and where the limits lie - is foundational for anyone seeking to practise in the criminal jurisdiction.

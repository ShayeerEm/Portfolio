Strata Title in NSW: Owners Corporations, By-Laws, Levies, and Dispute Resolution under the SSMA

SE
Shayeer Emanee
June 2026
5 min read
Property Law Case Analysis Australian Law
🏛

Strata title is the dominant form of property ownership for apartments, townhouses, and commercial suites in NSW. It is estimated that more than one in six Australians now lives in a strata-titled property, and the number continues to grow with urban densification. For property law practitioners - and for the clients of general practice firms in Western Sydney, where apartment development has accelerated significantly - a working knowledge of strata title law is not optional. This post provides an overview of the strata title regime in NSW, with focus on the Strata Schemes Management Act 2015 (NSW) (SSMA), the rights and obligations of lot owners, and the dispute resolution mechanisms available under the scheme.

1. What is Strata Title?

A strata scheme is a form of subdivision in which a parcel of land and the buildings on it are divided into "lots" (individual apartments or units) and "common property." Common property includes all parts of the scheme not comprised within any lot - typically corridors, lobbies, lifts, stairwells, gardens, carparking areas for common use, and building facades. Each lot owner owns their lot in fee simple (indefeasible Torrens title) and holds a "lot entitlement" - an interest in the common property as a tenant in common with all other lot owners.

2. The Owners Corporation

Every strata scheme has an owners corporation (formerly called a body corporate), which is created automatically by registration of the strata plan and consists of all lot owners. The owners corporation is a statutory corporation: it can sue and be sued, it holds the common property, and it is responsible for the management and maintenance of the scheme. The owners corporation acts through general meetings (the annual general meeting and special general meetings) and through its strata committee (formerly the executive committee), which is a smaller elected body that manages day-to-day affairs between meetings.

3. Lot Entitlements and Levies

Each lot has a "unit entitlement" which determines the proportion of the scheme's costs that the lot owner must contribute and the proportion of votes attributable to the lot at certain types of meeting. Levies are the mechanism by which the owners corporation funds its operations. There are two types of mandatory levy:

  • Administrative fund levies: for routine maintenance, insurance, and the day-to-day costs of managing the scheme.
  • Capital works fund levies: for major capital expenditure on the common property, such as lift replacement, facade repairs, or fire system upgrades. A 10-year capital works fund plan must be prepared and reviewed regularly.

Failure to pay levies on time makes the lot owner liable for interest under s 83 of the SSMA at the rate prescribed by the regulations (currently 10% per annum). Where a lot owner persistently defaults on levy payments, the owners corporation may seek recovery through the NSW Civil and Administrative Tribunal (NCAT) or through the Local Court.

4. By-Laws

By-laws are the rules that govern the behaviour of lot owners, occupants, and visitors within a strata scheme. Section 136 of the SSMA requires every strata scheme to have by-laws. The default by-laws are prescribed in Sch 3 to the SSMA (for residential schemes). Schemes may add, amend, or repeal by-laws by special resolution (a 75% majority in favour of the motion, with no more than 25% opposed) at a duly convened meeting.

By-laws are a significant source of property disputes. Common by-law disputes concern: keeping of pets; parking in common property; noise; alterations and renovations to lots; short-term rental (Airbnb); and by-laws that discriminate against children, the elderly, or people with disabilities. Under s 139 of the SSMA, a by-law has no force to the extent that it is unjust, inequitable, unreasonably oppressive, or inconsistent with legislation. A by-law that purports to discriminate against children, for example, is void for inconsistency with the Anti-Discrimination Act 1977 (NSW).

5. Maintenance and Common Property Disputes

The owners corporation is obliged under s 106 of the SSMA to properly maintain and keep in a state of good and serviceable repair the common property and any personal property vested in the owners corporation. This is an absolute obligation - the owners corporation cannot escape it by pleading insufficient funds or the fault of a contractor. Where the owners corporation breaches s 106, a lot owner may apply to NCAT for an order compelling maintenance or for compensation for damage suffered as a result of the failure to maintain.

The boundary between "lot" and "common property" is a common source of dispute, particularly where water damage or structural defects arise. As a general rule, the lot owner owns everything within the boundaries of their lot as defined on the strata plan, including internal walls, floor coverings, ceilings, and internal fittings. Common property includes the external envelope of the building, structural elements, and building services (such as pipes and wiring) that serve more than one lot. Where a pipe within a wall services multiple lots, it is usually common property and the owners corporation is responsible for its maintenance.

6. Dispute Resolution: NCAT and the Strata Commissioner

Disputes within strata schemes are primarily resolved through the NSW Civil and Administrative Tribunal (NCAT), which has jurisdiction under Chapter 5 of the SSMA to hear and determine applications relating to strata schemes. NCAT can make orders compelling compliance with by-laws, ordering payment of compensation, requiring the owners corporation to carry out maintenance, and resolving disputes about the conduct of meetings. NCAT also has jurisdiction to deal with defects disputes under the Design and Building Practitioners Act 2020 (NSW), a significant addition given the prevalence of building defect claims in new apartment developments.

For disputes that are urgent or that involve financial difficulty (such as levy recovery), the Local Court has concurrent jurisdiction. For major disputes - including strata termination, development applications affecting common property, and claims above NCAT's monetary limit - proceedings must be brought in the Land and Environment Court or the Supreme Court of NSW.

7. Building Defects and the Design and Building Practitioners Act 2020 (NSW)

Building defects in new strata developments have become one of the most significant areas of property law litigation in NSW. The Design and Building Practitioners Act 2020 (NSW) (DBPA) introduced statutory duties of care owed by designers and builders to owners (including owners corporations) in relation to defective design and construction. Section 37 of the DBPA creates a duty of care to avoid economic loss caused by defective construction work - a significant extension of the liability of builders and developers, previously limited by the High Court's reluctance to recognise pure economic loss claims in negligence.

The Farah Constructions Principle: Commonwealth Statutes, Interstate Courts, and Near-Binding Persuasive Authority

SE
Shayeer Emanee
June 2026
6 min read
Foundations of Law Case Analysis Australian Law
§

In a federated legal system like Australia's, the same Commonwealth statute may simultaneously be interpreted by courts in multiple separate hierarchies - the NSW Supreme Court, the Victorian Supreme Court, the Federal Court, and so on. If each court were free to reach its own independent interpretation without any deference to the others, the result would be that the same Commonwealth law could mean different things in different states. That outcome would be not merely inconsistent but fundamentally at odds with the purpose of federal legislation, which is to create uniform national standards.

The Farah Constructions principle, established by the High Court in Farah Constructions Pty Ltd v Say-Dee Pty Ltd [2007] HCA 22, addresses this problem by elevating the persuasive weight of interstate intermediate appellate decisions on Commonwealth statutes to near-binding status.

1. The Background: Why Uniformity Matters for Commonwealth Statutes

Commonwealth statutes operate as the law of the entire nation. The Migration Act 1958 (Cth) applies the same way in Queensland as in Western Australia. The Competition and Consumer Act 2010 (Cth) governs the same transactions in NSW as in Victoria. Where different courts reach inconsistent interpretations of the same provision, the result is that the legal rights and obligations of Australians depend on their geographic location - an outcome plainly inconsistent with the purpose of national legislation.

The ordinary rules of precedent do not solve this problem. Under ordinary persuasive authority principles, a NSW Supreme Court judge is not bound by - and need not even give particular weight to - a Full Federal Court decision. But if every intermediate appellate court in every state could freely depart from every other intermediate appellate court's interpretation of a Cth statute, the result would be interpretive fragmentation across the federation.

2. What Farah Constructions Decided

In Farah Constructions, the High Court held that intermediate appellate courts - courts at the level of the Court of Appeal, the Full Court of the Federal Court, or equivalent state appellate courts - should not depart from one another's interpretation of a Commonwealth statute unless they are convinced that the earlier interpretation was "plainly wrong."

The "plainly wrong" threshold is demanding. It is not enough that the court prefers a different construction, or thinks the earlier court gave insufficient weight to some consideration. The departure must be justified on the basis that the earlier interpretation cannot be sustained - that it is clearly, demonstrably wrong as a matter of law.

The High Court in Farah also made an observation about obiter dicta from the High Court itself: statements of legal principle made by the High Court in its judgments - even where those statements are technically obiter (not strictly necessary to decide the case before the Court) - should be "seriously considered" by lower courts and followed unless the lower court is convinced they are plainly wrong.

3. The Practical Effect: Near-Binding Persuasive Authority

The Farah principle does not change the technical classification of interstate intermediate appellate decisions - they remain "persuasive" rather than "binding" in the formal sense. But by raising the threshold for departure to "plainly wrong," it effectively makes those decisions near-binding in practice. A NSW Supreme Court judge who encounters a Full Federal Court interpretation of a Cth statute will follow it unless the judge is prepared to hold that the Full Federal Court was plainly wrong - a conclusion that requires cogent justification and will almost certainly be corrected on appeal.

Situation Effect Under Farah
Full Federal Court interprets a Cth statute NSW Supreme Court (same level or lower) should follow it unless convinced it is plainly wrong
Victorian Court of Appeal interprets a Cth statute NSW Court of Appeal should follow it unless convinced it is plainly wrong (even though they are different state hierarchies)
Full Federal Court interprets a Cth statute in one way; Victorian Court of Appeal interprets it differently Conflict - neither is technically binding on the other; both should consider the conflict and attempt resolution; ultimately the High Court should resolve it
High Court obiter (not strictly necessary to the ratio) on any legal question Lower courts should follow it unless convinced it is plainly wrong

4. When Does Farah Apply?

The Farah principle is specifically directed at the interpretation of Commonwealth statutes. It does not apply to the interpretation of state statutes or to purely common law questions. Where a court is interpreting NSW state legislation, there is no equivalent cross-hierarchical consistency principle - a Victorian court's interpretation of its own state legislation is merely ordinarily persuasive for NSW courts.

The principle applies to intermediate appellate courts - not to first-instance courts. A decision of a single judge of the Federal Court is not elevated to near-binding status under Farah; it remains ordinarily persuasive. The Farah principle engages specifically at the level of full courts, courts of appeal, and equivalent intermediate appellate bodies.

5. High Court Obiter and the Farah Principle

A distinct but related aspect of the Farah decision concerns the weight of High Court obiter. The High Court held that statements of principle made in High Court judgments - even where technically obiter - should be "seriously considered" by lower courts and treated as authoritative guidance that should be followed absent cogent reasons for departure.

This aspect of Farah reflects the High Court's concern about the fragmentation of the common law and the inconsistent development of legal principles across Australia. The Lipohar v The Queen [1999] HCA 65 decision had earlier addressed the related proposition that the common law of Australia is a single body of law - not different in each state - and that the High Court is its authoritative expositor. The Farah principle on obiter reinforces this: where the High Court has spoken, even in obiter, lower courts should listen.

The practical implication for law students and practitioners is important: when researching a legal question, High Court judgments should be read in full, not just for the ratio. Concurring judgments, separately reasoned judgments, and even dissenting judgments may contain obiter observations that carry significant weight for future courts.

6. Applying Farah in a Precedent Problem

In examination problems and in practice, the Farah principle is engaged when:

  • You are in a court that is part of one hierarchy (e.g., NSW state courts).
  • The legal question involves the interpretation of a Commonwealth statute.
  • A case from a different hierarchy (e.g., Full Federal Court or interstate Court of Appeal) has interpreted the same provision.

In that situation, you do not simply apply the ordinary "persuasive" analysis. You apply the elevated Farah standard: you should follow the interstate intermediate appellate decision unless you are satisfied it is plainly wrong. The question to ask is not "do I prefer this interpretation?" but "is this interpretation clearly unsustainable?"

7. Conclusion

The Farah Constructions principle represents the common law's pragmatic response to the interpretive fragmentation that could otherwise develop in a federated jurisdiction with multiple parallel court hierarchies. By elevating interstate intermediate appellate decisions to near-binding status when they interpret Commonwealth statutes, the principle promotes the national uniformity that federal legislation is designed to achieve. For law students, understanding Farah is essential not just as a doctrinal rule but as an illustration of the way the doctrine of precedent is shaped by institutional and structural considerations that go beyond the simple hierarchy of courts.

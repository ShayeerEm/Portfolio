Guideline Judgments in NSW: Mechanism, Limits, and the Problem of Judicial Discretion

SE
Shayeer Emanee
June 2026
6 min read
Criminal Law Case Analysis Australian Law
⚡

Guideline judgments are a distinctive feature of the NSW criminal sentencing landscape. Unique in Australia at the time of their introduction, they represent a conscious attempt by the legislature and the courts to reduce sentencing disparity and improve public confidence in the consistency of criminal punishment. This analysis examines the legislative basis for guideline judgments, the mechanism by which they operate, their relationship with judicial discretion, and the criticisms that have been levelled at their use.

1. Legislative Foundation

Guideline judgments in NSW are authorised by Pt 3, Div 4 of the Crimes (Sentencing Procedure) Act 1999 (NSW). Section 36 empowers the NSW Court of Criminal Appeal to give a guideline judgment in relation to sentencing for any category of offence, either on its own motion or on the application of the Attorney General. Section 37 provides that every court exercising criminal jurisdiction in NSW must have regard to such a judgment, but is not bound to follow it if the particular circumstances of the case justify departure.

The guideline mechanism was introduced following recommendations in the New South Wales Law Reform Commission Report No 79, Sentencing (1996), which found substantial inconsistency in sentencing across the state and recommended a mechanism for appellate courts to issue prescriptive guidance to sentencing courts below.

2. The R v Henry Model

The first and most influential guideline judgment in NSW is R v Henry (1999) 46 NSWLR 346. In Henry, the Court of Criminal Appeal identified the "frequently recurring case" of armed robbery of small businesses using a knife and established a sentencing range for a first-time offender pleading not guilty. The Court set a starting point of four to five years' imprisonment for the paradigm case, with the judgment providing guidance on adjustments for aggravating and mitigating circumstances.

The Court in Henry also addressed the role of prior criminal history, the weight of general deterrence, and the significance of specific offence features - including the degree of premeditation, the vulnerability of the victim, and the nature of the weapon. These considerations were bundled into a framework that sentencing judges were expected to work through when sentencing in the paradigm category.

3. The "Frequently Recurring Case" Concept

The defining feature of the NSW guideline judgment model is the concept of the "frequently recurring case." The guideline does not attempt to prescribe sentences for the full spectrum of factual variants; rather, it identifies a modal or typical case and establishes a sentencing benchmark for that scenario. Sentencing judges are expected to calibrate sentences for non-standard cases by reference to the benchmark and the distance of the particular case from it.

This approach has significant implications. Where a case falls outside the frequently recurring scenario - whether below it in seriousness or above it - the guideline does not directly prescribe a result. Instead, it functions as an anchor point for comparison. The sentencing judge must articulate the distance between the case and the guideline and explain how that distance has been reflected in the sentence. A failure to do so where comparison has been undertaken constitutes a material error: Luchetti v R [2025] NSWCCA 222.

4. Relationship Between Guideline and Judicial Discretion

The most significant constitutional and jurisprudential tension in the guideline judgment model concerns its relationship with the individualised sentencing discretion that lies at the heart of the common law tradition. In Veen v The Queen (No 2) (1988) 164 CLR 465, the High Court affirmed that sentencing must be an individualised exercise, tailored to the particular circumstances of the offender and the offence. Guideline judgments, by contrast, introduce a degree of standardisation.

The NSW Court of Criminal Appeal in R v Henry addressed this tension directly. The Court held that the guideline was not inconsistent with judicial discretion because it established a starting point - not a fixed outcome - and explicitly required adjustment for individual circumstances. The guideline was intended to anchor the discretion, not to eliminate it. This framing has been accepted in subsequent authority, although academic commentary has questioned whether the practical effect of guideline judgments is to constrain discretion more significantly than the Court acknowledged.

6. Critique of the Guideline Judgment Model

The guideline judgment model has attracted several lines of criticism in academic and practitioner literature. The principal criticisms can be grouped as follows:

  • Rigidity: Even though guidelines formally preserve judicial discretion, the practical pressure to sentence within the guideline range may constrain the exercise of that discretion for offenders whose circumstances place them near but not within the paradigm case.
  • Definitional uncertainty: The "frequently recurring case" concept is inherently ambiguous. Different judges may characterise the same facts as within or outside the paradigm case, producing inconsistency at the margins.
  • Temporal stagnation: Guideline judgments reflect the sentencing climate and case law at the time of their making. As criminal patterns and legislative sentencing policy evolve, outdated guidelines may produce sentences that no longer reflect community standards.
  • Democratic legitimacy: Guideline judgments vest in an unelected appellate court the power to set prescriptive sentencing policy that would ordinarily be the province of the legislature.
  • Downstream hardening: Where the Attorney General applies for guidelines in response to perceived leniency, there is a risk that guidelines systematically ratchet sentences upward, contributing to prison overcrowding without commensurate reduction in recidivism.

7. Recent Developments: The Guideline in 2025

The decision in Luchetti v R [2025] NSWCCA 222 provides the most recent authoritative statement on the scope of the Henry guideline. The Court's holding that the guideline has "no role to perform" where the factual circumstances are so remote from the frequently recurring case effectively carves out a category of cases in which the guideline is treated as irrelevant rather than merely as an anchor requiring adjustment. Whether this marks a significant limitation on the guideline's reach, or merely a well-established application of existing principle, remains to be seen in subsequent authority.

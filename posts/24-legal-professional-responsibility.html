Costs Disclosure and Trust Account Obligations: Legal Professional Responsibility under the LPUL

SE
Shayeer Emanee
June 2026
9 min read
Professional Responsibility Case Analysis Australian Law
📋

Legal professional responsibility sits at the heart of every solicitor's practice. Two areas generate a disproportionate share of complaints, disciplinary proceedings, and professional negligence claims: failure to properly disclose costs to clients, and mismanagement of trust money. For the paralegal or legal assistant, an understanding of these obligations - grounded in the Legal Profession Uniform Law (NSW) (LPUL) and its associated Rules - is not merely academic; it is a daily operational requirement.

This post examines the cost disclosure regime under Part 4.3 of the LPUL and the trust account framework under Part 4.2, drawing on the substantive provisions, the Legal Profession Uniform Law Application Act 2014 (NSW) (Application Act), and relevant disciplinary decisions of the NSW Civil and Administrative Tribunal (NCAT).

Part I: Costs Disclosure under the LPUL

1.1 The Policy Rationale for Cost Disclosure

The costs disclosure regime reflects the legislature's recognition of the information asymmetry inherent in the lawyer-client relationship. A client engaging a lawyer for the first time has no reliable basis on which to evaluate the likely cost of legal services. Without mandatory disclosure, clients may receive surprise bills they cannot afford, or may not appreciate that legal proceedings carry significant financial risk even for successful parties (who may not recover all their costs).

The LPUL (which applies in NSW by virtue of the Application Act) seeks to address this by requiring that lawyers provide clients with sufficient information to make informed decisions about whether to proceed with legal services and at what cost.

1.2 Section 174: The Disclosure Obligation

The principal disclosure provision is s 174 LPUL, which requires a law practice to disclose to a client, before or as soon as practicable after being retained, certain cost information. The obligation is triggered by the commencement of the retainer and applies to all matters where legal costs are likely to be significant.

The prescribed disclosure content under s 174(1) includes:

  • The basis on which legal costs will be calculated - whether by time, fixed fee, conditional arrangement, or otherwise.
  • An estimate of the total legal costs (or the range of costs) that the client is likely to incur, or, if not reasonably practicable, an explanation of the variables that will determine the costs.
  • The client's right to receive bills and to request an itemised bill.
  • The client's right to seek costs assessment under Division 11 of Part 4.3 LPUL, and the time limits for doing so.
  • The client's right to make a complaint to the Law Society or Bar Association.

Disclosure must be in writing: s 174(2) LPUL. An oral disclosure, while better than nothing, does not satisfy the statutory requirement and will not protect the solicitor in the event of a dispute. Best practice is to include the required information in the formal costs agreement or in a separate costs disclosure letter signed and returned by the client.

1.3 Section 172: The Fair and Reasonable Costs Standard

Section 172 LPUL establishes that a client is only liable to pay costs that are "fair and reasonable" in all the circumstances. This provision operates as a substantive constraint on the lawyer's entitlement to charge, not merely as a procedural requirement. Even where the client has signed a costs agreement specifying rates, those rates remain subject to the s 172 standard.

The fair and reasonable standard requires consideration of all relevant circumstances, including:

  • The complexity of the matter and the skill required.
  • The time and labour reasonably expended.
  • The urgency of the matter and inconvenience caused to the law practice.
  • The seniority and experience of the lawyer who performed the work.
  • Whether the lawyer achieved the result sought by the client.
  • Any relevant scale of costs.
  • The instructions given by the client (for example, to work urgently or to brief senior counsel).

The inclusion of "whether the lawyer achieved the result sought" is significant: it introduces a partial outcome-related criterion that mirrors the concept of value to the client. A lawyer who spent 100 hours on a matter that could reasonably have been completed in 20 hours will not be entitled to bill for all 100 hours merely because the client authorised the work.

1.4 Conditional Costs Agreements and "No Win, No Fee"

Part 4.3, Division 5 of the LPUL governs conditional costs agreements - commonly called "no win, no fee" arrangements. These agreements permit the law practice to charge a premium on success, but impose strict requirements designed to protect clients who may be in vulnerable financial positions.

A conditional costs agreement must be in writing, set out the circumstances in which costs are payable, and specify the success fee or uplift (which cannot exceed 25% of the amount that would otherwise be payable: s 183(4) LPUL). The agreement must also contain a plain-English statement of its effect and must be signed by the client.

1.5 Costs Assessment

A client who disputes a bill has the right to apply for costs assessment under Division 11 of Part 4.3 LPUL. The application must generally be made within 12 months of the bill being given (s 198 LPUL), though the Costs Assessment Scheme (now integrated into NCAT's jurisdiction under the Legal Profession Uniform Law Application Act 2014) has discretion to extend this period where there is a reasonable explanation for the delay.

In a costs assessment, the assessor applies the s 172 fair and reasonable standard and may reduce or disallow items that fail to meet it. The assessor's determination is final on questions of fact (such as the time reasonably spent on an item) but can be appealed to the Supreme Court on questions of law: s 209 LPUL.

Part II: Trust Account Obligations

2.1 The Trust Account Framework

Trust money is money received by a law practice in the course of or in connection with legal services, that belongs to another person or that the law practice is required to hold in trust. The trust account framework under Part 4.2 LPUL exists to protect clients (and third parties) from the risk that money entrusted to solicitors will be misappropriated, commingled with office funds, or otherwise lost.

The obligations in Part 4.2 are among the most strictly enforced in Australian legal regulation. Misappropriation of trust money - even if subsequently repaid - routinely results in striking off (cancellation of admission): see Law Society of NSW v Foreman (1994) 34 NSWLR 408. Even negligent trust account errors, without dishonest intent, have led to suspension and conditions on practice.

2.2 What is Trust Money?

Section 130 LPUL defines trust money to include:

  • Money received from a client as a retainer or advance on account of costs not yet billed.
  • Settlement funds received on behalf of a client.
  • Money received to be held pending the outcome of a matter (eg contract deposits, interpleader funds).
  • Money received from a third party in connection with legal services (eg insurance proceeds, third-party damages).

What is not trust money: money received as a fixed fee for a completed matter may be treated as general money (immediately transferable to the office account) if the law practice's costs agreement says so and the money is not held on behalf of a third party. The distinction between trust money and general money is fundamental; errors in classification constitute a trust account breach.

2.3 Obligations on Receipt of Trust Money

On receipt of trust money, the law practice must:

  • Deposit the money into a general trust account at an approved financial institution as soon as practicable: s 140 LPUL.
  • Issue a trust receipt to the person who paid the money: Legal Profession Uniform General Rules 2015 (NSW), r 52.
  • Record the transaction in the trust account ledger, with the client's name, matter number, date, and amount: r 42.
  • Maintain a running balance in each client's trust ledger account: r 43.
  • Not draw on trust money other than in accordance with the directions of the person on whose behalf it is held, or as authorised by law: s 143 LPUL.

2.4 Withdrawal from Trust

Money may be withdrawn from the trust account only in limited circumstances, principally:

  • In payment of a bill that has been rendered and is due: but only if the bill has actually been given to the client (not merely prepared).
  • In payment to or on behalf of the client: with written authority or pursuant to the retainer.
  • In payment to a third party: where required by the client's instructions.
  • In accordance with a court order.

A law practice may not withdraw trust money to pay its own expenses (including unpaid fees) without the client's authority. The practice of "helping oneself" to trust money to cover a cash shortfall in the office account - even temporarily and with an intention to repay - constitutes misappropriation: see Prothonotary of the Supreme Court of NSW v Bentley [2015] NSWCA 329.

2.5 Trust Account Auditing and Reporting

Law practices that hold trust money must:

  • Have their trust accounts audited annually by an approved trust account examiner: s 155 LPUL.
  • Lodge an annual trust account statement with the Law Society of NSW within the prescribed period after the end of each financial year.
  • Immediately notify the Law Society if a trust account is overdrawn or if the law practice suspects a trust account irregularity: s 156 LPUL.
  • Maintain trust account records (ledgers, receipts, bank statements) for at least seven years: r 44.

The statutory trust account auditing requirement is supplemented by the Law Society's power to conduct surprise inspections of trust accounts at any time: s 157 LPUL. Law practices should maintain their trust account records in a format that is readily accessible and reconcilable.

2.6 Paralegal Responsibilities in Trust Account Management

Paralegals and legal assistants frequently handle trust money in a practical sense - receiving cheques from clients, preparing trust transfer forms, and maintaining the physical trust ledger. A paralegal should understand that:

  • Any trust money received should be given immediately to the principal or bookkeeper for deposit - never held in a desk drawer or petty cash.
  • Trust receipts must be issued promptly and the ledger updated the same day.
  • A paralegal should never prepare a trust withdrawal form without written authority from the supervising solicitor.
  • If a paralegal suspects a trust account irregularity - such as a discrepancy between the ledger and the bank statement - they have an obligation to raise it with the principal immediately.
  • The prohibition on misappropriation applies to all persons in the law practice, not only the principal solicitor.

Conclusion

Costs disclosure and trust account compliance are not merely regulatory formalities; they are the practical expression of the fiduciary relationship between lawyer and client. Non-compliance carries consequences that range from costs being unenforceable (for disclosure failures) to striking off and criminal prosecution (for trust account misappropriation). For a paralegal entering legal practice, internalising these obligations from the outset - and developing the habits and systems to meet them - is foundational to professional competence.

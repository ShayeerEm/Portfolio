Alternative Dispute Resolution in NSW: Mediation, Arbitration, and the Without Prejudice Privilege

SE
Shayeer Emanee
June 2026
5 min read
Dispute Resolution Case Analysis Australian Law
⚖

Alternative dispute resolution (ADR) has become a central component of the NSW civil justice system. The cost, delay, and adversarial nature of traditional court-based litigation has driven legislative, judicial, and practitioner support for ADR mechanisms - principally mediation, arbitration, and conciliation - as primary pathways to the resolution of civil disputes. This post provides a comprehensive overview of the principal ADR mechanisms in NSW, their legislative basis, and their practical operation in the context of commercial and civil disputes.

1. The Policy Context: ADR as Justice Reform

The movement toward ADR in NSW accelerated following the recognition, in both the NSW Law Reform Commission's work and in legislative reforms in the 1990s and 2000s, that the court system was overloaded and that many disputes could be resolved more efficiently and satisfactorily through non-adjudicative processes. Section 26 of the Civil Procedure Act 2005 (NSW) empowers a court, at any stage of civil proceedings, to order the parties to attempt to resolve the dispute by a specified ADR process. This provision gives the court a supervisory role in directing parties to ADR even where one party is reluctant.

2. Mediation

Mediation is a process in which an independent, trained mediator assists the parties to a dispute to identify their interests, explore options, and negotiate a resolution. The mediator does not determine the outcome; they facilitate the negotiation and assist the parties to reach their own agreement. This is the fundamental distinction between mediation and arbitration: in mediation, the resolution is consensual; in arbitration, the arbitrator determines the outcome and that determination is binding.

In NSW, court-annexed mediation is available in the Supreme Court under the Community Justice Centres Act 1983 (NSW) and the Supreme Court Act 1970 (NSW). The NSW Court of Appeal and Supreme Court regularly order mediation in appropriate cases. Parties who engage in mediation in good faith - even if mediation is unsuccessful - demonstrate a commitment to the overriding purpose under s 56 of the Civil Procedure Act 2005 (NSW) and are less vulnerable to adverse costs orders for failure to engage with ADR.

The Without Prejudice Privilege

Communications made in the course of a mediation are protected by the "without prejudice" privilege. This privilege, recognised at common law and in s 131 of the Evidence Act 1995 (NSW), prevents the contents of settlement negotiations - including mediation sessions - from being adduced as evidence in subsequent litigation. The purpose of the privilege is to encourage frank negotiation by insulating parties from the risk that their concessions or offers during settlement discussions will be used against them at trial.

Section 131 creates a rebuttable exclusion for without prejudice communications, subject to narrow exceptions including fraud, illegality, and circumstances where the dispute relates to whether the settlement was in fact concluded. Legal practitioners must advise clients clearly on the scope and limits of without prejudice protection before mediation commences.

3. Arbitration

Arbitration is a private adjudicative process in which the parties submit their dispute to one or more arbitrators, whose decision (the "award") is binding. Arbitration in NSW is governed by the Commercial Arbitration Act 2010 (NSW), which is based on the UNCITRAL Model Law on International Commercial Arbitration. The Act applies to domestic commercial arbitrations; international arbitrations are governed by the International Arbitration Act 1974 (Cth).

Commercial arbitration is commonly used in disputes arising from construction contracts, infrastructure projects, commodities trading, and financial agreements. Many commercial contracts contain arbitration clauses that require disputes to be submitted to arbitration rather than litigation. Where a valid arbitration clause exists, a court will generally stay any court proceedings commenced in breach of the clause and refer the parties to arbitration: s 8 of the Commercial Arbitration Act 2010 (NSW).

4. Expert Determination

Expert determination is a process in which a technical expert - rather than a lawyer - is appointed to determine a specific disputed question. It is commonly used in construction disputes (for quantum assessments), partnership or shareholder disputes (for valuation of business interests), and insurance claims (for forensic accounting matters). The parties agree in advance that the expert's determination will be final and binding on the technical question referred.

The legal status of an expert determination was settled in Renard Constructions (ME) Pty Ltd v Minister for Public Works (1992) 26 NSWLR 234, where the NSW Court of Appeal confirmed that a binding expert determination clause in a commercial contract would be given effect, and that the grounds for challenging an expert's determination are narrow: fraud, bias, failure to act on the correct question, or departure from the mandate given.

5. Costs Consequences of Failure to Mediate

A party who unreasonably refuses to participate in ADR - whether court-ordered or voluntarily proposed - may face adverse costs consequences. Under the general costs discretion in s 98 of the Civil Procedure Act 2005 (NSW), a court may take into account a party's conduct, including failure to engage with ADR, in determining the appropriate costs order. A successful party who refused a reasonable offer to mediate may be denied their costs of the proceedings, or may be ordered to pay the other party's costs from the date of the refusal.

This principle has been applied in the context of Calderbank offers (offers made "without prejudice save as to costs") and formal offers of compromise under r 20.26 of the UCPR. The interplay between Calderbank offers, formal offers of compromise, and ADR engagement creates a costs landscape that strongly incentivises parties to resolve disputes before they reach trial.

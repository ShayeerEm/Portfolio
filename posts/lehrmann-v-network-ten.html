Lehrmann v Network Ten Pty Limited [2024] FCA 369: A Landmark Ruling on the Defence of Truth and Public Interest Journalism

SE
Shayeer Emanee
15 April 2024
10 min read
Defamation Law Media Law Federal Court Defence of Truth Briginshaw Principle
⚖

Few cases in recent Australian legal history have captured public attention as completely as Lehrmann v Network Ten Pty Limited [2024] FCA 369. At its heart, this was a defamation trial — but it became something far larger: a collision between the criminal and civil legal systems, a test of the limits of public interest journalism, and a very public reckoning with what it means to be found "innocent" in one court while being judged liable in another.

Justice Lee's judgment, handed down on 15 April 2024 after a lengthy and intensely scrutinised trial in the Federal Court, illuminates some of the most fundamental and, for many, unsettling features of Australian law. This case summary walks through the facts, the legal issues, the decision, and its wider implications.

Facts

The case of Lehrmann v Network Ten began with events that took place in the early hours of 23 March 2019 at Parliament House in Canberra. Bruce Lehrmann and Brittany Higgins, who were both junior staffers for then Minister for Defence Linda Reynolds, had been out drinking with colleagues before returning to the Minister's office. Security records showed they entered the office around 1:48 am. Ms Higgins alleged that while she was in a state of semi-consciousness due to intoxication, Mr Lehrmann engaged in non-consensual sexual intercourse with her on the Minister's couch. Mr Lehrmann has always denied these allegations, maintaining that no sexual contact occurred at all.

In February 2021, Network Ten's program The Project aired an interview between journalist Lisa Wilkinson and Ms Higgins. While Mr Lehrmann was not named in the broadcast, he argued that he was easily identifiable to his peers and the public. A criminal trial against Mr Lehrmann was launched in 2022 but was eventually aborted due to juror misconduct. No criminal verdict was ever reached. Following the abandonment of the criminal proceedings, Mr Lehrmann launched a civil defamation lawsuit against Network Ten and Ms Wilkinson, claiming the broadcast had destroyed his reputation by portraying him as a rapist.1

The Trial Court

The defamation trial was heard in the Federal Court of Australia before Justice Michael Lee. Unlike a criminal trial, which is heard by a jury, this civil matter was decided by a single judge. The trial was highly publicised and involved extensive testimony from both Ms Higgins and Mr Lehrmann, as well as various witnesses who worked at Parliament House.

Because the case was a civil lawsuit for defamation, the burden of proof shifted between the parties at different stages. Mr Lehrmann had to prove he was identified and defamed,2 while the defendants had to prove their legal defences.

Legal Issues

The court had to resolve several complex legal issues, the most significant being the Defence of Truth (also known as justification). In Australian defamation law, if a defendant can prove that the main point of the allegation is substantially true, the plaintiff cannot win. The central question was whether Mr Lehrmann had, in fact, raped Ms Higgins.

The Standard of Proof and the Briginshaw Principle

A major hurdle for the court was the standard of proof. In a criminal trial, the prosecution must prove guilt beyond a reasonable doubt. However, in this civil trial, the court applied the balance of probabilities standard,3 meaning the judge only had to decide if it was more likely than not that the events occurred.

Justice Lee also had to apply the Briginshaw principle.4 This legal rule states that when a very serious allegation is made in a civil court — such as rape — the judge must be very careful and require clear, high-quality evidence before being reasonably satisfied that the event happened. The principle does not raise the standard of proof; it demands that the quality and cogency of the evidence be proportionate to the gravity of the finding.

Further Issues Before the Court

Beyond truth, the court also had to consider:

  • Identification: Did the broadcast actually identify Mr Lehrmann even though his name wasn't used?
  • Qualified Privilege: Even if the story was false, did the media act reasonably in the public interest?5
  • Statutory Public Interest Defence: Did the defendants reasonably believe the publication was in the public interest under the new section 29A of the Defamation Act?6

The Court's Decision

Justice Lee handed down his judgment on 15 April 2024.7 He found that Mr Lehrmann was indeed identified by the broadcast. However, the court ultimately ruled in favour of Network Ten and Ms Wilkinson because they successfully proved the Defence of Truth.

Justice Lee found that, on the balance of probabilities, Mr Lehrmann had engaged in sexual intercourse with Ms Higgins without her consent. The judge noted that while Ms Higgins was an unreliable witness on some minor details, her core account of the assault was forceful and believable. Conversely, the judge described Mr Lehrmann as a "persistent, self-interested liar." The court found that Mr Lehrmann was recklessly indifferent to whether Ms Higgins was consenting, given her high level of intoxication.

"Having escaped the lion's den of the criminal trial, Mr Lehrmann made the mistake of going back for his hat."
— Justice Lee, Lehrmann v Network Ten Pty Limited [2024] FCA 369

Importantly, while the Truth defence succeeded, the Qualified Privilege and Public Interest defences both failed. Justice Lee criticised the journalists for failing to give Mr Lehrmann enough time to respond and for lacking reasonable grounds in how they prepared the story, describing the reporting as having a "want of reasonable scrutiny." Despite this, because the core allegation of rape was found to be true to the civil standard, Mr Lehrmann's claim was dismissed.

Implications of the Decision

The Parallel Nature of the Australian Legal System

This decision has massive implications for both defamation law and media reporting in Australia. First, it demonstrates the parallel nature of the Australian legal system. A person can be "innocent" in the eyes of the criminal law — because guilt was not proved beyond a reasonable doubt — but found to have committed the act in a civil court, where the standard is lower. This can be deeply confusing for the public but is a fundamental feature of how our laws work. The criminal standard protects the accused; the civil standard resolves disputes between parties. They serve different functions and it is constitutionally and jurisprudentially sound that they reach different outcomes.

A Warning to the Media

For the media, the case serves as a serious warning. Even though Network Ten won, the judge's rejection of their public interest defence demonstrates that courts will hold journalists to a high standard. If the Truth defence had failed — that is, if the court had not been satisfied that the rape occurred — Network Ten would likely have faced liability for millions of dollars because their reporting was deemed procedurally unreasonable. The message is clear: being right does not excuse being reckless in how you report.

Defamation Tourism and the Risks of Litigation

The case also highlights the extreme risks of defamation litigation. Mr Lehrmann chose to sue for defamation, and the result was a public judicial finding — applying the civil standard — that he is a rapist. Justice Lee's famous remark that Lehrmann, having escaped the "lion's den" of the criminal trial, made the mistake of "going back for his hat" encapsulates this perfectly. Sometimes the most strategically dangerous thing a person can do is press their luck in court.

Miscellaneous Observations

The Limitation Period

An interesting aspect of this case was its impact on the limitation period. Usually, you must sue for defamation within one year of the publication. Mr Lehrmann sued nearly two years later. The court allowed this extension because of the ongoing criminal proceedings — demonstrating the court's willingness to exercise flexibility when criminal and civil processes overlap and interact.

The Cost of Justice

The cost of this trial was estimated at over $10 million. Because Mr Lehrmann lost, he was ordered to pay the legal costs of Network Ten and Ms Wilkinson. Since he likely cannot afford this, the case raises profound questions about access to justice and who ultimately bears the cost of high-stakes public litigation.

The Appeal

In December 2025, the Full Federal Court dismissed Mr Lehrmann's appeal, confirming that Justice Lee's original findings were legally sound and that no appellable error had been made in the primary judgment.

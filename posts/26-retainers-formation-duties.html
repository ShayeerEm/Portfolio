Retainers in Australian Legal Practice: Formation, Scope, Implied Engagements, and the Duties They Create

SE
Shayeer Emanee
June 2026
8 min read
Professional Responsibility Case Analysis Australian Law
📋

Every engagement between a solicitor and a client is governed by a retainer. The retainer is not merely a billing document - it is the foundational contract from which the entire lawyer-client relationship flows. It determines who the client is, what work the solicitor is authorised to do, how the solicitor is paid, and what legal and ethical duties are engaged. For a first-year law student or paralegal entering practice, understanding retainers is understanding the architecture of the profession.

1. What is a Retainer?

A retainer is the contract between a law practice and its client. It defines the scope of the engagement - the specific legal services being provided - and sets the terms under which those services will be delivered. The term "retainer" has an older meaning (a fee paid to "retain" a lawyer on standby), but in modern Australian legal practice it refers to the overall contractual engagement.

A retainer does not need to be in writing to be valid. It can be formed orally, in writing, or implied from the conduct of both parties. However, written retainers are strongly preferred - and in many cases legally required - because they provide certainty, evidence the scope of the engagement, and are essential for complying with cost disclosure obligations under s 174 of the Legal Profession Uniform Law (NSW) (LPUL).

2. Formation: The Contract Law Elements

Because a retainer is a contract, it must satisfy the standard requirements of contract formation:

Element What It Means in a Retainer Context
Agreement Both the solicitor and client must actually agree to the engagement - by words, writing, or conduct
Consideration The solicitor promises legal services; the client promises to pay (even in pro bono matters, nominal consideration or a signed engagement letter is good practice)
Intention to be legally bound Both parties intend the arrangement to be enforceable, not merely social
Capacity The client must be legally able to enter a contract (raises special issues for children and adults who lack capacity - see separate post)
Free consent No duress, undue influence, or misrepresentation inducing the client to retain the solicitor
Statutory formalities Compliance with costs disclosure and other requirements under the LPUL

3. Implied Retainers: Steele v Marshan

The most practically important question in retainer law is often whether one exists at all. Clients who refuse to pay sometimes argue that they never formally engaged the solicitor. Australian courts have consistently rejected this argument where the parties' conduct is consistent with a retainer having been formed.

In Steele v Marshan [2012] NSWCA 141, the New South Wales Court of Appeal held that a retainer can be implied from the conduct of the parties. Mr Steele argued he had never signed a formal retainer and therefore owed no fees. The Court disagreed: because he had continued providing instructions to the solicitors and accepting their work product over an extended period, the parties' conduct was consistent only with a retainer having been agreed. The Court held an implied retainer existed and Steele was liable for the fees.

The existence of a retainer - and its terms - may be inferred from the whole of the circumstances of the dealing between the parties, including their conduct.

The lesson from Steele v Marshan is twofold: solicitors must document their engagements carefully to establish the scope of the retainer; and clients cannot retrospectively deny a retainer simply because they did not sign a formal document. Conduct is as binding as a signature.

4. Scope of the Retainer

The scope of the retainer defines the limits of what the solicitor is engaged to do. This matters enormously in professional negligence cases. A solicitor has no duty to give advice or take steps falling outside the scope of their retainer - but they may acquire a duty to advise on related issues that emerge during the engagement, even if not explicitly included.

In Roberts v Cashman [2000] NSWSC 770, a client joined a class action against a pharmaceutical company. The law firm never conducted a proper face-to-face interview - only a questionnaire - and as a result failed to identify that the client had a separate and stronger claim against a furniture manufacturer (a banana chair that had collapsed and caused her injuries). The firm did not have this claim within the scope of their retainer because it had never been identified. But the Court held the firm was professionally negligent for failing to conduct a proper interview that would have identified it. The limitation period on the separate claim expired and the client lost it entirely.

Roberts v Cashman establishes an important principle: a solicitor engaging with a client on one matter may have a duty to advise about related issues that are sufficiently connected to the subject matter of the retainer, even if those issues were never explicitly discussed. Questionnaires cannot substitute for an adequate face-to-face interview.

5. Who Is the Client?

The retainer defines who the client is - and this is not always obvious. Consider the following situations, all of which regularly arise in practice:

  • A company is the client, not its individual director. The solicitor owes duties to the company as an entity, not to the director personally. Instructions must come from authorised representatives of the company, not just any director or employee.
  • In a family property transaction, both spouses may be the client - or only one. If both, concurrent conflict rules immediately engage.
  • In litigation, the solicitor acts for the named party. A third party paying the legal fees (such as a parent funding a child's claim) is generally not the client and cannot give binding instructions.
  • In estate matters, the solicitor acts for the executor, not the beneficiaries - even though the beneficiaries have a direct interest in the estate.

Getting the client identity wrong creates serious problems: duties of confidentiality and loyalty run to the client. If you mistake who the client is, you may inadvertently breach duties to the real client or fail to properly serve them.

6. The Agency Relationship

When a solicitor is engaged under a retainer, they become the client's agent. This means that acts taken by the solicitor within the scope of their authority are legally attributable to the client and can bind the client in dealings with third parties.

There are two types of authority:

  • Actual authority: what the client has explicitly told the solicitor they are authorised to do. This comes from the client's direct instructions.
  • Implied authority: what is incidental and necessary to doing the work the solicitor has been engaged to do, even without specific instructions. A solicitor engaged to conduct litigation has implied authority to take ordinary procedural steps without getting client approval for each one.

A solicitor who acts outside the scope of their authority - whether actual or implied - may be personally liable to third parties and may have breached their duty to the client. This is a significant risk in large litigated matters where solicitors make statements or concessions in court that were not authorised by the client.

7. Duties Created by the Retainer

The formation of a retainer does not just create contractual obligations - it triggers a network of duties across multiple legal frameworks simultaneously:

Duty Source Key Obligation
Contractual duty The retainer itself Perform the agreed legal services with the skill and diligence promised
Duty of care in negligence Tort law Act competently; can be sued for professional negligence if the standard is not met
Fiduciary duty Equity Act in the client's best interests; avoid conflicts of interest; make no secret profit
Duty of confidentiality Contract, equity, and ASCR Rule 9 Keep all client information confidential, even after the retainer ends
Costs obligations ss 172, 174 LPUL Disclose costs upfront in writing; charge only fair and reasonable fees
Consumer law obligations Australian Consumer Law Legal services are 'services' within the ACL; statutory guarantees of acceptable quality apply
Professional conduct rules ASCR; LPUL Comply with the Australian Solicitors' Conduct Rules throughout the engagement

The fiduciary duty deserves particular attention. A fiduciary must act solely in the interests of their principal - the client. This means a solicitor must not allow their own interests, or the interests of any other client, to conflict with the client's interests. The fiduciary duty in equity supplements the contractual and professional conduct obligations; it is not replaced by them. The consequence of a fiduciary breach is an account of profits, constructive trust, or equitable compensation - remedies that operate independently of negligence or contract.

8. Terminating the Retainer

A retainer can be terminated by either party, but the circumstances and consequences differ. The client may terminate at any time (subject to paying for work done). The solicitor may withdraw from the retainer only in limited circumstances and must not withdraw in a way that prejudices the client.

Under ASCR Rule 13, a solicitor must not terminate a retainer unless the client has had a reasonable opportunity to make other arrangements for representation. Withdrawal without adequate notice - particularly in the middle of proceedings - may constitute professional misconduct and expose the solicitor to a costs order. In practice, withdrawal from litigation matters requires the court's permission at certain stages.

Even after the retainer is terminated, certain duties survive. The duty of confidentiality is the most significant: it continues indefinitely, beyond the termination of the retainer and beyond the death of the client. Former client conflicts rules under ASCR Rule 10 also continue to apply, preventing a solicitor from acting against a former client using that client's confidential information.

9. Conclusion

The retainer is the foundation of every engagement between solicitor and client. It establishes who is being served, what work is being done, and what duties are owed. Understanding retainer formation - including the possibility of implied retainers as confirmed in Steele v Marshan - is essential for both solicitors (who must document their engagements) and clients (who cannot escape obligations simply by refusing to sign). The web of duties that flows from the retainer extends far beyond simple contract: it encompasses fiduciary obligation, professional conduct rules, statutory costs requirements, and the duty of confidentiality that outlasts the engagement itself.

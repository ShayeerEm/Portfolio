Appellate Advocacy in NSW Criminal Law: Grounds of Sentence Appeal and the Resentencing Exercise

SE
Shayeer Emanee
June 2026
5 min read
Criminal Law Case Analysis Australian Law
⚡

The NSW Court of Criminal Appeal (CCA) is the primary appellate court for criminal matters in New South Wales. It hears appeals against both conviction and sentence from the District Court and the Supreme Court, and appeals against sentence from the Local Court (on a case stated basis via the Supreme Court). Understanding the grounds of appeal that are available, the procedural requirements for bringing an appeal, and the approach the CCA takes to re-sentencing is essential knowledge for practitioners working in criminal law firms.

1. The Appeal Jurisdiction

The jurisdiction of the NSW Court of Criminal Appeal is conferred by the Criminal Appeal Act 1912 (NSW). Section 5(1) of the Act provides that a person convicted of an indictable offence may, with leave, appeal to the Court against conviction, sentence, or both. The leave requirement ensures that only matters raising genuine legal issues reach the appellate court, and it serves as a gating mechanism against hopeless appeals.

Appeals against sentence under s 5(1)(c) are the most common category of CCA appeal. These require the appellant to identify an error of law or principle, or to establish that the sentence is manifestly excessive or manifestly inadequate. The prosecution may also appeal against sentence on the ground of manifest inadequacy under s 5D of the Act.

2. Grounds of Appeal Against Sentence

In order to succeed on a sentencing appeal, the appellant must establish error. The range of errors that can vitiate a sentence is broader than it might initially appear. In Markarian v R (2005) 228 CLR 357, the High Court set out the framework for appellate intervention in sentencing, affirming that an appellate court is not entitled to substitute its own view of the appropriate sentence unless it identifies error. The principal grounds of appeal against sentence are:

  • Error of principle: The sentencing judge applied an incorrect legal principle - for example, applying a guideline judgment to facts that fall outside its scope (Luchetti v R [2025] NSWCCA 222), or failing to give proper effect to the Verdins principles where mental health evidence was before the court.
  • Error of fact: The sentencing judge made an error in finding or assessing the facts - for example, incorrectly characterising the nature of the weapon used, or misattributing a statement to the wrong party.
  • Failure to take into account a relevant matter: The sentencing judge omitted to consider a factor that was required by law to be taken into account - for example, the utilitarian value of a guilty plea.
  • Taking into account an irrelevant matter: The sentencing judge gave weight to a factor that should not have influenced the sentence - for example, subjective factors in the objective gravity assessment (Towers v R [2025] NSWCCA 142).
  • Manifest excess or inadequacy: The sentence, taken as a whole, is so disproportionate to the gravity of the offence and the circumstances of the offender that it implies an error somewhere in the process, even if the specific error cannot be identified.

3. The Double Jeopardy Principle in Crown Appeals

Where the Crown appeals against sentence on the ground of manifest inadequacy, the principle against double jeopardy operates to moderate the sentence that the CCA would otherwise impose on resentencing. The principle reflects the fundamental fairness concern that an accused who has been sentenced and accepted their punishment should not face the prospect of a significantly heavier sentence as a result of prosecutorial dissatisfaction. The CCA, on a successful Crown appeal, typically imposes a sentence that is less than it would have imposed had it been the original sentencing court. The difference represents the "double jeopardy discount."

4. The Resentencing Exercise

Where the CCA allows an appeal against sentence, it may either: (a) quash the sentence and remit the matter for resentencing by the original court; or (b) quash the sentence and resentence the offender itself. The second approach - direct resentencing by the CCA - has become more common and is generally preferred where the facts are not in dispute and the resentencing exercise is straightforward. In Luchetti v R [2025] NSWCCA 222, the Court resentenced the offender directly, identifying a starting point of two years, applying a 25% discount to arrive at 18 months, and then holding that time already served exceeded the sentence, warranting immediate release.

When the CCA resentences, it does so on the facts as they existed at the original sentence date, not as they exist at the time of the appeal. This means that, for example, post-sentence rehabilitation, institutional behaviour, or parole compliance cannot ordinarily be taken into account on resentencing. However, the CCA will take into account the time already served, and may adjust the commencement date of the new sentence to reflect that the offender has already served a period in custody.

5. Preparing a Sentencing Appeal: The Practitioner's Checklist

  • Obtain a copy of the sentencing remarks (judge's written or transcribed reasons) and review carefully for errors of principle or fact.
  • Identify the specific statutory provisions relevant to the offence and confirm whether the judge applied them correctly.
  • Review the transcript of the sentencing hearing for any submissions not addressed, or any concessions made by counsel that should not have been made.
  • Obtain any expert reports (psychiatric, psychological, pre-sentence reports) relied upon at sentence and verify whether they were addressed in the remarks.
  • Calculate whether the sentence falls within the range established by comparable cases and guideline judgments.
  • Assess whether the non-parole period is correctly calculated (generally at least 3/4 of the head sentence under s 44 unless special circumstances are found).
  • Confirm that the discount for any guilty plea was correctly calculated and applied.
  • Identify any failure to apply the Verdins principles where mental health evidence was tendered.
  • Assess the strength of any "manifest excess" argument and whether the court would need to identify a specific error.
  • Review the time limits: a sentence appeal must be filed within 28 days of sentence under the Criminal Appeal Rules 1952 (NSW).

6. The CCA and Consistency in Sentencing

The CCA serves a systemic function beyond the correction of individual errors: it maintains consistency in sentencing across the NSW court hierarchy. Guideline judgments, discussed elsewhere, are one mechanism for achieving this. The "tariff" approach - where the CCA publishes and refers to sentencing statistics and precedent - is another. Over time, the accumulation of CCA decisions creates a body of comparative material that sentencing courts and practitioners rely upon to calibrate appropriate sentences for new cases. In this sense, the CCA's function in sentencing law is as much about institution-building as it is about individual justice.

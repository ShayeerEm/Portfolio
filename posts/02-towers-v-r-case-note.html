Towers v R [2025] NSWCCA 142: Keeping Objective and Subjective Factors Separate in Sentencing

SE
Shayeer Emanee
June 2026
5 min read
Criminal Law Case Analysis Australian Law
⚡

In Towers v R [2025] NSWCCA 142, the NSW Court of Criminal Appeal confronted a question that sits at the intersection of sentencing procedure and appellate review: when does a sentencing judge's articulation of objective gravity stray impermissibly into incorporating subjective considerations? The case provides an important comparator to the near-contemporaneous decision in Luchetti v R [2025] NSWCCA 222 and illuminates the boundaries of what constitutes a "material error" in the identification of objective seriousness.

Citation

Towers v R [2025] NSWCCA 142.

Procedural History

The appellant was convicted and sentenced in the District Court of New South Wales. He sought leave to appeal the severity of his sentence to the NSW Court of Criminal Appeal pursuant to s 5(1)(c) of the Criminal Appeal Act 1912 (NSW), on the ground that the sentencing judge had erred in the assessment of objective gravity by incorporating subjective factors - specifically the offender's age and prior criminal antecedents - under the heading of objective considerations.

Relevant Facts

The appellant was convicted of an offence involving a degree of violence. The sentencing judge, in the section of the remarks on sentence devoted to objective gravity, expressly listed the appellant's age and criminal antecedents among the factors that informed her assessment of how serious the offence was. On appeal, the appellant contended that this constituted a reversible error because objective gravity is a matter of the circumstances of the offence - not the characteristics of the offender.

Legal Issues

  • Whether a sentencing judge commits a material error by expressly listing subjective factors (age, criminal history) in the section of the remarks addressing objective gravity.
  • What the correct method is for distinguishing objective from subjective factors in sentencing remarks.
  • Whether such an error, if established, is sufficient to vitiate the sentence and require resentencing.

The Distinction Between Objective and Subjective Factors

The objective seriousness of an offence refers to the gravity of the criminal conduct itself - assessed by reference to the nature and circumstances of the offending, not the characteristics of the offender. Relevant objective factors include: the nature and degree of violence or threatened violence, the degree of premeditation, the vulnerability of the victim, the nature and value of anything taken, and the harm caused or risked.

Subjective factors, by contrast, are matters personal to the offender: age, criminal history, mental health, family circumstances, remorse, and prospects of rehabilitation. Under the framework in Markarian v R (2005) 228 CLR 357, these two categories of factors must be kept analytically distinct. While both ultimately inform the sentencing outcome, conflating them at the stage of objective assessment risks producing a sentence that is incorrectly calibrated - either inflating or deflating the baseline severity before mitigating or aggravating circumstances are applied.

Reasons for the Decision

The Court in Towers v R found that the sentencing judge had committed a material error. The express listing of the appellant's age and criminal antecedents under the heading "Objective Gravity" was not, on a fair reading of the remarks on sentence, a mere organisational imprecision. The judge had treated these matters as bearing on the seriousness of the conduct itself, rather than as separate considerations going to matters such as specific deterrence or the protection of the community.

The Court drew an important contrast between this case and what might be described as a "mere mislabelling" error - where a sentencing judge correctly identifies and weighs a subjective factor but mistakenly places it under the wrong heading. In Towers, the error went beyond mislabelling: the judge's reasons disclosed that the subjective factors had inflated her assessment of the objective gravity of the conduct.

This is to be contrasted with the treatment of a similar argument in Luchetti v R [2025] NSWCCA 222, where the Court expressly distinguished Towers. In Luchetti, the Court rejected the argument that the sentencing judge had incorporated subjective factors into her objective assessment, holding that a fair reading of those remarks did not support such an inference. The distinction is therefore one of substance, not form.

The Comparative Method: Towers v Luchetti

Issue Towers v R [2025] NSWCCA 142 Luchetti v R [2025] NSWCCA 222
Primary error alleged Conflation of subjective factors with objective gravity Erroneous application of Henry guideline to factually remote circumstances
Was the error established? Yes - expressly listed under "Objective Gravity" Yes - comparison not factually sustainable
Subjective factors argument (Ground 1(i)) Argument succeeded Argument rejected - no fair reading supported it
Result Sentence quashed; resentenced Sentence quashed; resentenced

Ratio

A sentencing judge who expressly lists the offender's subjective characteristics - age, criminal antecedents - as factors that bear upon the objective gravity of the offence commits a material error of principle. The error lies not in the mention of those factors, but in their deployment as part of the foundation for the assessment of how serious the conduct itself was. Where the reasons disclose this error, the sentence cannot stand.

Significance for Practitioners

Towers v R provides practitioners with a clear basis on which to challenge sentencing remarks where the judge's assessment of objective gravity is infected by subjective considerations. The key question is not whether subjective factors appear near an objective analysis, but whether the reasons reveal that they were used to inform the assessment of the gravity of the conduct. Written submissions to sentencing courts should proactively address this distinction, and legal representatives should scrutinise sentencing remarks carefully for the conflation of these categories.

It is equally important to understand the limits of the principle. Towers v R does not establish a rule that subjective factors must never be mentioned in proximity to objective assessments. A sentencing judge who correctly identifies objective gravity and then separately addresses aggravating subjective factors (such as criminal history under s 21A(2)(d) of the Crimes (Sentencing Procedure) Act 1999 (NSW)) commits no error, even if the structural organisation of the remarks places them close together. The vice is in the use of subjective factors to inform the objective assessment, not in their mere proximity.

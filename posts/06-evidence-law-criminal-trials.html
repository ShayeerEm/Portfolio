Tendency, Coincidence, and Hearsay in NSW Criminal Trials: The Uniform Evidence Act Framework

SE
Shayeer Emanee
June 2026
5 min read
Criminal Law Case Analysis Australian Law
⚡

The Uniform Evidence Acts - the Evidence Act 1995 (Cth) and its state counterparts, including the Evidence Act 1995 (NSW) - introduced a comprehensive codification of evidence law that replaced the common law in the jurisdictions that adopted it. In criminal proceedings, three categories of evidence generate the most contested litigation: tendency evidence, coincidence evidence, and hearsay. This post provides an analytical overview of each, with reference to the critical case law and the policy tensions that animate them.

1. The Uniform Evidence Law Framework

The Evidence Act 1995 (NSW) operates on a general exclusionary logic: evidence that is relevant is admissible unless excluded by a specific provision. The Act establishes a series of prohibitions - hearsay, tendency, coincidence, opinion, credibility - each of which excludes a category of otherwise relevant evidence, subject to exceptions that restore admissibility in defined circumstances.

The Act also includes an overarching discretion in s 137 to exclude prosecution evidence in criminal proceedings where its probative value is outweighed by the danger of unfair prejudice to the defendant. This discretion operates independently of the specific exclusionary rules and reflects the court's supervisory role in ensuring that criminal trials are conducted fairly.

2. Tendency Evidence (ss 97-98A)

Tendency evidence is evidence that shows that a person - typically the accused in a criminal trial - has a tendency to act in a particular way or to have a particular state of mind. Section 97 of the Evidence Act 1995 (NSW) provides that tendency evidence is not admissible to prove that a person acted in a way that is consistent with that tendency unless the court is satisfied that the tendency evidence has "significant probative value."

The significance of the probative value threshold - rather than the standard "probative value" in civil matters - reflects the heightened concern in criminal proceedings that evidence of bad character or prior misconduct may distort the fact-finder's reasoning, leading to a verdict based on moral condemnation rather than proof of the specific conduct charged.

In Hughes v The Queen (2017) 263 CLR 338, the High Court clarified the test for significant probative value in tendency evidence. The Court held that the question is whether the tendency evidence could rationally affect the assessment of the probability of the existence of a fact in issue, and that the significance of the probative value must be assessed in the context of the case as a whole - including any dispute about whether the conduct constituting the tendency actually occurred.

The Notice Requirement

A party intending to adduce tendency evidence must give reasonable notice to the opposing party: s 97(1)(a). In practice, this notice is given through a formal notice of tendency, which must identify the evidence to be adduced and the tendency it is said to establish. Failure to give notice operates as a ground for exclusion, though the court has a discretion to allow late notice where the failure was inadvertent and no prejudice results.

3. Coincidence Evidence (ss 98-99)

Coincidence evidence is evidence that more than one event or matter bears similarities to one another such that it is reasonably improbable that they occurred by coincidence. The rationale for admitting such evidence is that a high degree of similarity between two independently occurring events is itself probative of a common cause - typically, that the accused committed both acts.

The threshold for coincidence evidence is, like tendency evidence, "significant probative value": s 98. The courts have emphasised that the similarity between events must go beyond general pattern and must be sufficiently specific that coincidence is a genuinely implausible explanation. In Phillips v The Queen (2006) 225 CLR 303, the High Court held that the similarities must be so strong that it is improbable they are the result of coincidence rather than design.

4. Hearsay Evidence (ss 59-75)

The hearsay rule in s 59 of the Evidence Act 1995 (NSW) provides that evidence of a previous representation made by a person is not admissible to prove the existence of a fact that the person intended to assert by the representation. This rule excludes "second-hand" evidence - the witness on the stand is not permitted to tell the court what another person said, if the purpose of doing so is to prove the truth of what that other person asserted.

The policy underpinning the hearsay rule is the requirement for the maker of a representation to be present in court, on oath, and subject to cross-examination. Out-of-court statements cannot be tested in the same way; the credibility of the absent declarant cannot be assessed by the fact-finder, and the circumstances of the statement may affect its reliability in ways the jury cannot evaluate.

Exception Provision Conditions
First-hand hearsay (civil) s 63 Maker not available; representation made when event was fresh in memory
First-hand hearsay (criminal) s 65 Maker not available AND representation made when event was fresh, or as contemporaneous record, or under obligation to report
Business records s 69 Representation part of a record of a business; made in course of business
Admissions by defendants s 81 Hearsay rule does not apply to admissions made by party opponent
Prior consistent statements s 108 To rebut suggestion of recent fabrication

5. The Discretion to Exclude: Section 137

Even where tendency, coincidence, or hearsay evidence is technically admissible under the Act, the court must nevertheless consider whether to exclude it under s 137. This section requires the court to refuse to admit prosecution evidence in criminal proceedings if its probative value is outweighed by the danger of unfair prejudice to the defendant. The inquiry is prospective - the court must assess how the evidence will likely affect the fact-finder's reasoning process, and whether that effect may be unfair.

The probative value-to-prejudice balance under s 137 is distinct from the admissibility threshold under ss 97 and 98. Evidence may have significant probative value (sufficient to cross the tendency/coincidence threshold) and yet still be excluded under s 137 if the risk of misuse is sufficiently high. Courts must particularly guard against the use of tendency evidence to invite a general inference of bad character rather than specific reasoning about the charged conduct.

Duties to the Court: Candour, Witnesses, and the Prohibition on Ex Parte Communication

SE
Shayeer Emanee
June 2026
8 min read
Professional Responsibility Case Analysis Australian Law
📋

The duty to the court is one of the most foundational obligations in legal professional ethics. It reflects a structural reality of the adversarial system: while solicitors represent clients, they simultaneously serve the court as officers of the court, and the proper administration of justice depends on that dual role being honoured. The obligations that flow from the duty to the court - candour, limits on witness conduct, and restrictions on ex parte communications - are not merely rules of professional conduct. They are the conditions that make an adversarial system trustworthy.

1. Candour to the Court: The Baseline

ASCR Rule 19 imposes on solicitors a duty of candour to the court. This is the obligation not to mislead the court. It operates alongside, but is distinct from, the duty to advance the client's case. A solicitor must not, in any submission to the court, make a statement of fact that the solicitor knows to be false. This obligation extends to submissions, statements of case, documents tendered in evidence, and representations about the law.

The duty applies to knowledge, not suspicion. A solicitor who suspects a client's instructions may be unreliable does not automatically breach Rule 19 by advancing them - but a solicitor who knows an instruction to be false and advances it knowingly is in direct breach. The word "knowingly" does a lot of work in Rule 19, but it should not be read as a loophole: where facts are readily verifiable and the solicitor simply chooses not to verify them, courts have been willing to attribute knowledge.

The candour duty also requires a solicitor not to make legal submissions that they know to be false or to cite cases they know have been overruled. This catches the AI hallucination risk: a solicitor who submits an AI-generated case citation without verification, and who submits a case that does not exist, breaches the duty of candour even if no dishonesty was intended.

2. Witness Conduct: No Property in a Witness

One of the most important practical rules governing solicitor conduct in litigation is the principle that there is no "property" in a witness. This rule, established in R v Shaw (1991) 57 A Crim R 425, provides that witnesses - even witnesses who are expected to give evidence favourable to your client - do not belong to either party. You cannot instruct a witness not to speak to the other side. You cannot prevent the opposing party from interviewing a witness. You cannot tell a witness to avoid contact with the opponent's lawyers.

Justice Nathan in R v Shaw stated the principle directly: "Eye witnesses do not belong to a camp, but are within the class of persons whom juries expect and are entitled to hear." The rule reflects a fundamental feature of the adversarial system: witness evidence is not property to be owned, curated, or sequestered by the party who first finds the witness. The opposing party is entitled to hear what the witness has to say.

In practice, this creates a difficult tension. Solicitors regularly spend significant time and effort locating and interviewing potential witnesses. They may develop a degree of possessiveness about favourable witnesses. The rule requires them to resist this instinct: a witness may speak to the other side, and the solicitor's job is to present their evidence effectively - not to prevent the other side from knowing what it is.

3. Conferring with Witnesses Under Cross-Examination: ASCR Rule 26.1

While the "no property in a witness" rule addresses interactions before and between court appearances, ASCR Rule 26.1 addresses a specific and critical moment: the period when a witness is under cross-examination.

Rule 26.1 provides that a solicitor must not communicate with a witness who is under cross-examination about the evidence they are giving or about to give, without the express consent of the cross-examiner or the leave of the court. This applies from the moment cross-examination begins until it concludes - including during any adjournment, lunch break, or overnight recess.

The rationale is straightforward: cross-examination is designed to test the witness's evidence without any opportunity for coaching between sessions. If the solicitor could speak to their witness during an adjournment in cross-examination, the witness could be re-briefed, inconsistencies coached away, and the cross-examination undermined. Rule 26.1 prevents this. The solicitor who speaks to a witness during cross-examination - even on a matter they consider unrelated - without the cross-examiner's consent is in breach of this rule.

The narrow exceptions are: communications to instruct the witness on a genuine procedural matter such as an upcoming settlement offer (which would moot the need for continued cross-examination), or where the court specifically grants leave for communication on a defined subject. Even where an exception applies, the solicitor must immediately notify the cross-examiner of the substance of any communication.

4. Communication with the Court: No Informal Familiarity

ASCR Rule 18.1 provides that a solicitor must not deal with a court on terms of informal personal familiarity in the presence of other parties or their lawyers. This rule recognises a practical reality of legal practice: in smaller legal communities, judges and solicitors know each other personally. They may have been law school classmates, former colleagues, or personal friends. None of this changes the obligations in the courtroom.

In court, and in any interaction with the court that is conducted in the presence of the other side, the relationship must be scrupulously professional. The judge is "Your Honour." The tone is formal. The substance is about the legal merits of the submissions before the court. A solicitor who relies on personal familiarity with a judge - referring to their personal relationship, invoking social connections, or adopting an informality that is unavailable to a solicitor without such a relationship - is breaching Rule 18.1 and potentially undermining the other party's confidence that they are being heard by an impartial adjudicator.

5. Ex Parte Communications: ASCR Rule 22.5

ASCR Rule 22.5 is more absolute: a solicitor must not communicate with the court about any substantive matter relating to a case without the knowledge and consent of the solicitor for the opposing party. "Ex parte" means "without the other side" - and except in defined circumstances, ex parte communication with the court is prohibited.

The prohibition extends to emails and letters to the court, as well as oral submissions. A solicitor who sends a letter to the judge's associate outlining their client's position without copying the opponent's solicitor has breached Rule 22.5. The concern is obvious: if one party can communicate with the court without the other's knowledge, the opposing party cannot respond, correct errors, or ensure the court is receiving a complete picture.

The recognised exceptions are:

  • Ex parte applications - applications where, by their nature, notifying the other side would defeat the purpose of the application. An urgent injunction preventing a defendant from removing assets from the jurisdiction (a "freezing order") must be sought ex parte because advance notice would allow the defendant to remove the assets before the order is made. The court grants leave for these applications, and the solicitor must make full disclosure of all material facts - including facts that would favour the opposing party.
  • Court-initiated contact - if the court contacts the solicitor (through its registry or associate) and requires a response, the solicitor may respond without breach, provided they promptly notify the opponent of the communication and their response.
  • Opponent's express consent - if the opposing solicitor consents to a direct communication with the court, the prohibition is lifted.

R v Turner [1970] established that even in criminal proceedings, private communications between counsel and the judge are heavily restricted. Justice must be seen to be done in open court, and private arrangements between counsel and the bench - even if well-intentioned - undermine that principle.

6. The Duty to Advise on ADR: ASCR Rule 7.2

The duty to the court includes a positive obligation that is sometimes overlooked: a solicitor must advise clients about the availability of alternatives to fully contested court proceedings. ASCR Rule 7.2 provides that a solicitor must inform the client of any reasonably available alternatives to commencing proceedings, or to continuing proceedings, where such alternatives are likely to be appropriate.

This obligation reflects the court's own interest in the efficient resolution of disputes. Court time is a public resource. A client who proceeds to trial when a mediated settlement was available has incurred unnecessary cost and consumed scarce judicial resources. The solicitor's duty to the court includes a duty to discourage unnecessary use of it. This is not merely a professional responsibility requirement - it reflects a broader commitment to proportionality in dispute resolution that runs through the Civil Procedure Act 2005 (NSW).

7. Errors by the Opposing Party

An interesting and practically important question arises when the opposing party or their solicitor makes an error in a court document - for example, an error in a writ, a miscalculation of a sum claimed, or a misstatement of law. Is the solicitor obliged to correct it?

The general answer under Rule 19.3 is no - a solicitor is not automatically obliged to alert the other side to their error. The adversarial system operates on the assumption that each party advances their own case and identifies the weaknesses in the other's. But there is a critical limitation: a solicitor must not actively exploit the error in a way that constitutes dishonest conduct.

In Chamberlain v Law Society of the ACT, the ATO issued a writ against Chamberlain for $25,000 instead of $250,000. Chamberlain - himself a lawyer - identified the error and exploited it to extinguish a much larger tax debt. He was found guilty of professional misconduct. The distinction is between passively permitting an opponent's error to stand (which may be permissible) and actively exploiting it in a way that is dishonest - which is not.

8. Conclusion

The duties to the court are not adversarial opponents to the duty to the client - they are the framework within which effective advocacy is conducted. A court that cannot trust what solicitors tell it, that cannot be confident witnesses have not been coached, and that cannot ensure both parties are hearing what the other says is not a court that can deliver just outcomes. The solicitor's duty as an officer of the court is the structural guarantor of those conditions. Understanding it - and the specific rules that express it - is foundational to practising law with integrity.

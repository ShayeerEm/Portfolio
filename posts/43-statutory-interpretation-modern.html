The Modern Approach to Statutory Interpretation: Text, Context, and Purpose

SE
Shayeer Emanee
June 2026
8 min read
Foundations of Law Case Analysis Australian Law
§

Statutory interpretation is now one of the most important practical skills in Australian legal practice. Parliament produces enormous quantities of legislation, and that legislation is the source of most of the legal rights, obligations, and powers that govern everyday life - from tax to tenancy, employment to environmental protection, migration to marriage. Working out what a statutory provision means, and how it applies to a specific set of facts, is the daily work of most Australian lawyers.

The "modern approach" to statutory interpretation - developed through a series of High Court decisions and now reinforced by statute - requires courts to consider text, context, and purpose together from the very beginning of the interpretive exercise. This post examines that approach and the reasoning behind it.

1. Why Interpretation Is Necessary

Parliament drafts legislation in advance, using general language designed to cover a wide range of situations. But language is inherently imprecise. Words have multiple possible meanings. New technologies and social arrangements arise that Parliament did not and could not have anticipated. Old laws must be applied to new circumstances. The result is that the application of legislation to specific facts is rarely automatic - it requires a judgment about meaning.

Additionally, even carefully drafted legislation may contain genuine ambiguities - situations where the text, read in isolation, is consistent with two or more different meanings. The court must choose between them, and the choice should be principled rather than arbitrary. Statutory interpretation is the discipline that provides the principles.

2. The Traditional Approach vs the Modern Approach

Under the older, more formalistic approach to statutory interpretation, courts were expected to apply the "literal rule" - begin with the plain meaning of the words, and apply it, regardless of the consequences, unless it produced manifest absurdity. Context and purpose were secondary considerations, brought in only to resolve genuine ambiguity. This approach was consistent with the formalist judicial philosophy: the legislature's words are the law; the court's job is to apply them, not to second-guess Parliament.

The modern approach, decisively established in the High Court's decisions in CIC Insurance v Hannover Insurance (1994) and Project Blue Sky v Australian Broadcasting Authority [1998] HCA 28, rejects this sequential model. It holds that text, context, and purpose must all be considered together from the outset of the interpretive exercise - not one after another in a sequence that only reaches context and purpose when the text is already found to be ambiguous.

3. The Three Elements: Text, Context, Purpose

3.1 Text: The Starting Point

The text of the provision is always the starting point. The court reads the words of the statute and asks what they ordinarily mean - what would a reasonable person, reading this language, understand it to say? This is the plain, ordinary meaning of the words, unless the statute itself defines the term differently (in which case, the statutory definition prevails over ordinary usage).

Where the statute is aimed at a technical or specialist field, the relevant meaning may be the technical meaning - the meaning the words carry for specialists in that field - rather than the lay meaning. A statute governing medical procedures uses medical terms in a medical sense; a statute governing financial instruments uses financial terminology in a financial sense.

The text remains paramount even under the modern approach. Extrinsic materials - such as explanatory memoranda and Hansard - cannot be used to substitute for the actual words of the statute. As the High Court held in Re Bolton; Ex parte Beane (1987), if the words of the statute are clear, they prevail even if parliamentary history suggests a different intention. The court's task is to give effect to the statute as written - not to replace it with what some member of Parliament said about it.

3.2 Context: The Statute in Its Setting

Under the modern approach, context is not a fallback - it is relevant from the very beginning. The critical insight of CIC Insurance and Project Blue Sky is that words do not have meaning in isolation; they have meaning in context. The same word can mean very different things depending on where it appears and what surrounds it.

Context operates at two levels:

Intrinsic context - sources within the statute itself. These include the Act read as a whole; the long title and preamble (which describe the Act's general purpose); the objects clause (which states the Act's purposes explicitly); the headings of Parts, Divisions, and sections; and other related provisions within the Act. If a term in one section is clarified by a definition in another section, or limited by a provision elsewhere in the Act, the court will consider these internal sources from the beginning.

Extrinsic context - sources outside the statute. Under s 15AB of the Acts Interpretation Act 1901 (Cth) (for Commonwealth statutes) and s 33 of the Interpretation Act 1987 (NSW) (for NSW statutes), courts may have regard to extrinsic material. These sources include explanatory memoranda prepared for the Bill, the Minister's Second Reading Speech when introducing the Bill to Parliament (recorded in Hansard), reports of law reform commissions or parliamentary committees that led to the legislation, and (where the statute implements a treaty) the relevant international instrument.

The distinction between confirming and determining the meaning of a provision affects when extrinsic material may be used. Under s 15AB, extrinsic material can always be used to confirm the ordinary meaning of the text. But it can only be used to actually determine the meaning where the provision is ambiguous, or where the ordinary meaning would lead to a result that is manifestly absurd or unreasonable. The common law approach, established in CIC Insurance, is broader: courts may look at extrinsic material as part of context from the very beginning of the exercise, without needing to find ambiguity first. This represents a significant expansion beyond the statutory gateway.

3.3 Purpose: Promoting the Object of the Legislation

Section 15AA of the Acts Interpretation Act 1901 (Cth) requires that the interpretation of Commonwealth legislation be preferred that best promotes the purpose or object of the Act. The NSW equivalent is s 33 of the Interpretation Act 1987 (NSW). These provisions codify and strengthen the purposive approach to interpretation.

The "purpose" of an Act is what Parliament was trying to achieve - the problem or mischief the legislation was designed to address, the outcome it was designed to produce. Where a provision admits two or more possible constructions, the court must choose the one that best serves the Act's purpose over one that defeats or undermines it.

In Kingston v Keprose Pty Ltd (1987), the NSW Court of Appeal held that where the grammatical meaning of a provision does not give effect to the purpose of the Act, the grammatical meaning must yield to a purposive construction. This is a strong statement: even a grammatically clear reading may be overridden if it contradicts the evident purpose of the legislation.

4. The "Always Speaking" Principle

A principle closely associated with purposive interpretation is that statutes are "always speaking" - they are to be interpreted according to the current meaning of their words at the time of interpretation, not the meaning those words had when the statute was originally enacted.

This principle allows old statutes to apply to new technologies, new social arrangements, and new circumstances that Parliament could not have anticipated. A statute that requires a "public performance licence" for entertainment was interpreted to include radio broadcasts when radio was invented - even though no one in Parliament when the statute was passed had radio in mind. The statute spoke to the present, using present-day meanings of its terms.

The "always speaking" principle is not unlimited: it does not allow courts to give statutory words meanings that they simply cannot bear. A word can only be extended to a new context if the new context falls within the semantic range of the word - it must be genuinely within what the word means, not merely analogous to it.

5. Text, Context, and Purpose Together

The modern approach does not tell the court to start with text, then move to context when text fails, then move to purpose when context fails. All three must be considered together. The text provides the words. The context illuminates their meaning in the statute's setting. The purpose identifies which of the available readings best serves the legislative design. A court that considers only one of these elements is not applying the modern approach.

This integrated approach also means that a plain textual meaning is not automatically the end of the inquiry. If the textual meaning, read in context and against the statute's purpose, produces an outcome that is inconsistent with the legislation's evident objectives - and particularly if it produces a result that is manifestly absurd or unreasonable - the court will prefer a construction that avoids that result, provided the words can bear that construction.

6. Conclusion

The modern approach to statutory interpretation is not a single rule but a structured framework - text, context, and purpose considered simultaneously from the very beginning. It represents the common law's recognition that statutory language does not speak for itself: it speaks in a context and for a purpose, and understanding what it says requires attending to both. For law students and practitioners, the discipline of statutory interpretation is not merely a set of rules to be memorised; it is a mode of legal reasoning that is foundational to almost every area of practice.

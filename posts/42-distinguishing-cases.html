Distinguishing Cases: How Courts Avoid Binding Precedents Without Overruling Them

SE
Shayeer Emanee
June 2026
7 min read
Foundations of Law Case Analysis Australian Law
§

The doctrine of precedent would be oppressively rigid if courts had no mechanism for avoiding the application of earlier decisions to materially different facts. Distinguishing provides that mechanism. It is the process by which a court - including one that is technically bound by an earlier decision - declines to apply that earlier decision on the basis that the facts of the current case are meaningfully different from the facts that generated the earlier ratio. Distinguishing is not an escape hatch from binding precedent; it is a principled argument about the scope of a legal rule.

1. What Distinguishing Is (and Is Not)

Distinguishing is the argument that a binding precedent does not apply to the current case because the material facts are different in a legally relevant way. The key word is "material." Not every factual difference between two cases justifies distinguishing. The difference must be one that actually matters to the legal question - one that affects whether the same rule should apply.

What distinguishing is not:

  • It is not a way of disagreeing with a binding decision. A lower court that disagrees with a High Court ratio cannot "distinguish" it on the basis of disagreement; it must follow it. Distinguishing requires genuine factual differences, not mere disagreement.
  • It is not available where the facts are materially the same. If the facts of Case B are substantively identical to Case A, and Case A is binding, the ratio must be applied. A court that purports to distinguish on insubstantial grounds is effectively refusing to follow binding authority - which is an appellate error.
  • It is not the same as overruling. Only a higher court can overrule a precedent. Distinguishing does not question the correctness of the earlier decision - it says the earlier decision simply does not reach the current facts.

2. The Mechanics: How to Distinguish a Case

To distinguish a binding case effectively, the argument must proceed in three steps:

  • Identify the material facts of the earlier case - specifically, which facts generated the ratio. What was it about the situation in that case that led the court to establish the principle it did?
  • Identify the different facts of the current case. What is genuinely different about the current situation?
  • Explain why the difference matters. Why does the factual difference mean that the reasoning in the earlier case does not apply here? This is the critical step - it is not enough to simply note that the facts are different; the distinction must be shown to be legally relevant.

The quality of a distinguishing argument is measured by the quality of step 3. Courts can always identify factual differences between two cases - no two cases are ever perfectly identical. What makes a distinction convincing is the explanation of why this particular difference, in this particular legal context, means that the earlier rule should not govern.

3. Identifying Material Facts

A material fact is a fact that actually affected the legal outcome of the earlier case. Not every fact mentioned in a judgment is material; judges often describe the context of a case in considerable detail, much of which has no bearing on the ratio. The skill of identifying material facts is the same skill as identifying the ratio itself - you are asking: what features of this situation led the court to conclude what it did?

Consider a hypothetical case in which a court held that a dog owner was liable in negligence because the dog had previously bitten a neighbour's child and the owner had not taken steps to restrain the dog. The material facts might include: (1) the dog's known propensity for aggression, (2) the owner's actual knowledge of that propensity, and (3) the owner's failure to take precautions. If a new case involves a dog that had no known aggressive history but bit someone unexpectedly, the material fact of "known propensity" is absent - and that difference may be sufficient to distinguish the earlier case.

4. Examples of Legitimate and Illegitimate Distinctions

The following examples illustrate the difference between a distinction that is legally relevant and one that is not.

Example 1: The Disclosure Case

Case A held that a vendor who knew of structural defects in a property was liable for failing to disclose them to the buyer. In the current case, the vendor was unaware of the defects, which were hidden behind plasterboard and not discoverable on reasonable inspection. Proposed distinction: the earlier case rested on the vendor's knowledge; here, no such knowledge existed.

Assessment: This is a legitimate distinction. The ratio in Case A was built on the combination of knowledge and non-disclosure. The current vendor does not share the material fact of knowledge. The legal rule (you cannot fail to disclose what you know) simply does not apply to a person who does not know.

Example 2: The Contract Formation Case

Case B held that a contract for the sale of goods was concluded when the seller's acceptance was posted, not when it was received. The current case involves acceptance communicated by email. Proposed distinction: Case B involved postal communications; this case involves instantaneous electronic communication.

Assessment: This is likely a legitimate distinction, and courts have in fact treated it as one. The postal acceptance rule (the "postal rule" in contract law) rested on specific considerations about the nature of postal communication - delay, loss in transit, the merchant's ability to rely on posting. These considerations do not apply to instantaneous electronic communication where delivery is effectively immediate. The factual difference is legally relevant.

Example 3: The Irrelevant Difference

Case C held that a negligent driver who failed to keep a lookout at a pedestrian crossing was liable for injuries caused. In the current case, the driver was also negligent in the same way. Proposed distinction: in Case C, the driver was 32 years old; in the current case, the driver is 45 years old.

Assessment: This is not a legitimate distinction. The age of the driver has no legal relevance to the liability analysis. It does not affect what duty of care was owed, whether there was a breach, or whether the breach caused the harm. An attempt to distinguish on this basis would be rejected.

5. Distinguishing vs Overruling vs Declining to Follow

Technique Who Can Use It What It Does Effect on Earlier Case
Distinguishing Any court, including courts bound by the earlier decision Limits the application of an earlier ratio to cases with the same material facts; the earlier case remains good law for those facts Earlier case not affected - still good law on its own facts
Overruling Higher courts only (court above the earlier court) Holds that the earlier decision was wrong and should no longer be followed Earlier case is no longer good law
Declining to follow (horizontal) Courts at the same level as the earlier court A court at the same level concludes the earlier decision is plainly wrong and declines to follow it Creates a conflict; earlier case is still available to be argued; requires resolution by a higher court
Not following (lower court, persuasive only) Courts not bound by the earlier decision A court that is not bound simply prefers a different approach No effect on the earlier case's authority in its own hierarchy

6. Strategic Use of Distinguishing in Practice

In litigation, distinguishing is one of the most important advocacy tools available to the lawyer facing an unfavourable precedent. Rather than conceding that the precedent controls and asking the court to overrule it - which only a higher court can do - the advocate argues that the precedent simply does not reach the current facts. This approach allows the court to apply a favourable outcome without having to confront the question of whether the binding authority was wrongly decided.

Effective distinguishing arguments are economical and precise. They identify the specific material fact that is absent in the current case, explain why that fact was essential to the earlier ratio, and demonstrate that without it, the ratio does not extend. Arguments that accumulate a long list of minor factual differences without establishing that any of them matters tend to fail.

7. Conclusion

Distinguishing is the common law's safety valve. It allows the doctrine of precedent to be both principled and flexible - requiring courts to follow established law while acknowledging that law is developed in cases, and that cases have facts that limit the scope of the rules they generate. The discipline of distinguishing - of asking not just "are these cases different?" but "are they different in a way that matters?" - is central to legal reasoning and to the intellectual rigour that the common law demands.

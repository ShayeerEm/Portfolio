The NSW Conveyancing Process: From Contract Preparation to Electronic Settlement

SE
Shayeer Emanee
June 2026
5 min read
Property Law Case Analysis Australian Law
🏛

Conveyancing is the legal process by which ownership of real property is transferred from one person to another. In New South Wales, the conveyancing process is governed principally by the Conveyancing Act 1919 (NSW), the Real Property Act 1900 (NSW) (which governs Torrens title land), and the Conveyancing (Sale of Land) Regulation 2022 (NSW). For legal assistants and paralegals working in a conveyancing or property practice, an understanding of each stage of the transaction - from the preparation of the contract to the completion of settlement - is essential.

1. The Contract for Sale of Land

In NSW, the sale of residential land (and most commercial land) is effected by a contract in the form prescribed by the Conveyancing (Sale of Land) Regulation 2022 (NSW). Under s 66W of the Conveyancing Act 1919 (NSW), a vendor of residential property is required to attach a number of mandatory disclosure documents to the contract before it is offered for sale. The vendor's solicitor (or licensed conveyancer) prepares the contract and annexes these documents.

Mandatory Vendor Disclosure Documents

  • Section 10.7 certificate (formerly s 149 certificate): a Planning Certificate issued by the local council disclosing the zoning, planning restrictions, and development conditions applicable to the land.
  • Title search: a copy of the current certificate of title or folio of the register identifying the registered proprietor, the current dealing, and any registered interests.
  • Drainage diagram: a diagram from Sydney Water (or equivalent) showing the location of sewerage and stormwater infrastructure on or adjacent to the land.
  • Crown land statements (if applicable): disclosing whether the property is burdened by Crown reservations or conditions.
  • Sewerage information: confirming connection to the sewerage network.

Failure to attach any mandatory disclosure document at the time of sale entitles the purchaser to rescind the contract within 14 days after exchange: s 66Y of the Conveyancing Act 1919 (NSW). This rescission right is a significant protection for purchasers and a significant risk for vendors who exchange without checking that the contract is properly constituted.

2. Exchange of Contracts

The Cooling-Off Period

For residential property sales, a purchaser has a statutory cooling-off period of five business days after exchange of contracts under s 66T of the Conveyancing Act 1919 (NSW). During the cooling-off period, the purchaser may rescind the contract by serving a notice on the vendor, with forfeiture of 0.25% of the purchase price. The cooling-off period does not apply where the property is sold at auction, or where the purchaser serves a certificate from a solicitor (a "s 66W certificate") waiving the cooling-off period.

In practice, most purchasers who engage solicitors prior to auction will have a pre-exchange review of the contract and will exchange with a waiver of the cooling-off period. The s 66W certificate waiver is used where the purchaser needs to demonstrate to the vendor that they are committed to the transaction - for example, in a competitive market where the vendor will not accept an exchange subject to a cooling-off period.

The Deposit

On exchange, the purchaser pays a deposit - typically 10% of the purchase price - which is held by the vendor's solicitor (or real estate agent) as stakeholder. Under s 55(2A) of the Conveyancing Act 1919 (NSW), the purchaser is entitled to a return of the deposit where the vendor defaults. A reduced deposit (of as little as 0.25% or 5%) may be negotiated in competitive markets where the vendor is prepared to accept a lower initial payment in exchange for an exchange prior to finance approval.

3. Pre-Settlement Steps

Between exchange and settlement, a number of steps are carried out to prepare for the transfer of title. These include: searches and enquiries by the purchaser (council rates, water rates, land tax, body corporate levies, LPI searches for easements, caveats, or writs); preparation and execution of the Transfer instrument (the document that effects the change of ownership on the Torrens register); requisitions on title (formal questions directed to the vendor about matters affecting the property); registration of mortgage documents (where the purchaser is using finance); and preparation for settlement.

4. Electronic Settlement: PEXA and the Conveyancing Reforms

Since 2019, all mainstream property transactions in NSW have been conducted through the Property Exchange Australia (PEXA) electronic settlement platform. Electronic conveyancing is mandated for most types of transactions under the Electronic Conveyancing National Law (NSW). PEXA enables simultaneous electronic lodgement of the Transfer instrument with NSW Land Registry Services and electronic disbursement of funds at the moment of settlement, eliminating the need for physical settlement at a lawyer's office or bank branch.

PEXA has transformed the mechanics of conveyancing but has not altered the substantive law. The vendor still passes title; the purchaser still acquires the benefit of prior covenants and restrictions; and the obligations at exchange (deposit, conditions, cooling-off) remain as before. What has changed is the execution - the critical moment of settlement is now a simultaneous digital event that removes the risk of "same-time" title and funds exchange that attended paper settlements.

5. Title and the Torrens System

NSW is a Torrens title jurisdiction. Under the Real Property Act 1900 (NSW), the register is conclusive as to the identity of the registered proprietor and the interests registered against the land. The principle of indefeasibility - confirmed in Frazer v Walker [1967] 1 AC 569 - means that a registered proprietor's title cannot be defeated by a prior unregistered interest, except in cases of fraud. A purchaser who registers their Transfer obtains indefeasible title, protected against the claims of prior unregistered encumbrances.

The practical importance of this is that a purchaser's solicitor must ensure that the Transfer is lodged at settlement simultaneously with the vendor's discharge of mortgage. Any delay in lodgement creates a "registration gap" during which the purchaser's title is not yet indefeasible. PEXA's simultaneous lodgement model has substantially eliminated this risk for electronic settlements.

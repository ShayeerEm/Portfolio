Luchetti v R [2025] NSWCCA 222: When a Lighter is Not a Knife — Rethinking Guideline Judgments in Armed Robbery Sentencing

SE
Shayeer Emanee
17 December 2025
9 min read
Criminal Law Sentencing NSW Court of Criminal Appeal Armed Robbery Guideline Judgments
⚡

What happens when a sentencing judge compares an offence to the wrong benchmark? In Luchetti v R [2025] NSWCCA 222, the NSW Court of Criminal Appeal confronted precisely this question. A man used a cigarette lighter — brandished like a firearm — to deter a store employee while shoplifting a bottle of whiskey. The primary judge sentenced him to four years' imprisonment by reference to the landmark R v Henry guideline judgment, a case built around knife-wielding armed robbers and the ever-present threat of serious physical harm. The Court of Criminal Appeal said: that comparison was not open. This is its case note.

Citation

The medium neutral citation for this case is Luchetti v R [2025] NSWCCA 222.

The Court and the Judges

This appeal was heard in the Court of Criminal Appeal within the Supreme Court of New South Wales before Mitchelmore JA, Kirk JA, and Rigg J. All three judges agreed, with the reasons delivered by Rigg J.

Procedural History

This case is an appeal to the NSW Court of Criminal Appeal from a sentencing decision made in the District Court of New South Wales. At first instance, the appellant entered an early guilty plea to the offence of robbery whilst armed with an offensive weapon, contrary to s 97(1) of the Crimes Act 1900 (NSW). The primary judge, Hock DCJ, applied a 25% discount for the early plea and sentenced the appellant to a total term of four years' imprisonment, with a non-parole period of two years and three months. Following this decision, the appellant sought leave to appeal the severity and formulation of the sentence pursuant to s 5(1)(c) of the Criminal Appeal Act 1912 (NSW).

Relevant Facts

On 11 November 2023, the appellant entered a Liquorland store, placed a bottle of whiskey valued at $97 into his bag, and proceeded towards the exit. The victim, a store employee, approached the appellant and asked if he intended to pay for the item. In response, the appellant raised his right hand towards the victim and stated, "Don't come near me." The victim observed a black object in the appellant's hand, which he initially thought was shaped like a small gun, though he quickly realised it was not a real firearm. The object was, in fact, a cigarette lighter. The victim felt fearful and stopped approaching, allowing the appellant to leave the store. The appellant was subsequently arrested six days later on 17 November 2023.

At the time of the offending, the 38-year-old appellant was suffering from chronic, treatment-resistant schizophrenia, auditory hallucinations, and a substance use disorder. He had been released from custody to parole only a month before the offending, was hospitalised shortly after release, and described himself as homeless at the time. The psychiatric evidence established a close correlation — though not a direct causal link — between his substance use, his psychotic symptoms, and his offending behaviour.

Legal Issues

There were three main issues in contention on appeal:

  1. Did the sentencing judge err in assessing the objective seriousness of the offence by: (i) erroneously applying the R v Henry guideline judgment to the facts; (ii) failing to disclose her assessment of objective seriousness; and/or (iii) erring in fact regarding the nature of the weapon?
  2. Did the sentencing judge err by failing to consider whether the appellant's mental health conditions would make a full-time custodial sentence more onerous?
  3. Was the sentence imposed by the primary judge manifestly excessive?

Reasons for the Decision

Ground 1: Erroneous Application of the Henry Guideline

The Court found that the primary sentencing judge made a material error when assessing the objective seriousness of the offence by comparing it to the R v Henry guideline judgment without adequately accounting for the significant factual differences between that "frequently recurring case" and the circumstances before the Court.

The Court reasoned that the weapon used by the appellant — a lighter — was profoundly different in its capacity to inflict physical harm and engender fear when compared to the highly dangerous weapons, such as knives, contemplated in the Henry guideline. The Court surveyed the original Henry proceedings and associated cases in striking detail: Mr Henry himself had held a filleting knife to the skin of a console operator and wielded a long-bladed carving knife at a shop assistant; Mr Jenkins produced a "boning knife" or "nasty looking weapon" to the ribcage of his victim; and in associated proceedings, the use of a syringe filled with red liquid was described as falling "right at the outside limits of seriousness of the kind of weapon the guideline contemplates."

Against that backdrop, a cigarette lighter — capable of producing a small blue flame — was in a categorically different league. As Rigg J observed:

"The weapon and the way it was used was neither capable of killing or causing grievous bodily harm, nor any more frightening than necessary to make out the elements of the offence."
— Rigg J, Luchetti v R [2025] NSWCCA 222 at [47]

The Court noted that the level of threatened violence was exceptionally low — amounting to no more than what was strictly necessary to satisfy the basic legal elements of an armed robbery charge, falling far short of the "real threat" of actual violence described in the guideline. Because the circumstances of the appellant's case were so vastly different from the frequently recurring case outlined in Henry, the guideline offered no actual utility for this specific sentencing exercise.

Consequently, the primary judge's failure to explicitly explain how much less objectively serious the appellant's conduct was compared to the guideline constituted a material legal error. Having raised a comparison with Henry, it was necessary to explain the significant distance between the two scenarios — particularly in relation to the nature of the weapon.

Ground 1(i): Subjective Factors in Objective Assessment

The Court did not accept that the sentencing judge had incorporated subjective factors — the appellant's age and criminal antecedents — into her assessment of objective seriousness. The Court distinguished this case from Towers v R [2025] NSWCCA 142, where a sentencing judge had expressly listed such factors under the heading "Objective Gravity." Here, a fair reading of the judgment did not support that inference. Ground 1(i) was rejected.

Ground 1(ii): Failure to Disclose the Assessment

The Court declined to formally resolve ground 1(ii), given that the error in ground 1(iii) was sufficient to warrant re-sentencing. It noted, however, that comparison with a guideline judgment — particularly an extended consideration of the core criteria — is not inherently inadequate as a recognition of objective seriousness. The problem was that the comparison was factually unsustainable, not that no assessment had been made.

Ratio

The guideline judgment of R v Henry is not universally prescriptive for all armed robbery sentencing proceedings. It is a material error for a sentencing judge to assess objective seriousness by comparing an offence to this guideline without explicitly articulating when the nature of the weapon and the level of threatened violence fall substantially below the standards contemplated by that guideline. Where the circumstances of an offence are so factually removed from the frequently recurring case considered in the guideline, the guideline has no role to perform in the sentencing exercise.

Obiter Dicta

The Court's comments regarding the second ground of appeal — concerning the onerousness of custody due to the appellant's mental illness — were made in obiter dicta, as the success of the first ground meant this issue did not strictly require formal resolution.

The Court observed that, had a determination been necessary, it would have dismissed the ground. The psychiatric evidence demonstrated the appellant was institutionalised and actually felt more frightened in the community than in custody. It was therefore open to the primary judge — and the appellant's own representative at first instance — to focus on the appellant's preference for incarceration as the salient manifestation of his mental illness relevant to the sentencing exercise, rather than treating mental illness as a factor that made custody substantially more burdensome than it would be for other inmates.

The Decision of the Court

The Court held that the first ground of appeal was successfully made out, establishing that the primary sentencing judge made a material error in her assessment of the objective seriousness of the offence. Having established this error, the Court determined that the original sentence must be quashed and the appellant re-sentenced, which rendered the remaining two grounds of appeal unnecessary to resolve formally.

On re-sentencing, the Court found that the appropriate starting point — prior to discount for the utilitarian value of the guilty plea — was 2 years' imprisonment. This reflected the Court's view that this was "objectively a very low-level offence of armed robbery, committed by a man whose moral culpability for it was greatly reduced, and who was not a suitable vehicle for general deterrence." After applying the 25% discount, the sentence was reduced to 18 months' imprisonment.

Crucially, by the time the Court made its orders on 8 October 2025, the appellant had already spent close to two years in custody. The Court held that this pre-existing time in custody had "by a significant extent already completely addressed all the punitive, deterrent and denunciatory purposes of sentencing." Accordingly, there was no basis for any further punishment.

The Court's Orders

The Court formally granted leave to appeal and allowed the appeal. The Court ordered that the sentence of imprisonment imposed in the District Court on 7 November 2024 be set aside. In lieu thereof, the Court re-sentenced the appellant to:

  • A term of imprisonment of 18 months, commencing on 18 December 2023 and expiring on 17 June 2025; and
  • A non-parole period of 10 months' imprisonment, commencing on 18 December 2023 and expiring on 17 October 2024.

As these dates had already passed, Mr Luchetti was entitled to be released immediately.

Domestic Source: The Definition of "Offensive Weapon"

The Court cited s 4 of the Crimes Act 1900 (NSW) when evaluating the precise legal nature of the weapon used during the robbery. That provision defines "offensive weapon or instrument" to include:

"(c) any thing that, in the circumstances, is used, intended for use or threatened to be used for offensive purposes, whether or not it is ordinarily used for offensive purposes or is capable of causing harm."
— Crimes Act 1900 (NSW) s 4

The Court applied this legislative definition to determine that, by holding up the lighter and telling the store employee not to come near him, the appellant was legally using the lighter for an "offensive purpose" — satisfying the armed robbery charge. However, satisfying the legal definition of an offensive weapon did not mean the lighter's characteristics were comparable to those weapons contemplated in the Henry guideline. The legal and factual assessments are distinct. A thing may be an offensive weapon for the purposes of the offence while remaining profoundly less dangerous than the weapons that inform the guideline judgment.

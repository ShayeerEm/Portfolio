Judicial Philosophy in Action: Mabo, Al-Kateb, and the Stakes of the Debate

SE
Shayeer Emanee
June 2026
9 min read
Foundations of Law Case Analysis Australian Law
§

Judicial philosophy is not merely an academic exercise. The differences between formalism and activism, between applying the law and developing it, between deference to Parliament and independent judicial initiative, produce concrete and sometimes dramatic differences in outcomes for real people. Two cases from the High Court of Australia - Mabo v Queensland (No 2) [1992] HCA 23 and Al-Kateb v Godwin (2004) 219 CLR 562 - illustrate these differences with unusual clarity. In each case, the bench was divided, and the division tracked the philosophical fault lines almost perfectly.

Part I: Mabo v Queensland (No 2) [1992] HCA 23

1.1 Background

In 1982, Eddie Mabo and four other Murray Islanders brought an action in the Supreme Court of Queensland claiming ownership of land on the Murray Islands (Mer) in the Torres Strait, which they had occupied for generations under indigenous laws and customs. The legal obstacle was fundamental: Australian courts had long applied the common law doctrine of terra nullius, which held that Australia was legally unoccupied when British sovereignty was established, and that on settlement all lands became Crown land without any pre-existing property rights to recognise or extinguish.

Terra nullius was a fiction. The Murray Islands were inhabited - and had been for centuries - by people with complex systems of law, land tenure, and governance. The legal fiction ignored this reality because it was convenient for colonial administration and consistent with the prevailing 19th-century attitudes about the relative worth of European and non-European societies. By 1982, those attitudes had profoundly changed - but the doctrine remained.

The case eventually reached the High Court after years of litigation. By the time it was heard, Eddie Mabo had died. In 1992, the High Court delivered its landmark decision by a 6:1 majority: terra nullius was overturned, and the common law of Australia was held to recognise native title as a form of property right in land.

1.2 The Formalist Position: Dawson J (Dissent)

Justice Dawson, the sole dissenter, wrote a judgment that represents the formalist position in its purest form. Dawson J did not defend terra nullius as a matter of moral principle. He acknowledged, plainly and explicitly, that the doctrine reflected "a discriminatory denigration of Aboriginal peoples and their rights." He was not arguing that the legal outcome was just - he was arguing that it was not the court's role to change it.

Dawson J's position was this: terra nullius had been the settled law of Australia since the beginning of European settlement. It had been applied, relied upon, and built upon for two centuries. A change in the moral values of the community - however genuine and however welcome - did not automatically mean a change in the law. The law is not identical to prevailing moral consensus. If terra nullius was to be replaced, that was work for Parliament through legislation. Courts were the wrong institution to take on this reform.

The three hallmarks of formalism are all present in Dawson J's reasoning: deference to established rules regardless of their justice, respect for the legislature's primary role in law reform, and resistance to judicial law-making even in the face of clear social consensus for change.

1.3 The Activist/Realist Position: Brennan J (Majority)

Justice Brennan wrote the leading majority judgment. His reasoning was explicitly evaluative about the relationship between law and justice, and explicitly activist in its conclusion about the court's appropriate role.

Brennan J held that the common law could and should develop to reflect the "contemporary values of the Australian people." Terra nullius was not merely wrong - it was so fundamentally inconsistent with the values of a modern democratic society, and with the international legal standards that Australia had endorsed, that the common law had a positive obligation to reject it. For Brennan J, the common law was not a static set of rules handed down from the past; it was a living system that developed by being applied by courts across generations.

He concluded:

The common law does not necessarily conform with international law, but international law is a legitimate and important influence on the development of the common law, especially when international law declares the existence of universal human rights.

The activist elements are unmistakable: the court is using its power to develop the common law to achieve a just outcome, drawing on international human rights standards as a source for that development, and expressly grounding the decision in changed social values rather than in the application of pre-existing legal rules.

1.4 The Significance

Mabo is the most significant Australian case of the 20th century. Its legal consequences were profound: Parliament enacted the Native Title Act 1993 (Cth) to manage the recognition and extinguishment of native title across Australia. Its philosophical significance is equally profound: it demonstrated that the High Court could, in an appropriate case, overturn a foundational common law doctrine that had shaped Australian law since 1788. The decision drew its authority not from established legal rules but from a judicial judgment about what the law, rightly understood, required in a changed world.

Part II: Al-Kateb v Godwin (2004) 219 CLR 562

2.1 Background

Ahmad Al-Kateb was a stateless Palestinian man who arrived in Australia by boat in 2000 seeking asylum. His application for a protection visa was refused and his application for a bridging visa was rejected. Under the Migration Act 1958 (Cth) as it then stood, a person whose visa had been refused was to be detained until they were removed from Australia. Al-Kateb was stateless - no country would accept him. He could not be deported to anywhere. The consequence, if the Act was read literally, was that he could be detained indefinitely - potentially for the rest of his life - with no prospect of release.

Al-Kateb brought proceedings challenging the lawfulness of his continued detention. The High Court decided the matter by a 4:3 majority.

2.2 The Formalist Majority

The majority (McHugh, Hayne, Callinan and Heydon JJ) applied a strict literalist approach. The words of the Migration Act were clear: detention was to continue until removal was effected. The impossibility of removal did not, on the face of the Act's text, create any exception to the detention obligation. Parliament had not included such an exception. Courts should not read one in.

McHugh J, who delivered the leading majority judgment, was candid about the moral dimension of the outcome. He acknowledged that the result - indefinite detention of a person who had committed no crime and who could not be deported - was deeply troubling. But he held that it was not the court's role to remedy that injustice through interpretation. The remedy lay with Parliament: "If the result is unjust, Parliament is able to remedy it."

The formalist methodology is evident: the text is paramount; there is a correct answer that the text provides; the court's job is to find and apply that answer regardless of the consequences.

2.3 The Activist/Principle of Legality Minority

The minority - Gleeson CJ, Gummow and Kirby JJ - reached the opposite conclusion, though by different routes. Gleeson CJ applied the principle of legality: the right to personal liberty is so fundamental that Parliament cannot be presumed to have authorised its indefinite deprivation without clear and specific language. The Migration Act's general detention provisions did not constitute that clear and specific authorisation for indefinite detention.

Kirby J was the most explicitly activist of the minority. He drew on international human rights law, the principle of legality, and what he described as the fundamental values of a constitutional democracy committed to the rule of law. He argued that Australian law had always required that detention be for a defined and lawful purpose - and that detention without any prospect of the purpose (removal) being achieved could not be authorised by general language alone.

Both minority approaches represent the activist/purposive end of the interpretive spectrum: a willingness to use interpretive tools - the principle of legality, purposive construction, international law - to avoid an outcome that the court regards as fundamentally inconsistent with the rule of law, even where the literal text might support that outcome.

2.4 The Reversal: NZYQ (2023)

In NZYQ v Minister for Immigration (2023), the High Court revisited the question decided in Al-Kateb. A differently constituted bench - and a changed legal landscape, including significant development in constitutional jurisprudence around the Chapter III separation of judicial power - led the Court to a different conclusion. The High Court in NZYQ held that indefinite immigration detention of a person who cannot be deported to any country is unlawful. The Al-Kateb decision was overruled.

The NZYQ decision is significant for the study of judicial philosophy in two respects. First, it demonstrates that the High Court will overrule its own prior decisions where it is satisfied they were wrong - even controversial and contested decisions from only nineteen years earlier. Second, it vindicates the minority position in Al-Kateb, which had been sustained and developed in subsequent scholarship and litigation over almost two decades. The arc of the law moved toward the minority's understanding of what the Constitution and the common law required.

Part III: What These Cases Teach About Judicial Philosophy

Taken together, Mabo and Al-Kateb illustrate several important propositions about judicial philosophy:

  • The same judge can acknowledge the injustice of an outcome and still apply it: both Dawson J in Mabo and McHugh J in Al-Kateb were explicit that the legal outcome they were reaching was morally troubling. Formalism does not require judges to be indifferent to justice - it requires them to treat the legislature as the appropriate institution for changing unjust law.
  • Activism does not mean ignoring the law: the majority in Mabo and the minority in Al-Kateb were not inventing outcomes divorced from legal principle. They were using legitimate legal tools - the principle of legality, the power to develop the common law, the relevance of international human rights standards - to reach outcomes they regarded as legally better justified.
  • The philosophies produce different outcomes for real people: Al-Kateb remained in detention; NZYQ was released. Mabo's descendants received recognition of property rights that had been denied for two centuries. The stakes in these philosophical debates are not academic.
  • History tends to vindicate the more protective position: in both cases, the position that offered greater protection to the individual - the minority in Mabo (which became the majority) and the minority in Al-Kateb (which was vindicated in NZYQ) - was eventually accepted as the correct one. This does not prove that judicial activism is always right, but it suggests that the arc of legal development in Australia has tended toward expanding individual rights rather than contracting them.

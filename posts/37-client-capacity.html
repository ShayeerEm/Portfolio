Client Capacity in Legal Practice: Representing Children and Adults Who Lose Capacity

SE
Shayeer Emanee
June 2026
8 min read
Professional Responsibility Case Analysis Australian Law
📋

The lawyer-client relationship is built on the assumption that the client can give instructions, understand advice, and make decisions about their own legal position. When that assumption is disrupted - because the client is a child who lacks full legal capacity, or because an adult client has lost capacity through illness, injury, or age - the entire framework of the retainer must be adjusted. How a solicitor navigates capacity questions is one of the most practically complex and ethically sensitive areas of legal professional responsibility.

Part I: Children as Clients

1.1 The Legal Position

Children generally lack the legal capacity to enter binding contracts or give enforceable legal instructions. This is the general rule of contract law: a person under 18 is a minor, and contracts entered by minors are voidable at the minor's election (with narrow exceptions for contracts for necessaries). A retainer entered by a child without an appropriate adult or guardian is therefore potentially unenforceable.

However, children do encounter the legal system in a variety of contexts - as parties to Family Court proceedings about their own living arrangements, as witnesses or complainants in criminal proceedings, in Children's Court matters, in personal injury claims, and in care and protection proceedings. Each context requires a tailored approach to how the solicitor engages with the child.

1.2 Three Models of Representation

When a solicitor represents or otherwise acts in relation to a child's interests, three models exist, and the appropriate model depends on the child's age, maturity, and the nature of the proceedings:

Model Description When Appropriate
Direct representation The solicitor treats the child as a client in the ordinary sense - takes instructions directly from the child and advocates for what the child wants Older, more mature children (typically over 14) who have sufficient understanding of the proceedings and their implications to give genuine instructions; most appropriate in criminal or care proceedings where the child has a clear, stable position
Best interests representation The solicitor advocates for what is objectively best for the child, even where this differs from what the child actually wants Younger children, or proceedings (particularly Family Court parenting matters) where the child's expressed wishes may not reflect their true interests; the Independent Children's Lawyer (ICL) role in family law proceedings operates on this model
Guardian ad litem / Tutor A guardian or tutor is appointed by the court to make legal decisions on behalf of the child; the solicitor takes instructions from the guardian, not the child Children who cannot instruct at all due to very young age or cognitive limitations; complex multi-party proceedings where a conflict of interest would arise if a parent gave instructions

1.3 Practical Principles When Representing Children

Across all models of representation, a set of consistent practical principles applies when a solicitor is working with a child:

  • Assess capacity individually, not by age alone. Capacity is a spectrum. A mature 13-year-old may be more capable of giving meaningful instructions in a care proceeding than a developmentally delayed 16-year-old. Age provides only a rough guide.
  • Use age-appropriate language. A 9-year-old does not understand what "affidavit," "interim orders," or "legal representative" means. Communication must be calibrated to the child's level of comprehension.
  • Meet in person where possible. A face-to-face meeting, in a non-threatening environment, is vastly more effective at building the rapport necessary to understand the child's position than any questionnaire or phone call.
  • Apply the same confidentiality obligations. Information disclosed to a solicitor by a child client is subject to the same duty of confidentiality as information disclosed by any client. The child's interests - not those of the parent who is paying the fees - are paramount.
  • Watch for conflicts between siblings. Where two children from the same family are separately represented, or where a parent seeks to give instructions on behalf of multiple children with different interests, conflicts of interest may arise quickly.
  • Ensure the child's voice reaches the court. Regardless of the model of representation, the court is entitled to hear what the child's position is. Even in best-interests representation, the child's expressed views are a relevant factor (s 60CC(3)(a) of the Family Law Act 1975 (Cth) explicitly requires the court to consider any views expressed by the child and the circumstances in which they were expressed).

Part II: Adults Who Lose Capacity

2.1 The Problem

A person who enters a retainer with full legal capacity may lose that capacity during the engagement. A client with early-stage dementia may progressively lose the ability to understand their legal situation. A client who suffers a traumatic brain injury in an accident may lose capacity suddenly and completely. A client experiencing a serious mental health episode may temporarily lose the ability to give coherent instructions.

Capacity is not binary - it operates on a spectrum and can fluctuate over time. A person with dementia may have capacity to give instructions on a simple matter of their choosing while lacking capacity to understand a complex commercial transaction. The solicitor who encounters a client whose capacity is uncertain has a professional obligation to address the question directly rather than continuing to take instructions as if the problem does not exist.

2.2 Substitute Decision-Makers

When a client loses capacity, the solicitor can no longer take instructions directly from them. A substitute decision-maker must be identified - a person authorised by law to make decisions on the client's behalf.

In NSW, the relevant framework is established by the Guardianship Act 1987 (NSW) and the relevant provisions of the NSW Trustee and Guardian Act 2009 (NSW). Three categories of substitute decision-maker are relevant:

Role Appointment Scope of Authority
Guardian Appointed by the NSW Civil and Administrative Tribunal (NCAT, Guardianship Division) or the Supreme Court Decisions about lifestyle, accommodation, and medical treatment - NOT financial or legal decisions
Financial Manager Appointed by NCAT or the Supreme Court Decisions about property, finances, and legal matters on behalf of the person who has lost capacity
Guardian Ad Litem Appointed by the court specifically for one set of legal proceedings Gives instructions to the solicitor for the purposes of the specific proceedings; authority is limited to the matter before the court

It is important to note that an Enduring Power of Attorney - a document executed by a person while they have capacity, appointing someone to manage their financial affairs if they later lose capacity - is distinct from a court-appointed financial manager. An enduring attorney may give instructions to a solicitor on a client's financial matters after the client loses capacity, provided the power of attorney covers legal matters. Solicitors must inspect the power of attorney document carefully, as different enduring powers have different scopes.

2.3 What the Solicitor Must Do When Capacity Is in Question

Where a solicitor has concerns about a client's capacity, the appropriate steps are:

  • Stop taking instructions on significant legal matters until the capacity question is resolved. It is impermissible to continue taking instructions that may bind a client who cannot properly understand what they are authorising.
  • Raise the concern with the client directly (where this is possible without causing distress or prejudice). The client may be able to address the concern - for example, by demonstrating understanding of the matter, or by consenting to a medical assessment.
  • Obtain a formal capacity assessment where necessary. In complex matters, or where there is genuine uncertainty, a formal assessment by a medical or psychiatric specialist may be required.
  • Identify whether a substitute decision-maker already exists - an enduring power of attorney, an existing NCAT order, or a court-appointed guardian.
  • If no substitute decision-maker exists and one is required, the solicitor may need to initiate applications to NCAT for the appointment of a guardian or financial manager, or advise the client's family members of the process for doing so.

2.4 The Ethical Tensions

Capacity questions generate genuine ethical tension. A solicitor who too readily concludes that a client lacks capacity - and proceeds on the basis of instructions from a substitute - risks overriding the client's autonomy. People with fluctuating capacity have a right to participate in decisions about their own lives to the extent they are able. A solicitor who treats an elderly client with early dementia as incapable of giving any instructions is patronising and may be wrong.

On the other hand, a solicitor who continues to take instructions from a client who genuinely lacks capacity, on the grounds that this is what the client wants, risks participating in legal transactions that the client cannot validly consent to - transactions that may be later challenged and set aside, with costs consequences for everyone involved.

The resolution lies in careful, individualised assessment. Capacity is decision-specific: a person may have capacity to make some decisions (where to live, what medical treatment to accept) but not others (how to invest their superannuation, whether to initiate litigation). The solicitor who approaches capacity questions with that specificity, and who documents their assessment and reasoning carefully, will be better placed professionally than one who either reflexively accepts all instructions or reflexively refuses to take any.

3. Conclusion

Capacity is one of the most human dimensions of legal practice. It asks lawyers to recognise that the people they serve are not always the fully autonomous rational actors that contract law assumes. Children are still developing the understanding and judgment that instruction-giving requires. Adults may lose the capacity they once had, slowly or suddenly. Navigating these situations demands not only knowledge of the legal framework - the models of representation, the appointment of substitute decision-makers, the statutory provisions - but also sensitivity, patience, and a genuine commitment to serving the person whose interests are in play, not the convenience of the system.

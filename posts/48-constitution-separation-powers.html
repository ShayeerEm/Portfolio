The Australian Constitution and Separation of Powers: The Three Branches, Judicial Review, and the Washminster Mutation

SE
Shayeer Emanee
June 2026
8 min read
Foundations of Law Case Analysis Australian Law
§

The Australian Constitution is the foundational legal document of the Commonwealth of Australia. It came into force on 1 January 1901 when the six British colonies federated into a single nation. It is simultaneously the source of all Commonwealth legislative and executive power, the instrument that distributes that power between the three branches of government, and the guarantee of certain structural protections - most importantly, the strict separation of judicial power - that the High Court has interpreted as protecting individual rights even in the absence of an express Bill of Rights.

1. The Nature of the Australian Constitution

The Australian Constitution has two defining characteristics that distinguish it from some comparable instruments.

First, it is written and codified. The Constitution is a specific, identifiable document - the Commonwealth of Australia Constitution Act 1900 (Imp), enacted by the British Parliament and scheduled to it. Every aspect of the Commonwealth's structure and powers must be traced to, and must be consistent with, that document. Any law or action that exceeds the limits the Constitution imposes is invalid - ultra vires.

Second, it is rigid. Unlike the UK Parliament, which can amend its constitutional arrangements by ordinary legislation, the Australian Constitution can only be altered through the referendum mechanism in s 128. That mechanism requires a national majority vote AND a majority vote in at least four of the six states. This "double majority" requirement means that constitutional change requires a genuinely broad national consensus - amendments that might enjoy simple Parliamentary majorities can fail at referendum. In practice, only 8 of the 44 referendum proposals put to Australian voters since 1901 have succeeded.

2. The "Washminster Mutation"

Political scientist Elaine Thompson coined the phrase "Washminster Mutation" to capture the distinctive hybrid character of Australia's constitutional arrangements. The Constitution draws from two quite different constitutional traditions:

Element Source Description
Westminster parliamentary government United Kingdom The executive (Prime Minister and Cabinet) is drawn from and responsible to the legislature (Parliament). Ministers must be members of Parliament (s 64). The government of the day must maintain the confidence of the lower house.
Federal structure United States Division of power between a central (Commonwealth) government and constituent states, with a written Constitution defining the boundary between them.
Senate with equal state representation United States The Senate gives each state equal representation (12 senators per state, 2 per territory), regardless of population - like the US Senate.
Judicial review of legislation United States The High Court can strike down laws that exceed the constitutional limits - a power the US Supreme Court claimed in Marbury v Madison (1803) and that Australian courts have exercised since federation.

3. The Three Branches of Government

3.1 Chapter I: The Legislature

Chapter I of the Constitution establishes the Commonwealth Parliament, which consists of the King (represented by the Governor-General), the Senate, and the House of Representatives. The Parliament's legislative power derives from the enumerated heads of power in s 51, which lists the subject matters on which the Commonwealth may legislate - including trade and commerce, taxation, external affairs, marriage, corporations, currency, and defence.

The Senate provides equal representation for each state (currently 12 senators per state, and 2 senators each for the ACT and NT). The House of Representatives is apportioned by population, with larger states having more members. The practical consequence is a bicameral legislature in which the states, through the Senate, have a structural check on legislation that might disadvantage smaller states.

3.2 Chapter II: The Executive

Chapter II establishes the executive government, vested formally in the King as represented by the Governor-General, advised by the Federal Executive Council. In constitutional practice, the "Executive Council" is the formal mechanism through which executive decisions are made - but the Council meets with whoever is the acting government, and its decisions reflect the decisions of the Cabinet.

The key provision for understanding the relationship between the executive and legislature is s 64: Ministers of State must be members of Parliament (or become members within three months of appointment). This requirement embeds the principle of responsible government - the executive is drawn from, and accountable to, the legislature. It also means that the executive and legislature are not strictly separated in the way the US Constitution separates President and Congress. An Australian Prime Minister who loses a vote of no confidence in the House of Representatives must resign; a US President in the same position is constitutionally entitled to remain in office until their term expires.

3.3 Chapter III: The Judiciary

Chapter III establishes the federal judicial system. Section 71 vests judicial power in the High Court, other federal courts created by Parliament, and courts invested by Parliament with federal jurisdiction. Section 72 guarantees the independence of federal judges: they are appointed by the Governor-General in Council (effectively the Cabinet), hold office until age 70, and can only be removed from office by the Governor-General on an address of both Houses of Parliament.

The most significant feature of Chapter III is the strict separation of judicial power that the High Court has read into it. Unlike the relationship between the legislature and executive (which are partially fused through responsible government), judicial power is strictly separated from both. Only courts that comply with Chapter III can exercise federal judicial power; and Chapter III courts cannot be given non-judicial functions that compromise their independence and impartiality (the Boilermakers' Case principle).

4. The Separation of Powers: Three Arguments

The separation of powers doctrine - dividing government into legislative, executive, and judicial branches - rests on several distinct justifications that are worth articulating clearly.

4.1 Prevention of Tyranny

The classic argument, associated with Montesquieu and James Madison, is that concentrating all governmental power in one set of hands creates the conditions for tyranny. Madison wrote in The Federalist No. 47: "The accumulation of all powers, legislative, executive, and judiciary, in the same hands, whether of one, a few, or many, and whether hereditary, self-appointed, or elective, may justly be pronounced the very definition of tyranny."

Separation creates mutual checking: the legislature's laws are administered by the executive, which is accountable to the legislature; both are subject to the courts, which can declare their actions invalid. No single branch holds unchecked power over citizens.

4.2 Efficiency and Institutional Competence

Each branch performs a function for which it is institutionally suited. Legislatures are good at making policy - they can gather broad evidence, consult stakeholders, and represent diverse interests. Executives are good at administering policy - implementing laws through bureaucratic structures equipped for consistent application. Courts are good at resolving specific disputes - applying legal rules to particular facts through procedures designed to produce accurate and fair outcomes. Separation allocates functions to the branch best suited to perform them.

4.3 Arguments Against: Gridlock and Blurred Lines

The separation of powers also carries costs. Where the three branches are controlled by different political groupings, separation can produce gridlock - the inability to pass legislation or implement policy that has broad support. US government shutdowns - where Congress fails to pass appropriations legislation and federal agencies close - are the most visible example.

Critics also note that in practice the lines between the branches are never as clean as the theory suggests. Executive agencies make rules (legislative function), adjudicate disputes (judicial function), and implement policy (executive function) simultaneously. The separation of powers sets up an ideal that real government inevitably departs from.

5. Judicial Review in Australia

Judicial review is the power of courts to declare laws and executive actions invalid where they exceed the constitutional limits. In Australia, this power derives from the Constitution itself - the High Court's original jurisdiction under s 75 includes matters in which the Commonwealth is a party, and the Court's authority to determine whether Commonwealth legislation is constitutionally valid is implicit in the constitutional structure.

The principle was articulated for the common law world in Marbury v Madison (1803), where Chief Justice Marshall of the US Supreme Court stated: "It is emphatically the province and duty of the judicial department to say what the law is." Australian courts adopted this principle, confirmed most prominently in Australian Communist Party v The Commonwealth (1951) - the "Communist Party Case" - where the High Court unanimously struck down legislation purporting to dissolve the Australian Communist Party, holding that Parliament had no constitutional power to do so.

Judicial review does not exist in the United Kingdom. The UK operates under parliamentary sovereignty: Parliament can make or unmake any law, and courts cannot question its validity. Australia's written and rigid Constitution, by contrast, establishes limits on what Parliament can do - and gives courts the authority (and obligation) to enforce those limits.

6. The Three Lenses for Evaluating Judicial Decisions

When analysing the appropriateness of judicial decision-making - particularly in cases where courts are asked to extend or adapt legal principles - three evaluative lenses are relevant:

Lens Question Relevance
Institutional Competence Which branch of government is best placed to make this decision? Courts are good at applying principles to specific facts; legislatures are better at broad policy reform involving evidence-gathering and stakeholder consultation. Courts should be cautious about making decisions that require the latter.
Democratic Accountability Who is accountable to the people for this decision? Elected legislators are accountable to voters; appointed judges are not. But courts protect minorities that electoral majorities might ignore - the counter-majoritarian function of judicial review.
Representation and Diversity Does the decision-maker reflect the diversity of those affected? Courts function better when judges bring diverse life experiences. A bench that is demographically uniform may have blind spots about the experiences of those whose rights are in question.

7. Conclusion

Australia's Constitution is neither a purely Westminster document nor a purely American one. It is a carefully constructed hybrid that reflects the political compromises of federation and the institutional inheritance of both the British colonial tradition and American federalism. The three-branch structure, the Senate's role in protecting state interests, the strict separation of judicial power, and the power of judicial review together create a constitutional system that distributes and limits power in ways that have shaped Australian law and politics since 1901. For law students, understanding the Constitution is not merely a matter of knowing its structure - it is understanding why the structure exists and what values it is designed to protect.

Probate and Administration of Estates in NSW: Grants, Duties, and the Distribution Process

SE
Shayeer Emanee
June 2026
8 min read
Wills & Estates Case Analysis Australian Law
📜

When a person dies, their assets do not automatically pass to their beneficiaries. Before the estate can be distributed, a formal legal process must occur to establish the authority of the person responsible for administering it. In NSW, this process is governed by the Probate and Administration Act 1898 (NSW) (PAA), the Succession Act 2006 (NSW), and the Supreme Court (Probate) Rules 2015 (NSW). For paralegals in wills and estates practice, understanding the grant of representation - probate or letters of administration - is fundamental to almost every estates matter.

1. What is a Grant of Representation?

A grant of representation is an order of the Supreme Court of NSW authenticating the authority of a named person to administer the estate of a deceased. The grant serves two functions: it proves the executor's or administrator's entitlement to act, and it enables third parties (banks, Lands Registry Services, share registries) to safely transfer assets to the personal representative without personal liability.

There are two principal forms of grant:

Type of Grant When Applicable Granted To
Grant of Probate Deceased left a valid will appointing an executor who is willing and able to act The named executor(s) in the will
Letters of Administration with Will Annexed (CTA) Valid will exists but executor is deceased, renounced, or lacks capacity A person with entitlement under the will or intestacy
Letters of Administration (Intestate) Deceased died without a valid will Next of kin in order of priority under the PAA
Letters of Administration (de bonis non) Original administrator/executor has died before completing administration A person with residual interest in the estate

In common parlance, "probate" is often used loosely to refer to all grants of representation, but the technical distinction matters in practice: only an executor under a valid will may apply for probate, while administrators derive authority from the court's grant of letters of administration.

2. Applying for Probate

2.1 Who May Apply

An executor named in the last valid will is entitled to apply for probate. Where multiple executors are named, they may apply jointly or some may renounce (using Form 106). A person who has intermeddled in the estate - that is, taken steps to administer it - cannot subsequently renounce: PAA s 40. This principle guards against the selective acceptance of profitable executor duties while abandoning onerous ones.

2.2 The Probate Application Process

Applications for grants of probate in NSW are made to the Probate Registry of the Supreme Court and, for straightforward estates, are processed administratively without a hearing. The application requires:

  • A completed Summons for Probate (Form 1 - Probate), filed through the Online Registry.
  • The original will and any codicils (plus a photocopy for the Registry).
  • An Executor's Affidavit (Form 2), setting out the executor's identity, the deceased's details, the date of death, and confirming the will was not revoked.
  • An Inventory of Property (Schedule B), listing all NSW assets and their estimated values.
  • A filing fee, calculated on a sliding scale based on the gross value of the NSW estate.
  • Publication of a Notice of Intended Application on the Online Registry (at least 14 days before filing, except in urgent circumstances).

If the Registry is satisfied with the application, it will issue the grant of probate - a sealed court document that the executor can use to deal with the estate. Complex applications, such as those involving foreign wills, lost wills, or informal wills, may require a hearing before a Registrar or Judge.

3. Letters of Administration on Intestacy

Where a person dies intestate (without a valid will), no executor is named and the estate must be administered by an administrator appointed by the court under PAA s 61. The court grants letters of administration to persons in the following statutory order of priority:

  • Spouse or de facto partner of the deceased.
  • Children of the deceased (including adult children).
  • Grandchildren of the deceased.
  • Parents of the deceased.
  • Siblings of the deceased.
  • More remote next of kin.
  • The NSW Trustee and Guardian (where no eligible applicant comes forward).

Persons with equal priority may apply jointly. If a higher-priority person does not wish to apply, they may file a Renunciation of Administration to allow a lower-priority applicant to proceed. The NSW Trustee and Guardian acts as administrator of last resort where the estate is otherwise unadministered.

The intestacy distribution rules are separate from the administration rules and are governed by Chapter 4 of the Succession Act 2006 (NSW). Under s 102, where the deceased leaves a spouse and no issue, the spouse takes the whole estate. Where the deceased leaves both a spouse and issue, the spouse takes the "personal effects," a statutory legacy (presently $350,000 indexed), and one-half of the residue; the issue takes the remaining half.

4. The Executor's Duties

Once a grant of probate is obtained, the executor steps into a position of significant legal responsibility. Executor duties are fiduciary in nature: the executor must act in the interests of the beneficiaries and not in their own interests. The principal duties are:

Duty Description Key Risk if Breached
Collect assets Identify and secure all estate assets, recover debts owed to the estate, and register the executor's authority with relevant institutions Personal liability for assets lost through inaction
Pay debts and liabilities Discharge the deceased's debts in the correct priority order before distributing to beneficiaries Personal liability to creditors if assets improperly distributed
Administer in a timely manner Complete administration within the "executor's year" (12 months from grant) wherever possible Beneficiaries may take action after 12 months to compel distribution
Invest estate assets prudently Apply the Trustee Act 1925 (NSW) prudent person investment standard during the administration period Surcharge for investment losses; disallowance of expenses
Keep accounts Maintain proper accounts of all receipts and disbursements Difficulty passing accounts; potential breach of fiduciary duty
Account to beneficiaries Provide estate accounts to residuary beneficiaries on request Application by beneficiaries to pass accounts in court
Distribute the estate After payment of debts, legacies, and expenses, distribute the residue to residuary beneficiaries in accordance with the will Personal liability if assets distributed before debts paid (devastavit)

5. The Administration Process: Step by Step

In practice, the administration of an estate proceeds through the following stages, which a paralegal will assist with at each step:

  • Initial instructions: Obtain the will, certified death certificate, and instructions from the executor. Confirm testamentary capacity and identify all assets and liabilities.
  • Advertising for creditors: Publish a notice to creditors (Trustee Act s 60) to protect the executor against liability for unknown debts. The standard notice period is 30 days.
  • Application for probate or letters of administration: Prepare and file all documents with the Probate Registry. Manage correspondence with the Registry.
  • Collecting assets: Write to all institutions (banks, share registries, Lands Registry, superannuation funds) with a sealed copy of the grant to transfer assets.
  • Calling in and realising assets: Where assets are to be sold (eg real property), instruct agents and solicitors to conduct the sale.
  • Lodging and paying estate taxes: In Australia, there is no inheritance tax, but the executor must lodge tax returns for the period up to the deceased's death (and potentially for the estate itself as a taxable entity during administration).
  • Paying debts and liabilities: Discharge mortgages, credit card debts, funeral expenses, testamentary expenses, and other valid debts.
  • Distributing legacies: Pay specific and pecuniary legacies as directed by the will.
  • Preparing and distributing estate accounts: Finalise accounts and distribute the residue to residuary beneficiaries with a statement of account.
  • Releasing the executor: Obtain a receipt and release from beneficiaries to discharge the executor from further liability.

6. Section 92 PAA: Standing Searches

A practical tool for estates practitioners is the "standing search" - a mechanism under the Supreme Court (Probate) Rules 2015 allowing any interested person to lodge a caveat or an appearance that will notify them if a grant is applied for in the estate of a named deceased. Caveats against a grant are used to protect the interests of persons who dispute the validity of a will and wish to be heard before probate is granted.

A caveat remains in force for six months and may be renewed. On the filing of a caveat, the Registry will not issue a grant without notifying the caveator, who then has the opportunity to commence probate proceedings to contest the will. The grounds for contesting probate include lack of testamentary capacity, want of knowledge and approval, undue influence, and forgery.

7. Executor's Commission

An executor is not automatically entitled to payment for their services. However, under s 86 PAA, the court (or beneficiaries by agreement) may allow the executor a "commission" - a fee for their pains and trouble. Commission is typically calculated as a percentage of the capital value of the estate (commonly around 1-1.5%) plus an annual management fee for income, though the court has full discretion to adjust based on the complexity and skill involved.

Where a solicitor or professional trustee acts as executor, they may charge their ordinary professional fees in addition to commission under a properly drawn "executor charging clause" in the will. Absent such a clause, the court's approval is required: Re Beddoe [1893] 1 Ch 547.

8. Conclusion

Probate and estate administration are the backbone of any wills and estates practice. Every estate, whether simple or complex, requires someone to be authorised to deal with the deceased's property, and the grant of representation is the legal mechanism that confers that authority. For the paralegal, mastery of the procedural requirements, the executor's duties, and the administration timeline is essential. Errors at any stage - missed creditor claims, premature distributions, failure to advertise - can expose the executor to personal liability and the firm to a negligence claim.

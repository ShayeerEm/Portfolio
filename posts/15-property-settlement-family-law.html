Property Settlement under the Family Law Act: The Four-Step Process and Contributions Analysis

SE
Shayeer Emanee
June 2026
5 min read
Family Law Case Analysis Australian Law
🏠

Property settlement in family law proceedings is governed by Part VIII of the Family Law Act 1975 (Cth) (FLA), which applies to married couples, and Part VIIIAB, which applies to de facto couples (in those states and territories that have referred their legislative powers). The determination of a property settlement involves a structured discretionary exercise that has been codified in case law through the "four-step process" articulated by the Full Court of the Family Court in Hickey v Hickey (2003) FLC 93-143. This post examines each step, the principles that govern the assessment of contributions, and the adjustments that may be made for "future needs."

1. Section 79: Just and Equitable Orders

Section 79(1) of the Family Law Act 1975 (Cth) empowers the court to make such order as it considers appropriate to alter the interests of the parties in property. Section 79(2) provides that, before making such an order, the court must be satisfied that it is just and equitable to do so. This threshold requirement - often overlooked in practice - was given new significance by the High Court in Stanford v Stanford (2012) 247 CLR 108, which held that the court must positively identify the reasons why it is just and equitable to interfere with the parties' pre-existing legal interests in property. It is not assumed that a property settlement order is always appropriate merely because a relationship has broken down.

2. The Four-Step Process

The four-step process articulated in Hickey v Hickey (2003) FLC 93-143 provides the accepted analytical framework for property settlement:

  • Identify and value all assets, liabilities, and financial resources of both parties (the "asset pool").
  • Assess the contributions of each party - financial and non-financial - to the acquisition, conservation, and improvement of property.
  • Assess any future needs factors under s 75(2) (for married couples) or s 90SF(3) (for de facto couples) that require an adjustment.
  • Consider whether the proposed order is just and equitable in all the circumstances.

3. Step 1: Identifying and Valuing the Asset Pool

The asset pool includes all assets and liabilities of the parties, regardless of how they are held - jointly, individually, or in trust or corporate structures in which the parties have an effective interest. Assets include real property, superannuation entitlements, business interests, personal property (vehicles, jewellery, artwork), shares and investments, and contingent assets such as pending insurance claims or inheritance expectations. Liabilities include mortgages, personal loans, credit card debts, and tax liabilities.

Superannuation is treated separately from other assets under the superannuation splitting provisions in Part VIIIB of the FLA, but its value is included in the asset pool for the purpose of calculating percentage entitlements. Where parties cannot agree on valuations - particularly for real property and business interests - the court may appoint a single expert to provide an independent valuation.

4. Step 2: Contributions

Contributions under s 79(4) of the FLA are assessed broadly. They include financial contributions (direct contributions to the acquisition of property, such as cash payments) and non-financial contributions (homemaking and parenting). The long-standing principle, affirmed in Mallet v Mallet (1984) 156 CLR 605 and subsequently in numerous Full Court decisions, is that the homemaker's non-financial contributions are to be treated as equal in principle to the financial contributions of the income-earning spouse.

However, "equal contribution" does not automatically translate to a 50:50 split. Where one party made a significantly larger initial capital contribution - particularly an inheritance or a large pre-relationship asset - that contribution is weighted in the assessment. Similarly, where the relationship was short and both parties entered with approximately equal assets, the contributions-based analysis may result in something close to a restoration of the starting position.

Contributions to child welfare Primary carer for children during relationship; schooling management s 79(4)(d)

5. Step 3: Future Needs Adjustments (Section 75(2))

Having assessed contributions, the court may adjust the contributions-based split to account for the future needs of the parties. Section 75(2) of the FLA lists eighteen factors relevant to future needs, the most significant of which are: (a) the age and health of each party; (b) the income, property, and financial resources of each party; (c) the physical and mental capacity of each party to earn income; (d) whether either party has the care and control of children under 18; and (e) any disparity in the earning capacity of the parties arising from any period of care for children.

The future needs adjustment is not available as a matter of course to the lower-earning spouse. It must be established that there is a genuine disparity in future needs or earning capacity, and that the disparity is connected in a material way to the relationship or its breakdown. Where the parties are young, have comparable earning capacities, and there are no children, little or no future needs adjustment may be warranted even where the contributions were unequal.

6. Step 4: Just and Equitable

The final step is a holistic assessment of whether the proposed order - after contributions and future needs - is just and equitable in all the circumstances. This is not a licence to depart from the results of steps 2 and 3 without reason; the just and equitable requirement is a check, not an invitation to impressionism. However, in cases of egregious behaviour - such as wastage of assets, deliberate dissipation during the relationship, or fraudulent non-disclosure - the just and equitable step provides a further basis for adjustment beyond what steps 2 and 3 would dictate.

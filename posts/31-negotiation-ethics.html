Ethics in Negotiation: Disclosure Duties, Half-Truths, and Changed Circumstances

SE
Shayeer Emanee
June 2026
8 min read
Professional Responsibility Case Analysis Australian Law
📋

Negotiation is the dominant mode of dispute resolution in Australian legal practice. More than 90% of civil disputes settle before trial, and the vast majority of criminal matters are resolved through plea negotiations. Yet the ethical framework governing how solicitors conduct negotiations is less well understood than the courtroom duties that attract more academic attention. This post examines the solicitor's ethical obligations in negotiation: what must be disclosed, what may be concealed, and where the line falls between legitimate advocacy and professional misconduct.

1. The Court Duty Extended: "Court" Includes Negotiations

Before examining the specific disclosure rules, one foundational point must be made: the definition of "court" in the Australian Solicitors' Conduct Rules 2015 (NSW) (ASCR) is broad. It includes not only formal court proceedings but also any arbitration, mediation, and other dispute resolution process. This means that all of the duties applicable to conduct in court - including the duty not to make false or misleading statements - apply equally in mediations and negotiations.

A solicitor who would not lie to a judge must not lie to a mediator. A solicitor who must correct a false statement made in pleadings must also correct a false statement made at the mediation table. The venue changes; the ethical standard does not.

2. The Starting Point: No General Duty to Disclose

The starting point for negotiation ethics is that there is no general duty to volunteer information adverse to your client's position. In arm's-length commercial negotiations between parties with competing interests, silence about unfavourable facts is not dishonesty.

This principle was confirmed in Lam v Ausintel Investments Australia Pty Ltd, where the Court held that a solicitor in an adversarial negotiation is not required to disclose information that would harm their client's bargaining position merely because it would be useful to the other side. Commercial negotiations are understood by all participants to be adversarial - each side is advancing its own interests. The obligation of candour that applies between solicitor and court does not apply at the negotiating table to the same degree.

ASCR Rule 22.1, however, establishes a critical floor: a solicitor must not knowingly make a false or misleading statement to an opponent. Silence is one thing; a positive lie is another. The distinction between "not telling the other side something" and "actively misleading the other side" is the central ethical boundary in negotiation practice.

3. Three Exceptions: When You Must Disclose

Despite the starting point, three categories of circumstance impose a positive obligation to disclose. These are not rules that merely permit disclosure - they require it.

3.1 Special Relationships

Where the parties to a negotiation are in a fiduciary or other special relationship - one that generates an expectation of candour rather than adversarialism - a positive duty to disclose adverse information may arise. The clearest example is a trustee negotiating with a beneficiary. Because the trustee owes fiduciary duties to the beneficiary, the adversarial model of negotiation does not apply; the trustee must be frank about the matters the beneficiary is entitled to know.

In practice, the "special relationship" exception is narrower than it might appear. Most commercial negotiations are not fiduciary - they are between parties at arm's length whose interests are opposed. But practitioners acting in contexts involving ongoing trust, joint venture arrangements, or family transactions should be alert to whether a duty of disclosure has arisen from the nature of the relationship rather than the legal rule alone.

3.2 Half-Truths: Legal Practitioners Complaints Committee v Fleming

A statement that is literally true can nonetheless be misleading if, by omitting a material qualification, it creates a false impression. This is the "half-truth" rule, and it is one of the most important and underappreciated ethical constraints in negotiation.

The leading authority is Legal Practitioners Complaints Committee v Fleming [2006]. Fleming was acting for a widow whose recently deceased husband had left an unwitnessed will. Under the Succession Act, an unwitnessed (informal) will is not automatically valid - it requires a court order to be admitted to probate, and it can more readily be challenged. Fleming was negotiating with the deceased's siblings over the estate. Throughout the negotiation, he referred to the document as "the will" without disclosing that it was an informal will that had not been formally proved and whose validity was uncertain.

Fleming was found guilty of unprofessional conduct. He had made a statement - referring to "the will" - that was true in a narrow technical sense (there was a document that purported to be the testator's testamentary intentions) but was misleading because it conveyed to the other side that the estate was governed by a formally valid, undisputable will. The omission of the word "informal" and the absence of any disclosure about validity was a half-truth.

Two points from Fleming are of particular practical significance:

  • The defence that "I was acting on my client's instructions" was rejected. Fleming argued that his client had instructed him not to disclose the informal nature of the will. The tribunal held this is no defence. A solicitor cannot carry out a client's instructions to mislead the opposing party. If the client insists on a course of conduct the solicitor cannot ethically pursue, the solicitor must refuse and, if necessary, withdraw.
  • The conduct need not be deliberate or dishonest in the ordinary sense. The tribunal did not find that Fleming had deliberately set out to deceive. The finding of unprofessional conduct rested on the fact that the half-truth was objectively misleading, regardless of subjective intent.

3.3 Changed Circumstances: Legal Services Commissioner v Mullins

The third and most practically important exception is the changed circumstances rule. Where a solicitor has made a true statement in negotiations, but that statement has subsequently become false due to changed circumstances, the solicitor has a positive obligation under ASCR Rule 22.2 to correct the statement.

The authority is Legal Services Commissioner v Mullins [2006] QLPT 12. Mullins was a barrister representing Mr White in a personal injury claim. Mr White was a quadriplegic. The medical evidence used in the negotiations, including expert reports on Mr White's life expectancy, was based on an assessment of a 20% reduction in life expectancy - a significant figure, because damages for personal injury are calculated partly by reference to how long the plaintiff will need care and support.

Days before the scheduled mediation, Mr White was diagnosed with secondary cancer. The cancer dramatically reduced his life expectancy - potentially to a matter of months. This meant the expert reports being relied upon in the negotiation were now false: they stated a life expectancy that was materially higher than Mr White's actual prognosis.

Mullins did not disclose the diagnosis. The mediation proceeded on the basis of the outdated medical evidence, and a substantial settlement was reached - a settlement that, from the perspective of Mr White's estate, was likely far larger than it would have been had the other side known the true life expectancy.

Outcome: Mullins was found guilty of professional misconduct. He was reprimanded and fined $20,000. The reasoning was straightforward: the life expectancy evidence had been a true statement when made, but it had become false. ASCR Rule 22.2 requires that a solicitor take all necessary steps to correct any false or misleading statement as soon as they become aware of it. Mullins had done the opposite - he continued relying on the now-false evidence and allowed the mediation to proceed without correction.

The changed circumstances rule is sometimes misread as creating a general obligation of disclosure. It does not. It creates an obligation to correct previously made statements when those statements have become false. A solicitor who has said nothing about Mr White's health would have no obligation (under this rule) to volunteer information about the cancer diagnosis. But having placed life expectancy evidence before the other side, Mullins could not continue to rely on that evidence once he knew it was no longer accurate.

4. ASCR Rules 22.1 and 22.2: The Text

Rule Text (summary) Effect
ASCR Rule 22.1 A solicitor must not knowingly make a false or misleading statement to an opponent Prohibits positive deception in negotiations - covers both outright lies and half-truths
ASCR Rule 22.2 A solicitor must take all necessary steps to correct a false or misleading statement made to an opponent as soon as the solicitor becomes aware that it is false or misleading The changed circumstances rule - requires active correction, not merely silence

5. The ACL Overlay: Demagogue v Ramensky

Negotiation conducted in a commercial context is conducted "in trade or commerce" and therefore attracts s 18 of the Australian Consumer Law (ACL), which prohibits conduct that is misleading or deceptive or is likely to mislead or deceive.

The important case of Demagogue Pty Ltd v Ramensky established that silence can be misleading under s 18 ACL where there is a reasonable expectation that a particular fact will be disclosed. Unlike the common law position - where silence is generally not actionable - the ACL imposes liability where withholding information, in context, creates a false impression. A vendor who fails to disclose a known defect in a property when selling it "in trade or commerce" may breach s 18, even if they said nothing false.

This ACL obligation operates alongside, and independently of, the ASCR rules. A solicitor who does not technically breach Rule 22.1 may still be involved in misleading conduct under s 18 ACL - with consequences not only for the client but potentially for the solicitor personally, depending on their degree of involvement.

6. Conclusion

The ethics of negotiation can be summarised simply: you do not have to volunteer information that hurts your client, but you must not create false impressions - whether through positive misstatement, half-truths, or continued reliance on statements you know to have become false. Mullins and Fleming illustrate the two modes of ethical failure most common in negotiation practice: the solicitor who knowingly continues on the basis of information they know has changed (Mullins), and the solicitor who crafts technically true statements designed to mislead (Fleming). In both cases, the professional misconduct finding turned not on the solicitor's subjective bad faith but on the objective quality of the conduct. And in both cases, following client instructions was no defence.
